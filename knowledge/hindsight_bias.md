# Hindsight Bias

## Definition
The tendency to perceive past events as having been predictable and inevitable after they have already occurred — the "I knew it all along" effect. Once an outcome is known, people reconstruct their prior beliefs and expectations to align with that outcome, overestimating how confident they were in the correct prediction beforehand. Hindsight bias impairs learning from experience because it prevents accurate assessment of what was actually knowable at the time of decision. It also generates unfair evaluation of decision-makers who faced genuine uncertainty.

## Examples
In finance, investors who did not foresee a market crash claim in retrospect that "it was obviously a bubble." The same investors who held through the peak and lost money reconstruct their memory to include clear warning signs that they misidentified at the time.

In medicine, when a patient suffers an adverse outcome from a reasonable treatment decision, healthcare providers who review the case in hindsight rate the risk as more foreseeable than providers who assess the same case prospectively, contributing to unfair malpractice judgments.

In politics, historians and commentators frequently describe the fall of governments, the outcome of elections, or the outbreak of conflicts as "inevitable" in retrospect, despite extensive contemporary evidence that outcomes were genuinely uncertain.

In sports, losing coaches are second-guessed for tactical decisions that seemed reasonable at the time, with commentators calling the decisions "obviously wrong" after the outcome is known.

In technology, the failures of projects like Google Glass or early social networks are described as obvious in hindsight, despite receiving strong positive signals from sophisticated investors and users at the time of launch.

## Indicators
- "I knew it all along" statements after surprising outcomes
- Retrospective probability estimates for known outcomes that are far higher than prospective estimates
- Harsh evaluation of past decision-makers for not foreseeing what is now known
- Difficulty remembering one's actual uncertainty before the outcome was known
- Treating realized outcomes as the only plausible outcomes that existed

## False Positives
- Recognizing that warning signs existed before an event, when those signs genuinely were legible at the time, is accurate historical analysis, not hindsight bias
- Learning that a previous decision was suboptimal given information that was available and should have been considered is legitimate accountability, not bias
- Updating beliefs about what constitutes a warning sign based on observed outcomes is appropriate Bayesian learning

# Ambiguity Effect

## Definition
The tendency to avoid options with unknown probabilities in favor of options with known probabilities, even when the unknown probability option may have a higher expected value. Also called ambiguity aversion, it was identified by Daniel Ellsberg and is related to but distinct from risk aversion: risk aversion involves preferences about known probability distributions, while the ambiguity effect involves aversion to unknown probability distributions. People prefer a known risk to an unknown risk, even when the known risk is objectively worse.

## Examples
In finance, investors systematically overweight domestic equities over international equities, because they know more about domestic market dynamics. The ambiguity of foreign markets produces extra aversion beyond what risk calculations justify.

In medicine, physicians and patients prefer established treatments with known side effect profiles over newer treatments with uncertain but potentially better outcomes, even when evidence suggests the newer treatment is more effective.

In hiring, managers prefer candidates from known educational institutions or companies, because the quality distribution from those sources is familiar. Candidates from unfamiliar backgrounds trigger ambiguity aversion even when individual evidence is strong.

In product development, companies invest in extending existing product lines (known customer response distribution) rather than launching genuinely novel products (unknown response distribution), even when market analysis suggests the novel product has higher expected return.

In law, defendants prefer the certainty of a negotiated plea deal over the ambiguity of trial outcomes, even when the expected sentence from a trial is lower — certainty about the outcome is worth more than the favorable expected value.

## Indicators
- Chooses the familiar option over an unfamiliar one even when the unfamiliar option looks objectively better
- Refuses to decide until someone provides a specific probability, even when no probability exists
- Treats "I don't know the odds" as a reason to avoid an option rather than a reason to gather more information
- Relies on historical data in situations where past patterns genuinely don't apply to the current case
- Avoids acting on a new opportunity because there is no track record to consult, regardless of what the available evidence shows

## False Positives
- Requiring evidence before acting in domains where evidence is obtainable is rational, not ambiguity aversion
- Preferring well-understood options when the cost of being wrong is very high and the ambiguous option has real downside risk is appropriate caution
- Expertise-based preference for familiar domains where performance is genuinely better in familiar territory is calibrated judgment, not ambiguity aversion

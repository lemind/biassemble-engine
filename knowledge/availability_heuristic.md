# Availability Heuristic

## Definition
The tendency to estimate the likelihood or frequency of an event based on how easily examples come to mind. Events that are vivid, recent, emotionally charged, or frequently discussed in media feel more probable than statistically rare events that receive less attention. Availability is a cognitive shortcut — if examples are easy to recall, the brain treats this ease as a signal of frequency. This leads to systematic overestimation of dramatic but rare risks and underestimation of common but unglamorous risks.

## Examples
In public health, people overestimate the risk of dying in a plane crash compared to a car crash because plane crashes receive intensive media coverage. Statistically, driving is far more dangerous per mile traveled.

In finance, investors overweight recent market events. After a crash, risk estimates spike. After a long bull market, investors underestimate downside risk. The most recent data point dominates the historical distribution.

In law, jurors who have recently been exposed to news coverage of violent crime rate the likelihood of the defendant's guilt higher, independent of the evidence presented.

In medicine, a physician who recently treated several cases of a rare disease overestimates its prevalence and over-diagnoses it in the following weeks.

In politics, terrorism dominates security spending decisions disproportionate to its statistical contribution to mortality, because individual attacks generate overwhelming media attention compared to car accidents or preventable diseases.

[Political] A parent testifies at a town hall after seeing repeated news coverage of a school shooting. They demand immediate security spending, even though the school has had no incidents in decades and car accidents kill far more children every year. Because the disturbing footage played repeatedly on every channel, the risk feels immediate and certain — the statistics about relative risk are invisible next to the ease of recalling those images. Every politician listening experiences the same thing, and any vote against the security package looks like indifference to dead children.

[Social] After his son died in a car accident, Robert became convinced that road deaths were at crisis levels. He brings the topic into every conversation, organizes campaigns, talks to strangers about it. The event is so vivid and personal that it reshaped his entire sense of how often these deaths occur — he can recall it instantly, in full detail, while statistics about declining accident rates simply don't register. The ease with which he pictures his son's accident makes the danger feel constantly present, far more common than the numbers show.

## Indicators
- Judges something as common or dangerous because a vivid example comes to mind immediately
- Rates a risk as low because it is hard to picture happening, not because the statistics support that estimate
- Estimates how often something occurs based on how recently or personally it has been seen or heard about
- Worries more about dramatic or newsworthy dangers than about common hazards with higher actual rates
- Cannot bring base rates to mind when a memorable story about the event is available instead
- Updates a probability estimate when news coverage increases, even when the underlying risk has not changed

## False Positives
- Updating risk estimates based on genuinely new statistical evidence is rational, not availability bias
- A domain expert who can rapidly recall many examples of a phenomenon because they have genuine expertise is not exhibiting availability bias — their recall reflects real frequency
- Paying attention to vivid recent events when they carry legitimate signal is appropriate learning, not bias

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Recency Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Negativity Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] My cousin lost everything in a cryptocurrency collapse two years ago. I was there when he cried on the phone to his wife. Every time someone mentions crypto now I can see that call in my mind. I won't touch it. I know people are making money in it right now but I can't get past that image. It feels more dangerous than anything else I could do with my money.

[Medical] A woman in my neighborhood was diagnosed with ovarian cancer last year. I've been going to my gynecologist every six months now instead of annually and I've asked to be screened for everything. My doctor says my risk factors don't justify that frequency. I know that logically. But I can't help thinking: she seemed healthy too. It doesn't feel irrational to me — it feels like I finally woke up.

[Management] We had a major data breach three years ago that cost us clients and got us in the press. Ever since, I push for the most aggressive security spending at every budget meeting. My security team sometimes tells me we're over-investing relative to our actual threat profile. I hear them. But I remember exactly what those three months felt like. I'm not going back there.

[Consumer] I watched a documentary on food contamination at a slaughterhouse. It was disturbing and detailed. I stopped eating beef for almost a year. Not because I looked into the statistics on contamination rates — I never did — but because that footage played in my head every time I went to a grocery store. Something that vivid has to mean something.

[Legal] A colleague's son was wrongfully convicted and spent eighteen months in prison before it was overturned. I've been thinking about that case constantly as I advise my client on whether to accept a plea deal. The wrongful conviction rate is probably very low, but I keep telling my client to fight it in court. I'm not sure my advice is being driven by the evidence in this specific case.

[Social] I've been afraid to travel by train since an accident I read about in detail. The photos, the interviews with survivors — I still think about them. I drive everywhere now, even on long trips. My wife points out that she's seen the accident statistics and driving is significantly more dangerous mile for mile. I tell her it doesn't feel more dangerous. Trains feel dangerous. That's my experience of it.

[Family] My sister had a terrible experience with a home birth that ended in an emergency C-section. I was there. She was in danger for several hours. Now my wife is pregnant and she's interested in a birthing center option. I keep steering her toward the hospital with the most comprehensive NICU. My sister's case is so present to me that I can't assess risk the way I usually would.

[Educational] A student I mentored failed a dissertation defense last year — it was unexpected and painful for everyone. Now whenever I work with a new doctoral student I build in far more preparation time than our department's formal guidelines suggest. The guidelines are based on years of data. My recommendations are based on one vivid terrible afternoon.

[Technical] Our system had a major outage caused by a particular vendor's DNS issue. Eight hours of downtime, three engineers up all night, angry customers. Now whenever any vendor with DNS in their stack comes up in an evaluation, I steer us away. My team says our current exposure to that risk is well-managed. But that outage is so clear in my mind that the risk feels alive in a way others don't.

[Political] I grew up during a period of high crime in my city. Even though the statistics show crime has fallen significantly over the past twenty years, I still vote for law enforcement funding increases at every opportunity. My adult children tell me the neighborhood I worry about is now one of the safer ones. I tell them they didn't see what I saw. The vivid memories make the danger feel constant and current.

[Financial] A friend of mine got into penny stocks a few years back and lost almost everything in a few months. He was devastated. Now every time someone brings up small-cap investing, I think of him and wave it off. I know there are legitimate small-cap value strategies. I've read about them. But that story is so available to me that I can't engage with the category rationally.

[Management] We once fired an employee who then filed a discrimination lawsuit. It was expensive, time-consuming, and bruising even though we prevailed. I've been extremely gun-shy about terminations ever since. My HR team tells me I'm keeping people on far too long when performance issues are clear. They might be right. But that lawsuit is so vivid in my memory that every termination feels like the beginning of another one.

[Medical] My father had a stroke at 58. I've been obsessed with cardiovascular risk since I turned 50. I get full panels done every six months. My cardiologist says my risk factors are well-managed and annual checks are sufficient. I can't do annual. My father seemed fine the week before. The memory of that week makes my sense of risk completely different from what the statistics would suggest.

[Consumer] I bought a blender that caught fire. Nothing serious, I unplugged it quickly, but it shook me. Now I only buy kitchen appliances from one brand I trust. I've rejected cheaper alternatives with similar safety ratings. I haven't looked up the actual rate of appliance fires — I just know that fire, in my kitchen, that afternoon, and I'm not going back to that feeling.

[Legal] I worked on a case where a clearly fraudulent contract was enforced because the opposing party met a technicality. The client lost a substantial sum because we missed one clause. I've been obsessive about contract review ever since — I review things that don't need a second pass, ask for more time on every review than the matter requires. My partners sometimes ask me to dial back. I can't. That clause is always there in my head.

[Social] I've been careful about leaving my car unlocked since my neighbor's car was broken into. I know the neighborhood crime statistics and they're fine. But I watched them clean up the broken glass, I saw how violated she felt. Now I check my car locks three times before going to bed. It doesn't feel excessive. It feels like something I know can happen.

[Family] My brother-in-law had a bad reaction to anesthesia during routine surgery. He was hospitalized for a week. Now whenever any of my children need a procedure under general anesthesia, I become very difficult — requesting specific anesthesiologists, asking detailed questions that the surgery team clearly finds excessive, stalling approvals. I can't move past what happened to him.

[Educational] I graded a batch of plagiarism cases my first year as a professor and two of them were brazen and premeditated. I've been suspicious of unusual writing quality ever since — I run plagiarism software on every paper and personally review anything I think sounds too polished. My department head says I'm catching far fewer actual cases than my time investment would suggest. I don't care. Those two cases are so present to me.

[Technical] I watched a YouTube video about a SCAM robocall operation that was frighteningly effective. Now I don't answer numbers I don't recognize at all, won't click links in texts from known contacts, and have started being suspicious of emails from my own bank. I know I'm overcorrecting. But the sophistication in that video was so striking that every unfamiliar call feels like a threat now.

[Political] A close family friend was audited by the IRS, unfairly I believe, and it took years to resolve. It was financially and emotionally devastating to watch. Since then I've been deeply suspicious of any government expansion of financial data collection. Policy discussions about tax compliance reporting requirements that seem innocuous to my colleagues feel alarming to me. My friend's experience is what I see when those proposals come up.

[Financial] I read a detailed account of the 2008 mortgage crisis — not a summary but a long, narrative, deeply reported book. I could picture the families losing their homes. It changed my view of housing as an investment. I've been renting for eight years since finishing that book, even though my financial position would support buying comfortably. My partner calls it irrational. But those stories are so present to me.

[Management] A startup I admired failed catastrophically after expanding too fast. I knew people who worked there. I still see their LinkedIn posts looking for work months after the shutdown. Since then I've blocked every proposal to expand headcount faster than revenue growth. My team has missed some good windows. But that failure is so present to me that "let's hire ahead of demand" sounds like exactly what they said before everything fell apart.

[Medical] My aunt's misdiagnosis led to delayed treatment and significantly worsened her prognosis. I was involved in navigating that with my family. Now whenever I don't feel right I push hard for second opinions and additional tests even when my own doctor thinks the first assessment is clear. She sometimes seems frustrated. I don't apologize for it. What happened to my aunt is too present to do otherwise.

[Consumer] I once got food poisoning from a restaurant that I had eaten at a dozen times before. I was sick for three days. I haven't been back to that restaurant or any restaurant with similar cuisine. I know food poisoning is statistically rare and that one bad batch of food doesn't change the restaurant's overall safety. I know. But I still feel slightly nauseated driving past it.

[Legal] A news story ran for weeks about a contractor who defrauded homeowners in our state. Detailed interviews, photos of unfinished work, weeping families. I need some renovation work done now and I've been paralyzed by the decision. I've demanded five references from every contractor I consider. I've been quoted as being "unusually difficult" by two contractors who declined the job. That news coverage is so vivid that every contractor feels like a risk.

[Social] Someone at my gym was mugged walking to their car late at night in a parking garage. I heard about it from a mutual friend and the details stuck with me. Now I always park in lit areas, never in garages, and ask someone to walk with me if I have to leave events after 9 pm. My partner has pointed out that our neighborhood's violent crime rate is very low. I know. But that story follows me around.

[Family] My nephew had a serious allergic reaction to tree nuts when he was three. I was there. I watched my sister administer the EpiPen and it was terrifying. Now every time I cook for family events I am completely rigid about allergen-free preparation even for extended family members who have no known allergies. My wife thinks I'm overdoing it. That afternoon is not something I can argue away.

[Educational] I had a student fail their comprehensive exams because of test anxiety — they knew the material but froze. It was hard to watch. Since then I do extensive test preparation sessions before every high-stakes exam in my class, far beyond what the department requires. Some of my colleagues think it's excessive. That one student's face when the results came in is what drives that decision.

[Technical] A colleague walked me through a real incident where a healthcare system's server was ransomwared and patient data was compromised. It was detailed — she described the panic, the week of scrambling, the regulatory consequences. Now in every project review I push for security measures that my team sometimes says are disproportionate to our threat model. Maybe they're right. But her description of that week made it feel like something that could happen to us tomorrow.

[Political] I lived in a city during a significant labor strike that shut down public transit for three weeks. I remember the chaos, the exhaustion, the human cost. Now whenever there are labor negotiations in public service, I reflexively support management positions even when the union's case has merit. I know my position isn't purely based on the policy. That strike is what I think of first.

[Financial] I nearly lost my apartment in 2009 when I was unemployed for seven months. I had to ask my parents for money. That was the worst period of my adult life. Now I keep six months of expenses in cash, earning almost nothing, even though my job is stable and the sensible move would be to invest more of that. I've read the arguments. I understand them. I can't move that money. Those seven months are too present.

[Management] A product we launched failed publicly and messily — reviews, press coverage, customer refunds. It was humiliating and expensive. Since then I've been extremely conservative about product launches, requiring much longer testing cycles than the market probably demands. We've missed some windows as a result. But the failure is so vivid in my memory that every launch plan looks like a version of that situation.

[Medical] I saw an acquaintance collapse from a cardiac event at a community event and had to help administer CPR until the ambulance arrived. It was frightening and close. Since then I've been pushing for my entire extended family to get cardiac workups even when their doctors don't think it's indicated. That afternoon is always there when I think about heart health.

[Consumer] I was stuck in an elevator for forty minutes once — nothing serious, but I was alone and it was frightening. Now I take stairs up to six floors in any building even in business attire. I know elevator failures are extraordinarily rare. I know those forty minutes don't tell me anything meaningful about the risk. My body doesn't believe me when I try to reason about it.

[Legal] A news story about a wills dispute that tore a family apart ran in my local paper. I read the whole thing. It was detailed and sad and I knew vaguely of one of the families. That week I drafted and executed a comprehensive estate plan even though I'm thirty-four. My lawyer said most people my age don't need the level of documentation I put in place. I needed to feel like that family's story couldn't happen to mine.

[Social] A classmate of mine got a DUI five years ago and lost his license for a year. It upended his life — job, relationship, everything unraveled quickly. Since that happened I've been completely rigid about not driving after even one drink. My friends say I'm overcautious. I've called cabs from dinner parties when my blood alcohol was clearly negligible. His situation comes to mind every time I have a drink near a car key.

# Availability Heuristic

## Definition
The tendency to estimate the likelihood or frequency of an event based on how easily examples come to mind. Events that are vivid, recent, emotionally charged, or frequently discussed in media feel more probable than statistically rare events that receive less attention. Availability is a cognitive shortcut — if examples are easy to recall, the brain treats this ease as a signal of frequency. This leads to systematic overestimation of dramatic but rare risks and underestimation of common but unglamorous risks.

## Examples
In public health, people overestimate the risk of dying in a plane crash compared to a car crash because plane crashes receive intensive media coverage. Statistically, driving is far more dangerous per mile traveled.

In finance, investors overweight recent market events. After a crash, risk estimates spike. After a long bull market, investors underestimate downside risk. The most recent data point dominates the historical distribution.

In law, jurors who have recently been exposed to news coverage of violent crime rate the likelihood of the defendant's guilt higher, independent of the evidence presented.

In medicine, a physician who recently treated several cases of a rare disease overestimates its prevalence and over-diagnoses it in the following weeks.

In politics, terrorism dominates security spending decisions disproportionate to its statistical contribution to mortality, because individual attacks generate overwhelming media attention compared to car accidents or preventable diseases.

## Indicators
- Overestimating frequency of vivid or dramatic events
- Underestimating risk of common, unglamorous outcomes
- Strong influence of recent or personally experienced events on probability estimates
- Risk perception that tracks news cycles rather than statistics
- Difficulty recalling base rates when dramatic examples are salient

## False Positives
- Updating risk estimates based on genuinely new statistical evidence is rational, not availability bias
- A domain expert who can rapidly recall many examples of a phenomenon because they have genuine expertise is not exhibiting availability bias — their recall reflects real frequency
- Paying attention to vivid recent events when they carry legitimate signal is appropriate learning, not bias

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Recency Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Negativity Bias

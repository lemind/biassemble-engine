# Authority Bias

## Definition
The tendency to attribute greater accuracy and credibility to the opinion of an authority figure and to be more influenced by their statements than by their actual evidence or reasoning. Authority bias leads people to accept claims from recognized authorities without critical evaluation, to defer to positional or credentialed leaders even outside their domain of expertise, and to discount equivalent information from non-authorities. The Milgram experiments demonstrated the most extreme form: people will follow authority instructions against their own ethical judgment under sufficient social pressure.

## Examples
In medicine, patients are significantly more likely to comply with a treatment regimen when the recommendation is delivered by a physician in a white coat than by a nurse practitioner, even when the clinical content is identical.

In finance, investor behavior disproportionately follows well-known fund managers and celebrity investors — even when their returns do not outperform the market. Warren Buffett's stock purchases are imitated within hours regardless of independent analysis.

In law, expert witness testimony from prestigious institutions is weighted more heavily by juries than equivalent testimony from less prestigious institutions, independent of the underlying evidence quality.

In politics, voters update their positions more after a statement from a leader they recognize as authoritative, even when the leader is speaking outside their area of policy expertise.

In technology, architectural decisions in organizations are disproportionately influenced by the seniority of the person advocating them rather than by the strength of the technical argument, leading junior engineers' valid concerns to be dismissed.

## Indicators
- Accepts a claim as true because of who said it rather than evaluating the reasoning or evidence
- Defers to a credential or title instead of assessing whether the argument actually holds up
- Assumes expertise in one field carries over to an unrelated field where the authority has no track record
- Holds back a disagreement because the person on the other side holds a more senior or prestigious position
- Changes a view when a recognized authority disagrees, without requiring any new reasoning or evidence

## False Positives
- Deferring to genuine domain expertise when you lack the knowledge to independently evaluate a claim is rational, not authority bias — the bias involves over-deference, particularly when evidence is available and you are capable of evaluating it
- Institutional reputation often genuinely correlates with rigor — taking it into account as one factor is not a bias
- Respecting established consensus in mature scientific fields is appropriate epistemic humility, not authority deference

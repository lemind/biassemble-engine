# Authority Bias

## Definition
The tendency to attribute greater accuracy and credibility to the opinion of an authority figure and to be more influenced by their statements than by their actual evidence or reasoning. Authority bias leads people to accept claims from recognized authorities without critical evaluation, to defer to positional or credentialed leaders even outside their domain of expertise, and to discount equivalent information from non-authorities. The Milgram experiments demonstrated the most extreme form: people will follow authority instructions against their own ethical judgment under sufficient social pressure.

## Examples
In medicine, patients are significantly more likely to comply with a treatment regimen when the recommendation is delivered by a physician in a white coat than by a nurse practitioner, even when the clinical content is identical.

In finance, investor behavior disproportionately follows well-known fund managers and celebrity investors — even when their returns do not outperform the market. Warren Buffett's stock purchases are imitated within hours regardless of independent analysis.

In law, expert witness testimony from prestigious institutions is weighted more heavily by juries than equivalent testimony from less prestigious institutions, independent of the underlying evidence quality.

In politics, voters update their positions more after a statement from a leader they recognize as authoritative, even when the leader is speaking outside their area of policy expertise.

In technology, architectural decisions in organizations are disproportionately influenced by the seniority of the person advocating them rather than by the strength of the technical argument, leading junior engineers' valid concerns to be dismissed.

## Indicators
- Accepts a claim as true because of who said it rather than evaluating the reasoning or evidence
- Defers to a credential or title instead of assessing whether the argument actually holds up
- Assumes expertise in one field carries over to an unrelated field where the authority has no track record
- Holds back a disagreement because the person on the other side holds a more senior or prestigious position
- Changes a view when a recognized authority disagrees, without requiring any new reasoning or evidence

## False Positives
- Deferring to genuine domain expertise when you lack the knowledge to independently evaluate a claim is rational, not authority bias — the bias involves over-deference, particularly when evidence is available and you are capable of evaluating it
- Institutional reputation often genuinely correlates with rigor — taking it into account as one factor is not a bias
- Respecting established consensus in mature scientific fields is appropriate epistemic humility, not authority deference

## Related Biases
Halo Effect, Confirmation Bias, Bandwagon Effect, In-group Bias

## Story Patterns

[Medical] My cardiologist told me to cut out salt entirely. I've been on an extremely restrictive diet for eight months. Last week I saw a different doctor for something unrelated and she mentioned that the current evidence on dietary sodium is more nuanced than the old guidelines suggested. I didn't ask questions. My cardiologist is a specialist. He said cut it out; I cut it out.

[Management] Our CEO mentioned in a town hall that he thought blockchain had transformational potential for our supply chain. Nobody challenged him. Within a week three different teams were exploring blockchain initiatives. I was in those meetings. The CEO has no background in supply chain or distributed systems. But the room just moved in the direction he pointed.

[Financial] Warren Buffett mentioned in his annual letter that he liked a particular sector. I bought into an ETF in that sector within the week. I didn't read the full letter carefully or check how that sector fit my own portfolio goals. He's made more money than anyone I'll ever know. If he's pointing somewhere, that's where I want to be.

[Legal] The senior partner told us this contract clause has always been interpreted a certain way. I researched it myself and found a circuit court ruling that suggested a different reading. I brought it up once and she said she'd been practicing for twenty-eight years. I dropped it. She's been wrong before about other things but I'm a third-year associate. I'm not going to push.

[Educational] My thesis advisor told me my research question was too broad. I spent three months narrowing it in the direction she suggested. A colleague in another department read my revised proposal and said the original framing might actually be publishable in top journals. I don't know. My advisor is well-published. I haven't seriously questioned her direction.

[Technical] The CTO announced at a technical review that he wanted our architecture to be microservices-based. Several engineers including myself had concerns about operational complexity for a team our size. We raised them briefly. He said microservices is the right approach. The architecture is now microservices. I'm not sure we ever established that it was actually right for our scale.

[Social] My doctor, who I've seen for years, told me casually that he thought a particular diet was the healthiest approach. He's a physician. I went home and restructured how I eat. I didn't ask him what evidence that was based on or whether it was relevant to my health situation specifically. He's a doctor. That seemed like enough.

[Political] A well-known economist wrote an op-ed saying that a particular trade policy would harm workers. He's at a prestigious university, often cited, appeared on television. The op-ed didn't include much data. I changed my view on the policy. A few months later I read a detailed analysis that challenged his framing substantially. I'm not sure why I updated so quickly on the op-ed.

[Family] My father-in-law ran a successful business for thirty years and he told us not to buy a house right now — the market is too unstable. He's a smart, experienced man. We've held off for a year. I've shown him data suggesting prices in our target neighborhood have actually been steady. He shrugs and says he's seen cycles. We're still waiting. I find it hard to argue against decades of business experience.

[Consumer] The salesperson at the electronics store had a uniform, a badge, and spoke confidently about every product. He steered me toward a television I wasn't originally considering. He said it was the best mid-range option. I bought it. Later I looked at the review sites I usually check — it wasn't highly rated. I just assumed that someone standing in that store in that uniform knew more than I did.

[Medical] I asked my nutritionist about a supplement I'd been reading about. She said she hadn't seen convincing evidence for it. I stopped taking it immediately, even though I'd felt like it was helping. She's the expert. I didn't ask which studies she was referring to or whether my situation might be different. She spoke with authority and I believed her.

[Management] The external consultant we hired has an impressive resume and presented our strategic options confidently. He recommended Option B. Our internal data actually suggested Option A was stronger in our specific market. I presented his recommendation to the board as the preferred path. He has a PhD and has worked with Fortune 500 companies. I second-guessed our own analysis.

[Financial] My accountant told me to structure my business this way for tax purposes. I don't understand the details but she has a CPA and has done this for fifteen years. I signed what she put in front of me. A second accountant I consulted later said there was a more advantageous structure. I hadn't thought to get a second opinion. She's the expert.

[Legal] A senior government official testified that the regulation had always been interpreted to exclude cases like mine. The judge nodded. The regulation's text doesn't clearly say that — there's genuine ambiguity. But nobody pushed back against the official. The position and the tone of authority in the room seemed to settle it.

[Educational] My professor gave back my essay with a mark of 72. She said the argument was underdeveloped. I accepted this and spent weeks revising. Two years later, reading the essay again, I think the original argument was actually quite good. But in that room, with that red pen, from that professor, 72 felt like the truth about my work, not one person's reading of it.

[Technical] Our lead architect has been with the company for twelve years. He said the proposed caching strategy wouldn't scale. I'd done the analysis and thought it would. I backed down. He's been right about many things. I folded my slides and moved on. Six months later, working on a different team, I saw them successfully deploy nearly identical logic. I never followed up on whether the original concern was warranted.

[Social] I was at a dinner party and a retired general made a confident claim about foreign policy. I had read something that contradicted it and I started to say so. I didn't finish my sentence. He looked at me and said he'd served forty years in the military. I said "that makes sense" and moved to a different subject. I'm still not sure he was right.

[Political] The party's leading economist endorsed the new fiscal plan. I support the party and he's been right about forecasts before. I've been defending the plan in conversations with friends even though I haven't read it carefully. His name attached to it was enough. I assume if he thinks it's sound, it probably is.

[Family] My mother-in-law told us our baby should start solid foods at four months. She raised five children. Our pediatrician has said six months is the current recommendation. I find myself pulling toward four months because she has five kids and our pediatrician has never raised children. I know that's not a logical comparison but the weight of her experience is hard to argue with.

[Consumer] The wine sommelier at the restaurant recommended a bottle at $95. He described it in such knowledgeable terms — talking about the terroir and the vintage — that I agreed without looking at the wine list myself. I wouldn't have known how to choose anyway. He knows wine. That felt like the right person to follow.

[Management] The board brought in a renowned strategy expert who said our company should exit our legacy product line. He gave a compelling presentation. Our own product team's data suggested the legacy line was still contributing meaningfully to cash flow. The board voted to wind it down. I sat in that room. His reputation made the local data feel less reliable than it actually was.

[Medical] The hospital's chief of surgery said the standard of care for this type of injury was a specific surgical approach. I'm a patient, not a doctor. I didn't ask whether there were recent studies suggesting alternatives. He's the chief of surgery. That's his domain. I consented to the procedure he recommended.

[Financial] An analyst from a major investment bank put out a buy rating on this company. The report was full of jargon I didn't fully understand. But the bank is reputable, the analyst's name is well-known in this sector. I added to my position. My own reading of the company's debt ratios made me a bit nervous before I saw the report. Somehow the analyst's confidence resolved my doubt.

[Legal] Our immigration attorney told us the application process would take twelve to fourteen months. I've told my whole family this. A colleague who recently went through the same process said it took six months. I don't know who's right but I haven't questioned my attorney. She's been practicing immigration law for twenty years. I assume she knows.

[Educational] The department head told our cohort that certain journals were not considered credible in our field. I've been avoiding submitting to them. A researcher visiting from another institution mentioned that one of those journals is actually well-regarded in a cognate field and publishes strong work. I felt suddenly uncertain. I'd just taken the department head's ranking as accurate without checking.

[Technical] A well-known blogger in the engineering community wrote that our current database choice was unsuitable for high-concurrency workloads. He's widely followed. Our team spent two months evaluating alternatives. Our actual load never comes near the thresholds he described. I asked someone on the team how we'd come to question the database choice — he said he read that post. We'd reorganized work around one person's blog.

[Political] A professor from a prestigious university testified before a committee that a particular policing strategy was proven effective. She said it calmly and with a lot of data. The committee voted to fund it. I was in the public gallery. I didn't know enough about criminology research to evaluate her methodology. She spoke with authority. The committee treated her recommendation as settled.

[Family] My sister has worked in finance for fifteen years and told me confidently that I should pay off all my debt before investing. I restructured my financial plan entirely around this. Later I spoke with a fee-only financial planner who explained that low-interest debt should typically not be prioritized over tax-advantaged investing. My sister isn't a financial planner. I'm not sure why I didn't question her advice more.

[Consumer] The personal trainer at my gym has a physique I admire and he speaks about nutrition with complete certainty. I've been following his diet advice for months. I mentioned one of his recommendations to my doctor who seemed skeptical and said it wasn't based on current evidence. I'm not sure who to believe. But in the gym, with his build and his confidence, his advice felt like science.

[Management] The management consultant our firm hired has worked with three of our competitors. He said he knew exactly what our problem was within an hour of walking in. I found myself nodding. He framed everything with certainty. Later, one of my directors pointed out that two of his recommendations contradicted each other. By that point I'd already communicated his framework to the team as our new direction.

[Social] My neighbor told me confidently that the new housing development nearby would lower our property values. He said he'd read about it happening elsewhere. I started worrying about our investment. I brought it up with a realtor friend who said mixed-use development near single-family homes typically has a neutral or positive effect on values. My neighbor spoke with such certainty I hadn't thought to verify it.

[Financial] The portfolio manager at the bank had credentials listed on his door, an office with a view, and handled himself like someone who had been doing this for a long time. I moved a substantial sum into a product he recommended. I didn't ask about fees or historical performance in writing. Something about the environment — the office, the suit, the framed degrees — made me feel like critical questions were unnecessary.

[Legal] The other side's expert witness testified for hours. He had published extensively, taught at a known law school, and spoke in complete sentences that sounded like certainties. I was on the jury. His credentials were so heavy that the weaknesses in his methodology — which were apparently significant, our side's expert tried to explain them — barely registered. His authority made the technical critique feel like splitting hairs.

[Medical] I asked my GP about a newer blood pressure medication that I'd read had fewer side effects. She said she didn't typically prescribe it and preferred the medication she'd been using for years. I didn't push further. She's been my doctor for a decade. She probably has good reasons. I didn't ask what they were.

[Technical] Our lead data scientist, who has a PhD from a top program, told the team that our current feature selection approach was naive. He proposed something more complex. Several team members thought the simpler approach was performing well, but nobody pushed back directly. He made it sound like the simple approach was embarrassing. We adopted the complex one and spent three months rebuilding our pipeline. Our metrics didn't meaningfully improve.

[Political] The president of our professional association told a conference that remote work permanently reduces team cohesion and should be curtailed. Several member organizations changed their policies afterward. I've heard researchers in organizational behavior say the evidence on this is actually mixed and depends heavily on context. But the president of our association speaking at a large conference felt like it settled the question.

[Family] My uncle, who owned a restaurant for thirty years, told me restaurants are the worst investment you can make. I've been telling my business partner the same thing and keeping us away from the food service angle of our business plan. I've since read that the failure rate varies enormously by format and location, and some restaurant models are quite defensible. I haven't revisited my uncle's advice. It came from thirty years and it felt like wisdom.

# Stereotyping Bias

## Definition
The tendency to apply generalized beliefs about a group to individual members of that group, expecting them to conform to the group stereotype without regard to individual-level evidence. Stereotyping substitutes group-level statistics or cultural assumptions for individual assessment. It is efficient as a mental shortcut in low-stakes situations but produces systematic errors and unfair treatment in high-stakes contexts like hiring, lending, diagnosis, and law enforcement. Stereotyping is distinct from statistical discrimination in that stereotypes often rely on inaccurate or outdated group characterizations rather than true group statistics.

## Examples
In hiring, a manager who believes women are less committed to their careers than men underrates female candidates who have identical professional records, attributing gaps in their resumé to family obligations without inquiry.

In medicine, historical stereotypes about pain tolerance across racial groups have led clinicians to undertreat pain in Black patients, who report being prescribed lower doses of pain medication than white patients with equivalent diagnoses.

In finance, loan officers who hold stereotypes about particular neighborhoods or demographic groups deny credit to qualified borrowers, contributing to documented patterns of discriminatory lending even when income and creditworthiness are controlled.

In law enforcement, stereotype-based suspicion leads to differential enforcement of laws against groups who are perceived as more likely to commit offenses, independent of base rates and individual behavior.

In technology, products designed with a default user in mind that matches a narrow demographic fail to accommodate the needs of users who do not fit that prototype — stereotyping users before even meeting them.

## Indicators
- Applies group-level traits to an individual without examining that person's actual behavior or situation
- Uses a demographic characteristic — age, gender, nationality, profession — as a proxy for how someone will think or act
- Sticks with an initial assessment of an individual even when their actual behavior clearly shows it is wrong
- Treats a counterexample as "an exception that proves the rule" rather than as evidence against the stereotype
- Explains what an individual did by referring to their group membership rather than to their individual choices or circumstances

## False Positives
- Using statistically validated base rates about a group in probabilistic reasoning — while remaining open to updating on individual evidence — is statistical inference, not stereotyping bias
- Pattern recognition based on genuine behavioral signals rather than group membership is legitimate assessment
- Cultural competence — recognizing that norms differ across groups and communicating accordingly — is awareness, not stereotyping when it remains sensitive to individual variation

## Related Biases
In-group Bias, Halo Effect, Representativeness Heuristic, Fundamental Attribution Error

## Story Patterns

[Management] A candidate for the senior engineering role is 58 years old. Her technical portfolio is strong. But I keep thinking about whether she'll adapt to our fast-moving environment and whether she's up to date on current tools. I haven't asked her about either of those things directly. I've been forming assumptions based on her age rather than her specific experience and capabilities.

[Legal] A young defendant came to court in a suit that fit him poorly and he was visibly nervous throughout the proceedings. I kept catching myself reading his nervousness as evidence of something rather than as the expected state of a young person in a formal legal proceeding for the first time. I've been more skeptical of his account than the evidence independently warrants. I know this is happening and I'm trying to correct for it.

[Medical] A patient came in who was significantly overweight and reporting chronic knee pain. My initial mental path went straight to weight as the cause. I asked a few questions about weight before I'd asked anything about his physical activity level, his history of the pain, or possible structural causes. A colleague later asked if I'd considered a structural issue. I hadn't fully. My first assumption had organized my diagnostic approach.

[Consumer] I walked into a store looking for advice on a high-end audio setup. The two staff members visible were a young man in his twenties and a middle-aged woman. I walked toward the young man because I assumed the audio expertise would sit there. The middle-aged woman came over and she knew far more than he did. I had made an assumption about who had the technical knowledge based on demographics, not on any evidence.

[Educational] A student in my class speaks with a strong accent from a region I associate with lower English-language education. I've been slightly more patient with her written submissions, giving her benefit of the doubt on expression I found awkward. When her mid-semester essay came back, it was one of the sharpest analytical pieces I'd read all term. I'd been applying a different standard based on my assumption about her background, not her actual work.

[Financial] A small business owner came in for a loan consultation. Her business was in the beauty industry. Before I read the financials I had already built a mental picture of the kind of business this would be — small, informal, probably thin margins. The actual financial statements showed a well-managed, growing operation with solid margins and good cash flow. My initial assessment had been organized around the industry category, not the business.

[Social] I was at a party and started talking to a man who was wearing very casual clothes and had a simple manner. I assumed we probably didn't have much in common professionally. About twenty minutes in I found out he was a structural engineer who had worked on infrastructure projects I admired. I'd been half-engaged in the conversation because of an assumption I'd formed from the way he dressed.

[Technical] We were reviewing resumes for a DevOps position. One candidate had worked at several small companies I'd never heard of. Another had a familiar set of large-company logos on their resume. I skimmed the first one and read the second one carefully. My director asked me to go back to the first. She was right — the first candidate had done work that was actually more relevant to what we needed.

[Family] My son is bringing home a girlfriend for the first time. I looked up her social media before they arrived. She goes out a lot, her photos are social and casual. I formed an impression before I met her. At dinner she was thoughtful, asked good questions, and showed real consideration for my son. The person I met was different from the person I'd assembled from photos.

[Legal] A defendant's tattoos were visible above his collar during the hearing. I noticed several jurors looking at them. The prosecution's opening statements referenced lifestyle. I watched the jury's faces during the opening. The tattoos had done something to the room before any evidence was presented. The visible markings were being read as character evidence by people who hadn't examined the evidence.

[Management] I've been thinking about whether to recommend a colleague for a leadership role. She's young — twenty-eight — and I keep coming back to whether she's ready. When I try to identify specific things that make me uncertain, most of them are vague. The cases I can point to don't clearly support the hesitation. I'm not sure how much of the hesitation is about her demonstrated capabilities versus her age.

[Financial] A client asked about investment options for a small amount — about $5,000. I gave a shorter consultation than I'd give to a client with $100,000. The information is mostly the same. Small accounts can grow. People starting with small amounts can become significant clients. I gave a shorter consultation because of the amount on the table, not because of the person's potential.

[Medical] A middle-aged woman came in reporting fatigue and low mood. My early diagnostic thinking went to depression and stress before she'd finished describing her symptoms. I ordered the depression screener before the metabolic panel. My colleague reviewed the case and suggested the symptom profile warranted a broader workup. The thyroid test came back abnormal. I'd routed to the psychological pathway quickly based on patient demographics.

[Educational] A parent who works as a laborer asked very basic questions at the parent-teacher conference. I spent more time explaining foundational concepts than I would have with a parent who presented as a professional. By the end of the conference I realized he'd been asking basic questions to build a complete picture, not because he lacked the ability to understand more. My explanation calibration had been based on his occupation, not his actual understanding.

[Technical] I got a pull request from an intern. Before I read it I had already decided it would need significant revision and I went in with light engagement, planning to leave detailed educational feedback. The PR was cleaner and better-considered than several I'd seen from more senior engineers that week. I had to reset my engagement mode. My initial low-engagement reading had missed some of the details I later needed to compliment.

[Social] My neighborhood has been changing demographically and some longtime residents have been saying things feel less safe. When I've pushed them to be specific about what incidents they've experienced, it usually comes down to unfamiliar faces and different cultural expressions in public space. The police data doesn't show a change in incidents. The feeling of safety has changed without the data following it.

[Management] A job applicant had a gap in her resume — two years with no listed employment. I asked about it in the interview. She said she'd been caring for a sick parent. I noted it as a flag. Looking back, I don't know what the flag was for. I wouldn't have flagged two years of employment at a company I'd never heard of. I'd applied a different standard to a caregiving gap than I would to a vague employment history.

[Financial] A potential client spoke through a translator because her English was limited. I was slightly less attentive in the consultation — less thorough in my explanations, quicker to move through the options. My colleague who reviewed the notes said I'd given a consultation that was less complete than my usual standard. I'd unconsciously adjusted the quality of my work based on a communication barrier rather than the client's needs.

[Medical] An elderly man brought his wife to his appointment and she answered questions on his behalf frequently. I increasingly directed questions to her rather than to him. He noticed and redirected me several times. Afterward I reviewed the visit notes and my assumptions about his cognitive capacity had affected who I was communicating with. The assessment I'd made from initial observation had changed my behavior in ways I hadn't consciously decided on.

[Consumer] I was looking for a financial advisor and had consultations with three candidates. Two wore business formal. One wore business casual. On paper their qualifications were similar. I scheduled a second meeting with the two in business formal and didn't call back the third. I later realized I didn't have any specific concern about the third candidate. The clothing had been doing work I hadn't recognized at the time.

[Legal] Our firm was deciding whether to represent a client accused of a serious crime. One partner kept using language like "the type of person who" to describe the alleged behavior. She hadn't reviewed the evidence file. She'd reviewed the charges and formed a character picture. We're defense attorneys. That picture-before-evidence is the thing we're supposed to interrupt in other people, and we were doing it in our own case acceptance discussion.

[Technical] A user filed a bug report using casual, grammatically inconsistent language. I put it in the lower priority queue. The same bug, reported six months earlier by a corporate client in professional language, had been in the high priority queue. The underlying issue was the same. How the report was written had affected how seriously I'd taken the underlying problem.

[Family] My daughter is studying arts and humanities. My son is studying engineering. When my daughter describes a professional challenge, I listen with gentle sympathy. When my son describes the same type of challenge, I'm quicker to offer frameworks and problem-solving approaches. I've been applying different conversational modes based on their fields rather than on what each of them is actually asking for.

[Management] At a company meeting I presented data and two questions came from different employees. One was a confident, senior-sounding voice. One was quieter and from someone I didn't recognize. I engaged the first question at length. I gave the second a shorter, more procedural answer. My manager pulled me aside afterward and said the second question had actually been more substantive. My response to each had been shaped by presentation style, not question quality.

[Social] A friend introduced me to a woman who was very quiet in group settings. I assumed she wasn't particularly interested in the conversations we were having. Over dinner one night when it was just three of us, she was one of the most thoughtful and engaged people I'd had a conversation with in months. I'd been misreading quiet as disengagement when it had been something else entirely.

[Educational] I teach a course in a predominantly technical department. When liberal arts students enroll, I've occasionally assumed they might need additional support with quantitative methods. In three of those cases the liberal arts students outperformed the majority of the technical students on the quantitative components. My advance assumption had given me nothing predictive and had required me to consciously revise my assessment each time.

[Financial] A customer called in with a complaint about a transaction fee. Her accent and manner suggested to me she hadn't read the terms carefully and I prepared a patient explanation. She stopped me partway through and cited the specific clause in the agreement that she believed was being misapplied and was correct. I'd been preparing an explanation for someone who already understood the issue better than I'd assumed she would.

[Legal] I was advising on a workplace accommodation case. The employee had a mental health condition. Before I'd heard the specifics, I'd started mentally categorizing this as a high-complexity case with emotional variables. My colleague asked me afterward whether I'd have categorized it as high-complexity if the accommodation had been for a physical condition. I had to think about that honestly. I don't think I would have.

[Technical] A user interface review was submitted by a designer who works remotely in a location that I associate with a different design aesthetic than what we use. I went into the review expecting to suggest Western market adjustments. The submission was already well-calibrated for the market. My pre-review assumption had organized my attention in a direction that wasn't warranted.

[Medical] A patient told me she used herbal supplements regularly. I noted it with mild skepticism and moved on. A colleague later pointed out that I'd asked fewer follow-up questions about this patient's health practices than I typically do with patients who report more conventional health behaviors. I'd treated the supplement use as evidence of a certain category of health beliefs and adjusted my clinical engagement accordingly without examining whether that adjustment was warranted.

[Family] I was helping my teenage niece think through college options. She mentioned an interest in vocational training rather than a four-year university. I spent most of the conversation gently steering the discussion toward university options. She later told her mother that she felt like I hadn't heard what she said. I hadn't. I'd heard vocational training as something that needed reexamining rather than as a considered choice from someone who knew what she wanted.

[Social] I joined a community board that has members from a range of professional and educational backgrounds. When residents from the wealthier end of the community speak at meetings, I've noticed the board takes more time with their comments and asks more follow-up questions. When residents from other parts of the community speak, the responses are often more procedural. I've been part of this pattern even when I've been conscious of trying to interrupt it.

[Consumer] I was shopping for a plumber. A woman called me back from the service I'd contacted. I asked slightly more questions about her credentials and experience than I would have if a man had called back first. I noticed it while I was doing it and couldn't fully stop. She gave more detailed answers than most plumbers I speak to, answered every question without impatience, and did excellent work. The credential questions came from an assumption she had to demonstrate something the call-back from a male plumber wouldn't have required.

[Management] A new team member came from a company in an industry I don't associate with technical rigor. I assigned him to a simpler project to start rather than a complex one. His work on the simple project was excellent and showed both technical depth and quick learning. My director suggested giving him a more complex project earlier than I'd been planning. The industry origin had organized my assumptions about his capabilities before I had any actual evidence.

[Legal] My client was presented in court wearing clothing that the public defender had suggested — formal, neutral. The judge's demeanor changed when she spoke from what it had been during the opening statements about her. I later asked my client whether she'd chosen the outfit herself. She said the attorney had advised it specifically to manage impression. The courtroom was designed to evaluate evidence. It was also responding to appearance in ways that both sides were actively managing.

[Educational] I'm teaching a seminar on conflict resolution. Two students are in a persistent tension with each other. One is from a culture I associate with direct communication. The other is from a culture I associate with indirect communication. I've been attributing the tension to cultural communication differences. The actual content of their disagreement is substantive and has nothing to do with communication style. I'd applied a cultural framework before examining the actual situation.

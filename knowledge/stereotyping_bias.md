# Stereotyping Bias

## Definition
The tendency to apply generalized beliefs about a group to individual members of that group, expecting them to conform to the group stereotype without regard to individual-level evidence. Stereotyping substitutes group-level statistics or cultural assumptions for individual assessment. It is efficient as a mental shortcut in low-stakes situations but produces systematic errors and unfair treatment in high-stakes contexts like hiring, lending, diagnosis, and law enforcement. Stereotyping is distinct from statistical discrimination in that stereotypes often rely on inaccurate or outdated group characterizations rather than true group statistics.

## Examples
In hiring, a manager who believes women are less committed to their careers than men underrates female candidates who have identical professional records, attributing gaps in their resumé to family obligations without inquiry.

In medicine, historical stereotypes about pain tolerance across racial groups have led clinicians to undertreat pain in Black patients, who report being prescribed lower doses of pain medication than white patients with equivalent diagnoses.

In finance, loan officers who hold stereotypes about particular neighborhoods or demographic groups deny credit to qualified borrowers, contributing to documented patterns of discriminatory lending even when income and creditworthiness are controlled.

In law enforcement, stereotype-based suspicion leads to differential enforcement of laws against groups who are perceived as more likely to commit offenses, independent of base rates and individual behavior.

In technology, products designed with a default user in mind that matches a narrow demographic fail to accommodate the needs of users who do not fit that prototype — stereotyping users before even meeting them.

## Indicators
- Applies group-level traits to an individual without examining that person's actual behavior or situation
- Uses a demographic characteristic — age, gender, nationality, profession — as a proxy for how someone will think or act
- Fails to revise an assessment of an individual even when their behavior clearly contradicts the group expectation
- Treats a counterexample as "an exception that proves the rule" rather than as evidence against the stereotype
- Explains what an individual did by referring to their group membership rather than to their individual choices or circumstances

## False Positives
- Using statistically validated base rates about a group in probabilistic reasoning — while remaining open to updating on individual evidence — is statistical inference, not stereotyping bias
- Pattern recognition based on genuine behavioral signals rather than group membership is legitimate assessment
- Cultural competence — recognizing that norms differ across groups and communicating accordingly — is awareness, not stereotyping when it remains sensitive to individual variation

## Related Biases
In-group Bias, Halo Effect, Representativeness Heuristic, Fundamental Attribution Error

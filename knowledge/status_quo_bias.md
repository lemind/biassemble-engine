# Status Quo Bias

## Definition
The preference for the current state of affairs, such that any change from the baseline is perceived as a loss rather than an opportunity. People tend to prefer existing options over new ones, existing policies over alternatives, and existing arrangements over changes — even when the expected value of the alternative is higher. Status quo bias is closely related to loss aversion: the potential downsides of change are weighted more heavily than equivalent upsides, and inertia is the path of least psychological resistance. Default options exploit status quo bias — people disproportionately stick with whatever is presented as the default.

## Examples
In finance, investors hold their existing portfolio allocation year after year without rebalancing, even as market movements push their allocation away from their stated risk preferences. Inertia costs these investors real returns.

In technology, enterprise organizations continue using legacy software long after better alternatives exist and the switching costs have declined, because "if it ain't broke, don't fix it" becomes organizational policy.

In organ donation, countries with opt-out systems (where donation is the default) achieve dramatically higher donation rates than opt-in countries, despite identical legal outcomes from active choice — the default is the status quo.

In policy, voters resist healthcare, pension, or tax reforms even when presented with evidence that the current system is suboptimal, because proposed changes are framed as losses relative to the current baseline.

In career decisions, professionals stay in unsatisfying roles because the familiarity of the current position feels safer than the uncertainty of a job search, even when the expected value of switching is clearly positive.

## Indicators
- Prefers the current arrangement without actively evaluating whether it is the best option
- Treats any change as risky by default, without assessing the actual risks and benefits of the proposed change
- Accepts a default option without deliberating on whether it is right for the situation
- Justifies a current practice primarily because it is how things have always been done
- Avoids experimenting with a change even when the cost of trying it is low and the outcome is reversible

## False Positives
- Genuine preference for stability after careful evaluation of alternatives is a legitimate value, not status quo bias
- Conservatism in high-stakes irreversible decisions is rational caution — not all preference for the status quo is bias
- Sticking with a current approach when it is genuinely performing well and alternatives have meaningful switching costs is efficient, not biased

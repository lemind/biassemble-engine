# Status Quo Bias

## Definition
The preference for the current state of affairs, such that any change from the baseline is perceived as a loss rather than an opportunity. People tend to prefer existing options over new ones, existing policies over alternatives, and existing arrangements over changes — even when the expected value of the alternative is higher. Status quo bias is closely related to loss aversion: the potential downsides of change are weighted more heavily than equivalent upsides, and inertia is the path of least psychological resistance. Default options exploit status quo bias — people disproportionately stick with whatever is presented as the default.

## Examples
In finance, investors hold their existing portfolio allocation year after year without rebalancing, even as market movements push their allocation away from their stated risk preferences. Inertia costs these investors real returns.

In technology, enterprise organizations continue using legacy software long after better alternatives exist and the switching costs have declined, because "if it ain't broke, don't fix it" becomes organizational policy.

In organ donation, countries with opt-out systems (where donation is the default) achieve dramatically higher donation rates than opt-in countries, despite identical legal outcomes from active choice — the default is the status quo.

In policy, voters resist healthcare, pension, or tax reforms even when presented with evidence that the current system is suboptimal, because proposed changes are framed as losses relative to the current baseline.

In career decisions, professionals stay in unsatisfying roles because the familiarity of the current position feels safer than the uncertainty of a job search, even when the expected value of switching is clearly positive.

[Consumer] Sofia defaults to government bonds and keeps defaulting there year after year, even as her financial situation stabilizes and her advisor recommends gradual diversification. Changing the allocation feels like choosing to take on risk, even though staying put is already costing her through suboptimal returns. Keeping everything as it is does not feel like an active decision — it feels like the absence of a decision, which makes it the natural path. The question she never asks is: if she were starting fresh today, would she choose this allocation?

## Indicators
- Prefers the current arrangement without actively evaluating whether it is the best option
- Treats any change as risky by default, without assessing the actual risks and benefits of the proposed change
- Accepts a default option without deliberating on whether it is right for the situation
- Justifies a current practice primarily because it is how things have always been done
- Avoids experimenting with a change even when the cost of trying it is low and the outcome is reversible

## False Positives
- Genuine preference for stability after careful evaluation of alternatives is a legitimate value, not status quo bias
- Conservatism in high-stakes irreversible decisions is rational caution — not all preference for the status quo is bias
- Sticking with a current approach when it is genuinely performing well and alternatives have meaningful switching costs is efficient, not biased

## Related Biases
Sunk Cost Fallacy, Loss Aversion, Anchoring Bias, Framing Effect

## Story Patterns

[Financial] My retirement account has been in the same balanced fund since I opened it twelve years ago. My financial advisor has recommended rebalancing twice. I always say I'll look into it. It's been fine the way it is. I'm not sure it's optimal anymore but I haven't wanted to actively make a change that might turn out to be wrong. Keeping it the same doesn't feel like a decision. It feels like nothing.

[Management] We've been running our weekly team meetings on Monday mornings for eight years. A few team members have mentioned that Monday morning meetings are hard after the weekend and suggested moving to Tuesday. I've been saying we'll revisit it. Monday morning is how we do it. Changing the day would require everyone to update their calendars and adjust their planning habits. I keep tabling the conversation.

[Technical] We've been using the same project management software for five years. A tool comparison I ran last year showed a clear better option at similar cost. I haven't migrated. The new tool would require two weeks of setup and migration time. The current tool is fine. Familiar problems feel more manageable than unknown problems.

[Legal] The partnership agreement we use was drafted in 2014. Several things have changed since then — our business model, applicable regulations, our composition as a firm. Every year I say I'll have it updated. Every year I don't. The agreement has been fine. Starting a revision process will surface things we'll need to decide. I'm not ready to open that process.

[Consumer] My internet provider raised their price last year. I've seen advertisements for a better plan at a lower price from a competitor. Switching requires calling the provider, returning equipment, and being without internet for a day. The current provider is slow but reliable. The potential downside of switching feels more concrete than the monthly savings feel. I've been meaning to call for seven months.

[Medical] I've been with the same GP for fifteen years. She's fine — not exceptional, sometimes hard to reach, not always up to date on the latest guidelines. My insurance changed and there's a different practice in my network that multiple people have recommended highly. I haven't switched. Finding a new doctor means transferring records, explaining my whole history again, and building a new relationship. Staying is easy. Moving requires something.

[Educational] Our department's curriculum hasn't been substantially revised in nine years. Every year a faculty member proposes updates and we discuss them. Every year we end up making minor adjustments and leaving the core structure intact. Revising the curriculum would require reaching consensus across faculty with competing views. The current structure is known and functional. Each year the default wins.

[Political] I've been registered with the same political party since I turned eighteen. Over the years my views have evolved and I find myself in genuine agreement with some positions in a different party and in genuine disagreement with some positions in my current one. I haven't re-registered. Changing registration feels like a statement, like a loss of something. Staying registered feels like not making a decision.

[Social] Our friend group has been meeting at the same bar on the second Thursday of every month for six years. The bar has gotten worse — service is slow, it's louder, the prices went up. We've mentioned it to each other. Nobody has proposed a different venue in a way that resulted in actually going somewhere different. The second Thursday at the same place is what we do. Changing it would require someone to actually find an alternative and organize the change.

[Financial] My mortgage is at a rate that made sense when I took it out. Rates changed significantly and I could probably refinance at a better rate. I've thought about it four times. Every time I look at the paperwork involved, the closing costs, and the time required, I decide to keep looking into it. At some point the staying is costing more than the switching. I know that. I haven't done the calculation honestly.

[Technical] Our team still uses a documentation tool that is slower and less capable than several alternatives we've evaluated. We've been saying for two years that we'll migrate when we have a slow sprint. We never have a slow sprint. The current tool works. Moving would require a migration effort that the team is reluctant to commit to during busy cycles. The current tool is worse. It's also the current tool.

[Management] Our hiring process includes four rounds of interviews. That structure made sense when we were hiring for a certain type of role in a certain competitive environment. Our needs and the market have changed. I've been meaning to redesign the process. Every time I need to hire quickly, I use the existing process because it's defined and understood. Designing a new process would take time I don't have when I'm actively hiring.

[Consumer] My gym is far from my current home. I moved two years ago and now there's a better gym twenty minutes closer. I keep saying I'll cancel my current membership and join the new one. Canceling requires calling, serving a notice period, and submitting a form. Starting new requires different paperwork. Every month I pay for the gym that's far away. Every month the gym that's closer stays an intention.

[Legal] Our standard service agreement hasn't been updated since the company was much smaller and operating in one jurisdiction. We now operate in three jurisdictions. My general counsel has mentioned this. I've asked for a revision to be scoped. The revision has been scoped but hasn't been prioritized. The current agreement gets used in new contexts it wasn't designed for. Starting from scratch is the right move and I keep not starting.

[Family] My parents have their house set up the way they set it up thirty years ago. The layout made sense when they had young children and different physical needs. Now they're in their seventies and the layout creates real challenges — the main bathroom is on the second floor, the kitchen is organized for large-scale cooking. We've talked about modifications several times. Each time we decide to wait and see. The house as it is has been fine for thirty years. Changing it feels larger than it probably is.

[Technical] Our deployment pipeline has a manual approval step that made sense when we first set it up and were deploying tentatively. Three years later our testing coverage is much better and the manual approval mostly adds friction without adding safety. I've been saying we'll remove it. Removing it means revisiting the risk assessment that justified it in the first place. That's a conversation I haven't started.

[Financial] I've been with the same bank for twenty years. A few years ago I opened a high-yield savings account at a different institution for better rates. Most of my assets are still at my original bank out of inertia. Moving them would require paperwork, transfer coordination, and probably a few hours of administration. The interest rate difference is real money over time. I've been meaning to consolidate to the better account for three years.

[Educational] Our school's assessment calendar was designed before we adopted our current curriculum. The calendar no longer aligns well with where students are in their learning when assessments occur. We've acknowledged this as a faculty. Every year when the calendar is set, we use last year's calendar with minor adjustments. Redesigning the calendar requires buy-in from multiple departments and would surface disagreements about scheduling priorities. We keep the current calendar.

[Social] I've been using the same email program since 2009. It's outdated, slower than alternatives, and requires workarounds for some tasks my job now requires regularly. My IT team has recommended migrating twice. I've asked them to wait until I have time to learn the new system. I've been asking them to wait for four years. The old program works for most of what I do. The transition cost feels real and immediate.

[Political] Our city uses a voting system that was adopted in 1998. Better systems have been adopted elsewhere and evidence suggests they'd improve participation and representation. A proposal to modernize the system comes up periodically. Each time, the objection is implementation cost and transition risk. The current system has been used for twenty-five years without major visible problems. The absence of obvious disaster is the argument for keeping it.

[Management] The reporting structure in my department made sense before we reorganized around products rather than functions. The old structure creates coordination friction now. I've sketched a better one. Changing the structure means uncomfortable conversations with several senior people about their scope. As long as the current structure is in place I don't have to have those conversations. Every month I mean to. The month ends with the same structure.

[Financial] My car insurance has been with the same company for nine years. I've been quoted better rates from two competitors. Switching requires paperwork, calling my current company to cancel, and a brief overlap period to ensure coverage. My current rate isn't terrible. Every year at renewal I think about switching and then renew because renewal is a single click. The alternative requires a series of steps I never seem to complete.

[Technical] Our team has been using a homegrown internal tool for task management that one engineer built three years ago. There are better tools available and the homegrown one has pain points everyone complains about. Nobody wants to be the person who retires a tool that a colleague built with care. And migration would take time. The tool is functional. Its problems are familiar problems.

[Consumer] The phone plan I'm on made sense when I signed up. My usage patterns have changed significantly. A different plan structure would cost less and serve my actual usage better. Every month I think about calling to change it. Calling customer service for phone companies is never pleasant and always takes more time than expected. I've been on the same plan for four years and it costs more than it should.

[Legal] Our company's privacy policy was written when we had far fewer user types and far less data. The current policy doesn't fully reflect how we actually process data. Our general counsel has said it needs updating. The update would require a cross-functional process, external counsel, and a coordinated rollout. The current policy technically covers our operations under reasonable interpretation. Starting the revision process is something we keep planning for next quarter.

[Family] My home office setup was configured when I started working from home three years ago. The chair is uncomfortable, the monitor positioning isn't right, and the lighting gives me eye strain. Several small changes would significantly improve my workday. Each change requires purchasing something and spending a Saturday setting it up. The current setup works. I've adapted. Every few months I mean to address it and don't.

[Educational] I teach this course from notes I developed six years ago. The field has moved. New research should update several sections. Revising the course thoroughly would take a summer. Every summer I update small sections and leave the core intact. My students are getting a course that reflects where the field was six years ago in several important areas. Revising it would require me to acknowledge that some things I've been saying are outdated. The notes I have are the notes I use.

[Technical] Our company's email has been hosted on-premises since 2008. Cloud-hosted email would be cheaper, more reliable, and require less internal maintenance. The migration would be disruptive and complex. Every year we budget for IT infrastructure and every year the migration gets pushed back. The on-premises system is old but stable. Stability and optimal aren't the same thing. We've been choosing stability.

[Social] I've been going to the same barbershop since I was in my twenties. The barber I liked left two years ago. The current barber is fine but I've noticed the work is less careful. Several people have recommended a different shop near my current neighborhood. I keep going to the old place. I know the layout, I've been there hundreds of times, and switching would require a few awkward firsts at a new place. The current shop isn't great. It's familiar.

[Political] Our department has been using the same performance evaluation framework for twelve years. Research on what predicts employee effectiveness has advanced significantly. The current framework measures things that correlate only loosely with actual performance and misses things that matter. Implementing a new framework would require retraining managers, recalibrating processes, and managing transition anxiety. The current system is imperfect but understood. We use it.

[Financial] My brokerage account charges fees that have become less competitive over the past decade. Lower-cost alternatives are widely available. Moving my account would require paperwork, a transfer period, and potentially tax implications for timing. I've been with this brokerage for eleven years. Every year the fee difference represents real money. Every year I don't move the account. The inertia has a cost I've been implicitly choosing to pay.

[Management] We run our annual planning cycle in October even though our fiscal year starts in January. The timing means we're doing planning during our busiest quarter, the results are often rushed, and the final plan doesn't incorporate Q4 results that would inform it. Changing the planning cycle would require resetting everyone's calendars and habits. October is when we've always done planning. The problems are familiar enough that they don't feel urgent to fix.

[Consumer] My prescription has changed twice in the past four years but I've been filling it at the same pharmacy out of habit. A pharmacy near my current home has shorter wait times and a delivery option that would be genuinely useful to me. Switching requires transferring the prescription and enrolling in a new system. The current pharmacy is twelve minutes away. The new one is three minutes away. I've been choosing twelve minutes over the effort of a one-time transfer.

# Halo Effect

## Definition
The tendency for a positive impression of a person, company, brand, or product in one area to positively influence opinions in unrelated areas. A single salient positive trait creates a "halo" that colors evaluation of all other traits. The reverse — the horn effect — occurs when a negative trait colors all other evaluations negatively. The halo effect causes evaluators to assume coherence and consistency across unrelated qualities, leading to incomplete assessment and overlooked weaknesses.

## Examples
In business, a CEO who is tall, well-spoken, and physically attractive receives higher performance ratings from boards and investors independent of financial results. Studies show attractiveness predicts CEO compensation even when controlling for company performance.

In technology, products from companies with strong brand reputations — Apple, Google — receive more favorable initial reviews and higher initial user ratings than technically equivalent products from unknown brands.

In law, physically attractive defendants receive lighter sentences than less attractive defendants when controlling for the severity and type of crime. Jurors attribute more positive character traits to attractive individuals.

In academia, students who write a well-formatted, clearly presented essay receive higher grades for the quality of argumentation, even when the actual argument is identical to a poorly formatted submission.

In hiring, candidates who perform well in the first interview question are rated higher on all subsequent questions, while candidates who stumble early are rated lower on otherwise identical responses.

## Indicators
- Assuming competence in one domain implies competence in unrelated domains
- Attributing positive character traits to physically attractive or prestigious individuals without evidence
- Applying brand reputation to product quality assessments regardless of specific product merits
- Reluctance to criticize a person with strong positive traits in one visible area
- Generalizing from a single positive or negative interaction to a holistic judgment

## False Positives
- Some traits genuinely correlate across domains — a person who is highly organized may genuinely be reliable in both personal and professional contexts
- A company with a strong track record in one product category may have real skills transferable to adjacent categories
- Positive first impressions that are updated and refined through subsequent information are appropriate, not halo effect-driven

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Authority Bias, Stereotyping Bias, Framing Effect

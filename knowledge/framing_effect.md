# Framing Effect

## Definition
The tendency to draw different conclusions from the same information depending on how it is presented. Logically equivalent statements produce different responses when framed differently — as gains versus losses, percentages versus absolute numbers, or through different emphasis. The framing effect demonstrates that people do not evaluate options in absolute terms but relative to the way they are described. Framing interacts with loss aversion: people are more motivated to avoid losses than to acquire equivalent gains, so loss-framed messages typically produce stronger responses than gain-framed ones.

## Examples
In medicine, patients are more likely to consent to surgery when told it has a "90% survival rate" than when told it has a "10% mortality rate," despite the statements being mathematically identical.

In finance, a fund advertised as having "outperformed 70% of its competitors" receives more investment than one advertised as "underperforming 30% of competitors," even though both statements describe the same performance rank.

In politics, describing an immigration policy as "protecting jobs for citizens" versus "excluding foreign workers" activates different evaluative frameworks and produces different support levels for the identical policy.

In retail, ground beef labeled "75% lean" is perceived as higher quality than ground beef labeled "25% fat" in consumer taste tests, despite the products being physically identical.

In public health, a disease prevention program described as "saves 200 lives" out of 600 at risk is preferred over a statistically equivalent program described as "400 people will die," because the first is gain-framed and the second loss-framed.

## Indicators
- Reactions that change based on presentation rather than substance
- Strong preference for gain-framed versus loss-framed descriptions of identical options
- Sensitivity to absolute versus relative risk presentations
- Varying risk tolerance depending on whether a choice is framed as a potential gain or a potential loss
- Different evaluations of logically equivalent information when numerical format changes

## False Positives
- Responding differently to genuinely different presentations that carry different information is rational — not all framing differences are misleading
- Preferring clear, simple communication over technically equivalent but confusing communication is not a bias
- Expert reframing that highlights genuinely relevant context is informative communication, not manipulative framing

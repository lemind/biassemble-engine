# Framing Effect

## Definition
The tendency to draw different conclusions from the same information depending on how it is presented. Logically equivalent statements produce different responses when framed differently — as gains versus losses, percentages versus absolute numbers, or through different emphasis. The framing effect demonstrates that people do not evaluate options in absolute terms but relative to the way they are described. Framing interacts with loss aversion: people are more motivated to avoid losses than to acquire equivalent gains, so loss-framed messages typically produce stronger responses than gain-framed ones.

## Examples
In medicine, patients are more likely to consent to surgery when told it has a "90% survival rate" than when told it has a "10% mortality rate," despite the statements being mathematically identical.

In finance, a fund advertised as having "outperformed 70% of its competitors" receives more investment than one advertised as "underperforming 30% of competitors," even though both statements describe the same performance rank.

In politics, describing an immigration policy as "protecting jobs for citizens" versus "excluding foreign workers" activates different evaluative frameworks and produces different support levels for the identical policy.

In retail, ground beef labeled "75% lean" is perceived as higher quality than ground beef labeled "25% fat" in consumer taste tests, despite the products being physically identical.

In public health, a disease prevention program described as "saves 200 lives" out of 600 at risk is preferred over a statistically equivalent program described as "400 people will die," because the first is gain-framed and the second loss-framed.

[Political] Describing the same economic data as "proof our policies are working" versus "a modest recovery still lagging projections" produces opposite assessments of the same administration, even among voters shown the identical underlying statistics. A speech that opens with job-creation numbers frames the economy as a success; one that opens with wage-stagnation figures frames it as a failure. Framing controls the verdict before the numbers are even examined.

[Social] A school safety campaign presents the same statistics two ways. One version says "3 children were involved in school safety incidents last year in this county." The other says "Every single parent knows the gut-wrenching fear — your child could be next, at any moment." The first produces measured discussion about resources. The second produces fear-driven demand for immediate action, even though both describe the same data. The emotional language adds no information; it adds arousal that replaces deliberate evaluation.

## Indicators
- Responds differently to the same information depending on whether it is presented as a gain or a loss
- Prefers a treatment described as "90% survival rate" over one described as "10% mortality rate," even though they are identical
- Feels more willing to take a risk when options are framed as potential gains, and more cautious when the same options are framed as potential losses
- Evaluates the same statistic differently when expressed as a percentage versus a frequency
- Chooses differently between two logically identical options based on which words the presenter used to describe them

## False Positives
- Responding differently to genuinely different presentations that carry different information is rational — not all framing differences are misleading
- Preferring clear, simple communication over technically equivalent but confusing communication is not a bias
- Expert reframing that highlights genuinely relevant context is informative communication, not manipulative framing

## Related Biases
Anchoring Bias, Negativity Bias, Loss Aversion, Halo Effect

## Story Patterns

[Medical] My surgeon said there's a 91% chance this procedure goes well. I agreed to it that afternoon. My wife looked up the same procedure later and mentioned that one in eleven people have a serious complication. She said those are the exact same odds. I was surprised — it hadn't hit me the same way when she described it that way. I'm still going to have the procedure but I'm asking for a longer consultation now.

[Financial] The fund brochure said it had beaten 80% of actively managed peers over five years. I was impressed and allocated money to it. My advisor later showed me the same fund described in a regulatory filing as underperforming 20% of its category. She said that was identical information. I knew she was right. It still felt different. I was more cautious about the fund when I thought of it as part of the underperforming 20%.

[Management] I told my team that if we don't hit our Q3 targets, we'll lose our expanded headcount in Q4. Urgency and focus immediately increased. My COO told me later that an equivalent message — "hitting Q3 targets earns us expanded headcount in Q4" — describes the same situation. I believed her. But the version I'd used felt more motivating. I keep using the first version.

[Consumer] The ground beef at the store said 85% lean. I put it in my cart thinking it was a healthy choice. There was another package next to it from a different brand that said 15% fat. I put the second one back. My husband pointed out they were the same. I knew that. The first one still felt healthier. I bought the 85% lean one.

[Political] The policy was described to me as "protecting jobs in manufacturing communities." I supported it. Two months later a journalist described it as "restricting access to cheaper imported goods." I pushed back and said those were completely different policies. She showed me the text. They weren't. My whole reaction had been built on how the policy was described, not on what it actually did.

[Legal] The settlement offer was described to me as "you'll get $80,000 guaranteed." I was inclined to accept. Then the other side described it as "you'll give up the chance to win $200,000." Both my attorney and I agreed that was technically the same offer. We both found ourselves leaning slightly different ways after each version. I made us write out the actual numbers without the framing before we decided.

[Technical] My team was told: "Three out of ten deployments in the last quarter had incidents." Engineering morale dropped. The following month the same data was presented as "seven out of ten deployments in the last quarter had no incidents." The team felt markedly better about the same performance. I noticed the difference in the room but I couldn't exactly argue with either number. They were both true. They did not feel equal.

[Educational] I wrote on a student's essay: "This argument doesn't fully develop the implications." A different student got: "You stopped just before the most interesting part of the argument — push further." Both comments pointed to the same gap. The second student came to office hours excited to revise. The first seemed discouraged. Same observation. Very different landing.

[Social] When I heard that 95% of skydiving jumps go without incident, I felt reassured and almost considered trying it. My friend said that means roughly 1 in 20 jumps has some kind of incident. Same data. Suddenly it felt much riskier. My brain treated these as different facts even though I knew intellectually that they weren't. The second version is what came to mind when I thought about signing up.

[Medical] The clinical trial described the drug as having "a 30% response rate." I thought: only three out of ten people benefit. I was skeptical. Then the coordinator told me that "70% of patients experience no meaningful response." I said that was worse news. She told me it was identical information. I've been sitting with that for a week. I don't think I fully believe it even now.

[Financial] My advisor told me we could "lock in a guaranteed 4% annual return" on a particular product. Felt safe and attractive. Then she reframed it as "you'll accept a maximum of 4% in exchange for giving up upside above that." Same product. The second description made me feel like I was giving something away. We ended up having a different conversation than I expected.

[Management] Our monthly report said we were 15% below our revenue plan. I was frustrated. The same report could have said we captured 85% of our ambitious target. I know both are true. The presentation the CFO chose set the tone for the entire leadership conversation that followed. A different starting number — same reality — would have produced a different meeting.

[Consumer] I was choosing a car with a fuel efficiency rating. One version said 40 miles per gallon. The other model's brochure said it used 2.5 gallons per 100 miles. My engineer husband said they were equivalent for my driving patterns. I preferred the one described in miles per gallon. The miles-per-gallon framing felt meaningful in a way that gallons-per-100-miles didn't connect with. I knew they were the same. I still chose by feel.

[Political] Our neighborhood association voted on a plan described as "redirecting 10% of the public safety budget toward mental health services." It passed narrowly. The same proposal was described at a different meeting in an adjacent district as "reducing the police budget by 10%." It failed there. Both descriptions were accurate. Both communities knew what they were voting on. They voted differently.

[Legal] The arbitration clause was described to me as "a streamlined alternative dispute resolution process." I signed. Two years later I needed to make a claim and my attorney told me I'd waived jury trial rights. She said the same clause is often described as "a process that removes your right to a court hearing." I asked if those were the same clause. They were. I had never encountered the second description.

[Technical] We tested two versions of a product alert. Version A: "Your storage is 90% full." Click-through rate to upgrade: 12%. Version B: "You have 10% of your storage remaining." Click-through: 31%. Same information. I wasn't surprised by the result once it came in — I'd expected them to differ. I was surprised by how large the gap was. People respond so differently to what's left versus what's used.

[Social] I told someone I was "sober-curious and cutting back" versus telling another person I "don't really drink." Same behavior. The first person thought I was making a lifestyle experiment. The second person assumed something had happened that required me to stop. Both reactions shaped how they talked to me afterward. The label I used changed the entire dynamic before I'd said anything about my actual life.

[Family] My father's doctor said he had a 20% chance of a serious complication in surgery. I became very reluctant. My mother pointed out that the same statistic could be stated as an 80% chance of no serious complications. She was right. I spent a week responding to 20% as if it were large and not quite engaging with the 80% as if it were also real. The framing I first encountered was the one that drove my reaction.

[Educational] I presented our school's reading improvement results two ways in different meetings. In one: "35% of students are still below grade level." That meeting was grim. In another: "65% of students are now at or above grade level, up from 52% last year." That meeting was energized. Both descriptions were true and both contained the same student performance data. The room I was in shaped everything based on which number I led with.

[Consumer] I was shown two insurance plans. Plan A: covers 80% of eligible procedures. Plan B: you pay the remaining 20% out of pocket. My husband and I both knew these described the same coverage. We still felt differently about them. Plan A felt generous. Plan B felt like exposure. I had to deliberately remind myself they were identical before I could evaluate them on actual cost.

[Technical] The performance report said our app loaded in 2 seconds. The other team's report said their equivalent page took under 3 seconds. We all agreed ours was faster. But the framing of "under X" made theirs sound like it cleared a threshold, while ours just stated a fact. In the stakeholder meeting, the framing had done something to the perceived comparison before anyone did the math.

[Management] I told a new hire during feedback that he'd failed to meet three expectations. He looked defeated. A colleague suggested I reframe it: he'd fully met seven expectations and needed to develop three more. She pointed out I was describing the same performance. I had been unconsciously leading with what was missing. The same evaluation, started differently, would have produced a completely different posture in the person sitting across from me.

[Political] Two candidates described the same national unemployment figure. Candidate A said, "Employment is at a 20-year high." Candidate B said, "Nearly 4% of workers are unable to find jobs." Both were talking about the same numbers for the same period. The first sounded like success. The second sounded like failure. Most voters hearing either version didn't calculate that they were equivalent. I had to think about it carefully before I was sure they were.

[Financial] My credit card statement shows my balance and a "minimum payment due." For three years I paid the minimum. I thought I was making responsible choices. When I finally looked at the "payoff if you pay minimum" projection table buried at the bottom, I calculated that I had paid thousands in interest thinking I was staying current. The framing of "minimum payment" suggested I was doing something adequate. It was designed to make a harmful behavior feel responsible.

[Legal] An environmental enforcement case framed the company's discharge level as "within 8% of the regulatory limit." My reaction: they were basically compliant. The opposing counsel described it as "discharging at 8% above the safe threshold." I stopped and asked them to clarify whether they were describing the same number. They were. Both were correct. I had to consciously reset my reaction after both framings because I couldn't trust that my gut responses were independent of the language.

[Social] I described my neighborhood's new restaurant to a friend as "a casual spot with inexpensive food." She seemed interested. Later my partner described the same place as "a cheap diner." Same restaurant. My friend's face changed. The place hadn't changed. The words had shifted what she was picturing before she'd even visited. She came anyway. She liked it. But the framing had almost kept her away.

[Medical] The therapist offered me two different descriptions of the same treatment commitment. Version one: "We'd meet weekly for about a year." I felt the weight of a year. Version two: "We'd meet for about 50 sessions." Somehow 50 sessions sounded like a program with a clear end. My partner said that's the same thing. I know. I signed up for the 50-session program. I probably wouldn't have committed to "a year of therapy" as readily.

[Consumer] A restaurant menu had two dishes: one described as "grilled salmon with braised fennel and citrus reduction" and one described as "salmon, fennel, and orange sauce." Same dish in the kitchen — my server confirmed it when I asked. Different prices. The first was $4 more. Most tables ordered the first. Including me. The description had added value that didn't correspond to any difference in what arrived on the plate.

[Technical] We ran a pilot program and described it in two different internal documents. One: "We saw a 15% reduction in error rates during the pilot." Another document sent to a different team: "Error rates remained at 8.5% after the pilot, down from 10%." Same pilot, same result. The first document generated enthusiasm for a full rollout. The second generated a question about whether 8.5% was acceptable. The baseline for evaluation had been set by which number was listed first.

[Educational] I described our graduation rate to two different audiences. To the alumni board: "Our four-year graduation rate has improved by 12 percentage points over five years." To the state accreditation committee: "Our four-year graduation rate is 71%." Both were true. The alumni board celebrated. The accreditation committee noted that 29% of students weren't completing on time. I was describing the same program to both groups and getting completely different reactions because I'd selected the frame that would land best in each room.

[Political] A ballot measure described as "allocating $2 million annually to improve school infrastructure" passed with 68% support. An identical measure in an adjacent district was described as "spending $2 million of taxpayer funds annually on school renovations." It passed with 44% support. I was a researcher observing both campaigns. Same dollars. Same purpose. The word "spending" associated with "taxpayer funds" changed the vote by nearly 25 points. We wrote about it. Nobody wanted to believe us.

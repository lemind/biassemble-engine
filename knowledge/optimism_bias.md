# Optimism Bias

## Definition
The tendency to overestimate the likelihood of positive events and underestimate the likelihood of negative events affecting oneself, relative to others or to base rates. People consistently believe they are less likely than average to experience illness, divorce, accidents, or financial failure, and more likely than average to live long, have successful careers, and achieve their goals. Optimism bias is the most prevalent cognitive bias — studies show more than 80% of people believe they are above-average drivers. It underlies planning fallacy, overconfidence, and excessive risk-taking in both individuals and organizations.

## Examples
In finance, startup founders systematically underestimate time to profitability, capital requirements, and competitive threats. Studies show that most entrepreneurs estimate their own venture's survival probability at 70–80% despite knowing that base rates for new business survival are under 50%.

In construction and infrastructure, projects routinely overrun cost estimates by 20–50% or more. Analysis of major infrastructure projects shows systematic optimism in initial cost and timeline projections across countries and project types.

In medicine, patients consistently underestimate their personal risk of chronic disease even when told they share risk factors with the broader population. Smokers believe their own cancer risk is lower than that of "the average smoker."

In law, litigants overestimate their probability of winning, leading to fewer settlements and longer, more expensive trials. Both sides in a dispute simultaneously believe they will prevail.

In technology product development, feature completion estimates are consistently too optimistic, leading to chronic schedule overruns despite teams knowing their own historical track record.

## Indicators
- Estimates personal odds of a bad outcome as lower than statistical rates, without a specific reason to expect different results
- Makes projections that consistently exceed what comparable situations have historically produced
- Skips contingency planning because failure doesn't feel like a realistic possibility
- Treats the best-case scenario as the plan rather than as one scenario among several
- Dismisses historical base rates with "but this situation is different" or "that was them, not us"

## False Positives
- Accurate positive expectations in domains where the person has genuine skill that justifies above-average outcomes are not optimism bias
- Motivational optimism that improves performance — sometimes called self-efficacy — is distinct from biased probability estimation
- Expressing confidence publicly while privately maintaining calibrated uncertainty is a communication behavior, not a cognitive bias

## Related Biases
Overconfidence Bias, Planning Fallacy, Dunning-Kruger Effect, Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I'm starting a restaurant. Everyone I've told has been supportive. A friend who works in hospitality mentioned the failure rate for restaurants in their first three years but I told him my situation is different — I have a strong concept, a great location, and the timing is right. The failure rate is for restaurants without those things. Mine has them.

[Management] We're launching this product in Q4. My team's estimate is five months of work. I told the board four months. I've pushed my team hard before and we've always delivered. This time we might be dealing with something slightly more complicated but I know what my team can do. We'll find a way to compress the timeline.

[Medical] My doctor mentioned that people with my family history should get screened more frequently for this type of cancer. I told her I was planning to get the screening but I haven't scheduled it. That disease is for people who don't exercise or who eat badly. I do neither. My family history is concerning on paper but I feel genuinely healthy. I'll get to it.

[Consumer] I'm buying this house at the top of my budget. My mortgage payment will be tight for the first few years. I'm not worried — my salary should increase, the market in this neighborhood only goes up, and I'm handy enough to handle any maintenance issues myself rather than paying contractors. Something will always work out.

[Legal] We're in a contract dispute and I'm certain we're going to prevail. Our position is clearly stronger. I've told my business partner not to worry — the other side will back down once they see we're serious. My attorney has been a bit more cautious in her language but I think she's just being appropriately conservative. I've been in disputes before. I know which way this is heading.

[Technical] I told my manager this integration would take two weeks. I said that in my head because two weeks is when I want it done, not because I mapped out all the steps. There are a few unknowns in the third-party API that I'm not worried about. APIs always have documentation, and if the documentation is incomplete you figure it out. I'll have this wrapped up by the end of the sprint.

[Educational] I enrolled in three courses this semester and also committed to a research assistantship. My advisor said it was a lot for one semester. I said I work well under pressure and I'm good at time management. The last two semesters I've done fine with demanding schedules. I have a system. This is manageable.

[Social] I said yes to hosting a dinner party for twelve people even though I've never hosted more than six before. I'll figure out the logistics. People always say hosting is harder than it looks but they're probably not that organized. I'm extremely organized. I wrote a rough menu in twenty minutes. This is going to be great.

[Family] We're doing the kitchen renovation ourselves to save money. We've watched a lot of tutorial videos and we're both pretty handy. Our contractor friend said a kitchen renovation typically takes twice as long as you expect when you're doing it yourself. I told him we've accounted for that. We've budgeted for twelve weeks. Our contractor friend said fourteen at minimum. We're starting in eight weeks.

[Political] I'm running for a school board seat in a district where my party hasn't won in twelve years. I know the incumbents have advantages — name recognition, a volunteer base, a track record. But this cycle feels different to me. I've been getting good responses at community events. People are ready for change. I'm going to win this. I feel it.

[Financial] I'm leaving my job to start a consulting practice. I have three contacts who have expressed interest in working with me. I've told my wife I expect to replace my income within three months. I haven't done a formal analysis of how long it typically takes to build a client base or what my monthly burn rate will be if it takes longer. Three months feels right to me. I know my field well and I have good relationships.

[Management] I promised a client we'd finish the project in six weeks. My team is stretched and this project is more complex than it initially appeared. But I told the client six weeks because I want to keep the relationship, and I believe my team can do it. When there's a deadline and a client relationship on the line, my team always finds a way. We always have.

[Medical] I've been putting off getting my moles checked. I know I should — my skin type and the amount of sun I got growing up make it something I should monitor. But I've been telling myself I'd know if something looked wrong and nothing looks wrong. I feel healthy. I'll get to the dermatologist when I have time. I don't think I'm the kind of person who has to worry about this.

[Consumer] I'm planning my renovation budget at $60,000. My neighbor who had a similar renovation said hers came in at $95,000. I told her our project is more straightforward. She said she'd thought that too. I smiled and said we'd researched our contractors better. I believe this. I don't have a specific reason to believe it other than the fact that I've planned it carefully.

[Educational] I'm writing my dissertation in one summer. I've blocked eight weeks and I have a clear outline. My advisor says most dissertations take longer than students plan. I know that's true in general. I'm an efficient writer, I have the data collected, and I know exactly what I want to say. Eight weeks is aggressive but I work best under pressure. I'll have a draft to her by September.

[Financial] I've put almost all of my savings into this startup — my own startup. I believe in it deeply. The product is genuinely better than what's on the market. When I think about the probability of failure I believe it's low. The failure rate for startups in general is high but that's because most founders don't have my level of domain expertise, my network, or this specific market insight. My startup is different.

[Legal] We're suing our landlord for lease violations. The evidence is straightforward and I'm confident we'll win. My attorney said commercial landlord-tenant disputes often turn on procedural details that aren't obvious at the start. I said our documentation is solid. I believe we're going to prevail. We've followed every step correctly. I can't see how this goes badly for us.

[Social] I'm planning to train for a marathon this year. I've run 5Ks before. The jump from 5K to marathon is large but I'm young and I've been active my whole life. I've downloaded a training plan. I haven't mapped out the time commitment against my actual schedule or spoken to a running coach. It'll work out. I'll find the time. I always do.

[Technical] I'm pitching a new architecture to the engineering team next week that would require migrating our core database. I'm confident the migration will take about three months. I haven't done a detailed dependency analysis yet but based on my experience with similar systems, three months feels right. There will probably be some complications but nothing my team and I can't handle.

[Management] I took on two new direct reports this quarter in addition to my existing team. My manager asked if I had bandwidth for that. I said yes. I've been managing people for eight years — I know how to scale up. The first few weeks have been busier than I expected but it's just an adjustment period. Everything will normalize. I don't think I was wrong to say yes.

[Medical] I've been under a lot of stress for about a year. I know chronic stress has real health consequences — my doctor has mentioned it. I keep telling myself it's temporary, that things will calm down soon, that I'm handling it. I've been saying things will calm down for most of the year. I haven't changed anything structurally. But I feel like I can continue at this pace. People manage this all the time.

[Financial] My brother and I are launching a food truck together. We have no food service experience. We've eaten at many great food trucks and we know what makes a good product. We've budgeted $40,000 for the whole operation, which our research suggests might be slightly low. We're confident we can make it work for that budget. We're careful spenders and we're motivated. The $40,000 will be enough.

[Consumer] I'm booking this trip on a tight budget. I haven't built in much buffer for unexpected costs. Every trip I've taken I've managed to stay on budget by being resourceful. There will probably be a few surprises but I know how to handle them. It's going to be a great trip. I'm not a worrier.

[Technical] I said yes to a client's requested feature in six weeks. I said yes before checking with my development team whether that was feasible. I have a good team. Six weeks seemed like enough time for what he described. I'll brief the team tomorrow. If there are concerns I'll address them then. I'm confident we can make it work.

[Political] My city is planning a major infrastructure project that we estimate will cost $180 million. Our finance director mentioned that comparable projects in other cities have run 30–50% over budget. I told her our procurement process is better and we've learned from those other cities' mistakes. We've budgeted a 10% contingency. I believe we'll come in on time and under budget. I have strong reasons for believing this. I also have many fewer specific reasons than I'd need to actually justify the belief.

[Management] I'm not going to get sick this quarter. I know that's not something anyone can plan on. But I haven't built any contingency into my project schedule for illness or personal disruptions. Everyone on my team has their tasks sequenced with no buffer. We'll be fine. Nothing significant has gone wrong in the last two quarters. We're in a good rhythm.

[Family] I've started a college savings plan for my daughter. I expect to contribute for seventeen years and have more than enough for tuition. I've modeled this assuming my income grows at a steady rate, the education costs don't rise faster than my contributions, and I don't have any significant financial disruptions in the next seventeen years. Those are large assumptions. I believe they're reasonable.

[Educational] I'm applying to twelve graduate programs. My undergraduate record is strong and my research experience is good. I've told my parents I expect to get into at least four or five of them. My advisor has been more cautious and said top programs are unpredictable and I shouldn't expect anything specific. I think she's being appropriately conservative on my behalf. I feel good about my application. I'm going to get in somewhere excellent.

[Legal] I'm representing myself in a small claims dispute. I have the facts on my side. The law seems clear. I've read the relevant statutes. I feel prepared. My neighbor who went through something similar with a lawyer said it was harder than it looked even with legal representation. I told him I'm a good writer and a clear thinker. I'll do fine. The judge will see what happened.

[Social] I'm starting a podcast. I know there are millions of podcasts and most don't build a meaningful audience. But I have a good voice, a clear niche, and genuine knowledge of my subject. The ones that fail are mostly people without a clear concept. Mine has a clear concept. I'll grow an audience in six months. I've been planning this for two years. It's going to work.

[Technical] We're launching in beta next month. There will be bugs — there always are. But I expect the beta to go smoothly enough that we can move to general release within eight weeks. My QA team has found everything significant. I believe we're in good shape. Betas always surface some things. I'm not anticipating anything serious. Nothing in our testing suggests serious issues. We're ready.

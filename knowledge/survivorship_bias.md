# Survivorship Bias

## Definition
The logical error of focusing on entities that passed a selection process while overlooking those that did not, typically because the failures are invisible or underrepresented. The surviving population is not representative of the original population. Drawing conclusions only from survivors leads to systematically optimistic assessments of success rates, performance of strategies, and properties of successful entities. The bias is particularly dangerous in fields where failures leave no record.

## Examples
In finance, mutual fund performance data overstates average returns because failed funds are removed from historical databases. Only funds that survived long enough are included in performance comparisons, making the average look better than the true outcome for investors who chose randomly.

In business, startup success stories dominate business school curricula and popular media. The companies that followed identical strategies and failed are absent from the record, making the featured strategies appear more reliable than they are.

In military history, the WWII example: analysts recommended adding armor to the parts of planes that had the most bullet holes. Abraham Wald pointed out that the planes with holes in those places were the ones that returned — they survived. Armor was needed where there were no holes, because planes hit there didn't make it back.

In medicine, historical treatments evaluated only on patients who completed them ignore dropout rates, leading to overestimated efficacy.

## Indicators
- Draws conclusions about what works by looking only at the successes, without asking what the failures looked like
- Believes a strategy or trait leads to success because all the visible successful people used it or have it
- Does not ask what traits or strategies the people who failed also had
- Makes optimistic estimates about average outcomes based on data that only includes the cases that survived to be counted
- Forms a confident view of what leads to success from examples that survived long enough to be visible, without noticing that failed examples are absent from the picture

## False Positives
- Studying successful practitioners to learn from them is not survivorship bias when the goal is to understand what they did, not to estimate base rates of success
- Filtering a sample for relevance is not survivorship bias — selecting only relevant cases for analysis is appropriate study design
- When the survivor population is acknowledged as a non-representative sample, using it to generate hypotheses (not conclusions) is valid

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Optimism Bias, Confirmation Bias, Representativeness Heuristic

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I've been listening to interviews with successful angel investors talking about their strategy — how they evaluate teams, what sectors they focus on, how they think about risk. I've been building my own approach based on their patterns. I don't know anything about investors who followed similar patterns and never had a successful exit. The ones being interviewed are by definition the ones who had wins to talk about.

[Management] My CEO loves to share business books featuring successful company turnarounds. We've read three this year and distilled lessons about decisive leadership, bold moves, and willingness to bet on big changes. I've been applying those principles to our own challenges. I haven't read any books about companies that made those same bold moves and didn't survive. The books on the shelf are the ones with happy endings.

[Educational] I read an essay by a novelist who described dropping out of college to pursue writing and said it was the best decision of her life. The essay was moving and she's now a celebrated author. I've been thinking about it in the context of my own decisions about formal education. I haven't read the essays by people who made the same choice and are working administrative jobs while still trying to publish. Those essays don't typically get featured in literary magazines.

[Technical] Everyone I talk to in my engineering circle uses a specific technical stack and credits it with enabling their fast iteration and product quality. They're all at companies that scaled well. I haven't spoken to anyone at a company that used the same stack and struggled or failed. The companies that used this stack and failed aren't at conferences talking about it.

[Social] My extended family is full of stories about people who emigrated, worked hard, and built successful lives. The family narrative is one of determination and success. I've noticed we don't tell stories about the family members who emigrated and struggled permanently or returned. The success stories have been preserved and repeated. The difficult outcomes have been absorbed quietly and aren't part of the family's self-understanding.

[Consumer] I've been reading reviews for this investment product and most of the customer testimonials describe excellent returns over two to five years. The product's marketing highlights these success stories. I don't know whether those stories represent most customers or the exceptional ones who were selected for testimonials. The people who lost money on the product aren't writing endorsement testimonials.

[Political] Our policy team studied a set of cities that had implemented the same urban renewal program and saw strong outcomes. We presented the approach to our council as proven. A researcher reviewing our work asked which cities had tried the same program and not seen the same outcomes. We hadn't looked. The cities in our analysis were probably not a random sample.

[Family] My grandfather built a successful small business from nothing. The family talks about it as proof that hard work and quality product are enough to succeed. I've been starting my own business partly inspired by this. I've been thinking less about the many small businesses of the same type from the same era that didn't survive. My grandfather's story is in front of me because it succeeded. The others aren't.

[Legal] Our firm has been compiling case studies of times we won against larger, better-resourced opponents. We use them in pitches to show what's possible. A client asked us about cases where we faced similar asymmetries and lost. We struggled to answer. We don't tend to analyze our losses as systematically as our wins. The pitch material is drawn from the cases that survived to become success stories.

[Technical] I've been studying the architecture decisions of highly successful tech companies and trying to extract generalizable lessons. I've read about companies that made unconventional choices that paid off. I haven't looked at companies that made those same unconventional choices and failed. The successful companies get books written about them. The unsuccessful ones disappear and their architecture decisions disappear with them.

[Financial] I keep hearing about people who bought property in this city twenty years ago and are now sitting on significant appreciation. The clear lesson everyone draws is that real estate in this city is a reliable wealth builder. I've been looking at this as my next investment. I haven't looked for data on people who bought in neighborhoods that didn't appreciate, or who bought at peaks, or who had to sell under distress. I'm seeing the successful real estate stories because those are the ones being shared.

[Management] I'm designing a leadership training curriculum and I've been studying the habits and practices of CEOs who have led long-tenured, high-performing companies. I've been building the curriculum around the patterns I see in their stories. A researcher I consulted asked what I knew about the habits and practices of CEOs who led companies that performed poorly. I hadn't sought that out. The long-tenured high performers are visible. The failed CEOs are mostly invisible.

[Educational] Our department's alumni magazine features success stories from graduates. We use these in our admissions presentations. They're all doing interesting and rewarding things with their degrees. I was recently part of an alumni survey effort and the response rate was about 12%. The magazine features people who respond to alumni outreach. People who had difficult post-graduation experiences are likely underrepresented in the 12%.

[Medical] A cancer specialist I spoke to said his treatment program has had excellent outcomes in the cases he can cite — several long-term remissions, patients with better quality of life than expected. I've been researching whether this treatment is right for my father. I asked to see published outcomes data rather than case narratives. Published outcomes include the patients who didn't do as well. The specialist's citable cases were the ones that went well.

[Financial] My friend group is full of crypto enthusiasts who all have stories about significant gains. Some of them are on their second or third wave. I've been hearing about this long enough that I started assuming most people who get into crypto do well. I recently read a survey showing median returns for retail crypto investors are negative over most holding periods. I'd been hearing from the people who had wins and they were the most vocal members of my social network.

[Consumer] I follow several personal finance influencers who describe their investment returns and financial independence journeys. They've all done extremely well. I've been calibrating my own expectations based on what they've achieved. I don't follow any accounts from people who followed similar strategies and ended up in worse financial shape — they don't create personal finance influencer accounts.

[Social] I've been asking successful people in my field for advice on career development. Every conversation produces insights about what worked for them — specific moves, specific risks they took, specific relationships they built. I've been building my own playbook from these conversations. None of the people I'm talking to are people who made similar moves and didn't reach similar success. The advisors I have access to are the ones who won.

[Political] Our campaign team is studying recent electoral upsets — cases where underdogs with limited resources defeated heavily favored incumbents. We're identifying patterns in their messaging and ground game to inform our approach. My campaign strategist raised a question I hadn't asked: how many campaigns tried similar approaches and lost without becoming famous examples? The celebrated upsets are famous because they're rare. The template includes the wins, not the attempts.

[Technical] I've been reading post-mortems from companies that successfully navigated major technical debt cleanups. The patterns are consistent — they're encouraging stories about teams that took brave technical decisions and emerged with better codebases. I haven't looked for post-mortems from companies that undertook major technical overhauls and didn't survive the process or regressed significantly. Those post-mortems either weren't written or aren't shared as widely.

[Family] My grandmother always said that she and my grandfather succeeded in life because they never gave up and worked harder than everyone around them. I believe that about them. I also think about the historical period they lived in and how many factors were specific to their context — a particular moment in the economy, networks they had, luck on certain timing decisions. The "never give up" lesson is true. It's also incomplete. People who never gave up and didn't succeed are not in the family stories.

[Financial] The venture fund I'm considering investing in has a returns track record that looks excellent. The fund manager shared it in the pitch. I asked how they handle funds that were wound down — are those in the track record? They said the track record shows realized returns from active investments. Funds wound down before generating returns may not be systematically included in the presented track record. I need to look at all the funds, not the ones that survived to report returns.

[Management] I've been reading memoirs by successful founders who describe how they navigated near-failure moments, how they pivoted, how they maintained conviction through adversity. The memoirs are inspiring. The people who navigated the same near-failure moments and then actually failed mostly didn't write memoirs. The lesson about resilience and conviction is drawn from a selected sample.

[Educational] Our medical school has a wall of distinguished alumni who went on to lead institutions, build research programs, or make significant clinical contributions. We show prospective students this wall. It's genuinely impressive. It doesn't show the graduates who practiced for a few years and left medicine, who struggled with the demands of the profession, or who experienced difficult outcomes. The wall is a survival narrative.

[Technical] I've been reading about companies that successfully transitioned from on-premises software to SaaS and emerged stronger. These transitions are celebrated in tech media as strategic pivots done right. I haven't found equivalent coverage of companies that attempted the same transition and failed — lost customers, couldn't sustain the development cost, saw the subscription model undermine their existing revenue. The coverage I'm finding is drawn from the successful transitions.

[Financial] A real estate investor I admire has generated strong returns with a specific strategy in a specific market. She's been generous with her approach and I've been adapting it for my own portfolio. She bought in a period and a market with specific tailwinds. I don't know how many people followed similar strategies in adjacent markets during the same period and lost money. Her strategy might be broadly applicable or it might have succeeded partly because of conditions that may not repeat.

[Consumer] My gym's wall has photos of members who achieved significant physical transformations. The gym sells programs based partly on these stories. I signed up. I haven't asked what fraction of members who follow the same programs achieve similar results. The people on the wall are there because they had excellent results. The people with typical results aren't on the wall.

[Social] I've been inspired by stories of people who made significant career pivots in their forties and built thriving second careers. The stories I've read are from people who succeeded at this. The people who pivoted and found themselves in economically precarious situations don't show up in the career pivot success story genre. The sample I'm drawing from is not representative.

[Legal] Our firm was evaluating whether to take on a class of litigation that's new to us. We found three firms that had entered this space and succeeded. We used them as evidence that entry is viable. A litigation consultant asked us how we'd identified those three firms — had we looked at all firms that entered this area, or only the successful ones? We'd found them through their own press releases and bar association recognition. Those are, by design, firms that had success to publicize.

[Technical] I've been studying successful open-source projects to understand what drives adoption. All the projects I've studied have large communities, clear documentation, and active maintenance. These characteristics might cause adoption or they might be characteristics of successful projects that happened to get adopted for reasons I'm not seeing. The projects that had clear documentation and active maintenance and didn't get adopted aren't in my dataset because no one wrote articles about them.

[Political] Our party has been conducting a lessons-learned analysis from recent election cycles where we won. We're identifying campaign strategies, messaging approaches, and candidate profiles that correlate with the wins. We're not systematically analyzing losses with the same frameworks. A political scientist reviewing our analysis said the lessons from winning campaigns are only meaningful when compared to what was tried in losing campaigns. We're building a theory from the wins.

[Management] I've been looking at companies that successfully implemented four-day work weeks and stayed competitive. There are quite a few compelling examples now. My chief people officer raised a question I hadn't thought carefully about: are the companies I'm finding a random sample of all companies that tried four-day work weeks? Or are they the ones that tried it, had positive outcomes, and therefore publicized the story? She asked me to look for companies that tried and reversed course. That data is harder to find.

[Financial] I hired a financial advisor whose marketing materials feature client success stories — clients who achieved early financial independence, doubled their net worth over a decade, navigated difficult markets successfully. These stories helped me choose her. I didn't ask whether those stories represented her typical client outcomes or her exceptional ones. The exceptional outcomes make the marketing. The typical outcomes don't.

# Survivorship Bias

## Definition
The logical error of focusing on entities that passed a selection process while overlooking those that did not, typically because the failures are invisible or underrepresented. The surviving population is not representative of the original population. Drawing conclusions only from survivors leads to systematically optimistic assessments of success rates, performance of strategies, and properties of successful entities. The bias is particularly dangerous in fields where failures leave no record.

## Examples
In finance, mutual fund performance data overstates average returns because failed funds are removed from historical databases. Only funds that survived long enough are included in performance comparisons, making the average look better than the true outcome for investors who chose randomly.

In business, startup success stories dominate business school curricula and popular media. The companies that followed identical strategies and failed are absent from the record, making the featured strategies appear more reliable than they are.

In military history, the WWII example: analysts recommended adding armor to the parts of planes that had the most bullet holes. Abraham Wald pointed out that the planes with holes in those places were the ones that returned — they survived. Armor was needed where there were no holes, because planes hit there didn't make it back.

In medicine, historical treatments evaluated only on patients who completed them ignore dropout rates, leading to overestimated efficacy.

## Indicators
- Drawing conclusions from a sample that excludes failures
- Belief that strategies work because successful practitioners used them
- Attributing survivor traits to success rather than to survival selection
- Absence of data about the failed population
- Overoptimistic assessments of average outcomes

## False Positives
- Studying successful practitioners to learn from them is not survivorship bias when the goal is to understand what they did, not to estimate base rates of success
- Filtering a sample for relevance is not survivorship bias — selecting only relevant cases for analysis is appropriate study design
- When the survivor population is acknowledged as a non-representative sample, using it to generate hypotheses (not conclusions) is valid

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Optimism Bias, Confirmation Bias, Representativeness Heuristic

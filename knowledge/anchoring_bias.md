# Anchoring Bias

## Definition
The tendency to rely disproportionately on the first piece of information encountered — the anchor — when making subsequent judgments or decisions. Once an anchor is set, all subsequent adjustments are made relative to that anchor, even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant. The anchor exerts a pull even when people are explicitly told it is random. Anchoring is particularly powerful in negotiations, pricing, and numerical estimation, where the first number stated becomes the reference point around which discussion converges.

## Examples
In finance, a stock that traded at $200 last year feels "cheap" at $120 even if $120 is still overvalued based on fundamentals. The $200 price is the anchor. Traders anchor to their purchase price and hold losing positions waiting to "break even."

In retail, a product listed at $500 crossed out with a sale price of $300 anchors customers to the original $500, making $300 feel like a bargain regardless of the item's actual value.

In law, the first damages figure mentioned in a civil lawsuit influences the jury's final award, even when judges instruct the jury to disregard it. Higher initial demands lead to higher settlements.

In salary negotiation, whichever party names a number first anchors the negotiation. A candidate who asks for $120,000 will generally receive a higher offer than one who asks for $90,000, even with identical qualifications.

## Indicators
- Treats the first number heard as the center of gravity for all subsequent estimates
- Stays close to an opening offer in negotiation even when the opening was arbitrary or extreme
- Judges a price as fair or unfair based primarily on what it was before, rather than what it is worth now
- Uses last year's budget or last quarter's price as the primary reference for a current decision without questioning whether that reference is still valid
- Adjusts from a starting point rather than reasoning from scratch about what the answer should be

## False Positives
- Using a well-researched comparable as a benchmark is rational reference-class forecasting, not anchoring
- A negotiator who starts high as a deliberate tactic is not themselves anchored — they are exploiting the other party's anchoring tendency
- Adjusting gradually toward new information when evidence is genuinely uncertain is appropriate under ambiguity, not a failure to adjust from an anchor

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Status Quo Bias, Framing Effect, Recency Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I bought this stock at $180. It dropped to $90 and I've been holding ever since. I keep waiting for it to get back to $180 before I sell. My advisor says the company's fundamentals have changed and $90 might actually be fair value now. But I can't see it that way. $180 is what it's worth to me.

[Management] My team asked for $240,000 for the new marketing budget. I said that was too high and pushed back to $180,000. We ended up settling at $210,000. I felt like I'd negotiated a win. My CFO later pointed out that the project probably only needed $150,000 — we'd been working around that first number the whole conversation.

[Consumer] The original price tag said $850. It was on sale for $520. I felt like I was saving $330. I didn't stop to wonder whether $850 was a realistic price for what it was. The moment I saw that crossed-out number, $520 felt like a deal.

[Legal] The plaintiff's attorney opened by asking for $4.2 million in damages. The number seemed outrageous but it stuck in my head. When we finally discussed what was reasonable, our estimates kept hovering around the million-dollar mark. I don't know where that came from. But looking back, nothing we said was disconnected from that opening ask.

[Medical] My doctor mentioned that patients who don't treat this early can end up taking 12 pills a day in five years. Now I'm seriously considering the 2-pill daily regimen she's recommending. Two pills feels modest compared to twelve. I'm not sure I would have agreed as quickly if she hadn't mentioned the twelve first.

[Social] I was asked to guess how old the wine was before they told me. I said maybe six years. Then someone mentioned a figure — twenty — and we all started adjusting from there. By the time the bottle was revealed to be nine years old, we'd all been orbiting twenty for ten minutes. The first number had pulled us across the room from the right answer.

[Family] We were buying our first house and the listing was at $595,000. We thought it was overpriced by maybe 5–10%. We offered $570,000. We ended up at $580,000. I felt good about that. Six months later I found two comparable sales in the same street at $520,000. We'd been negotiating around their number the whole time.

[Educational] The department chair mentioned that the last time this fellowship stipend was reviewed, the committee landed on $28,000. The conversation this year started from that point. Nobody went back to check what the work actually required or what peer institutions pay. We ended up at $29,500 and called it an increase. I'm not sure anyone questioned whether $28,000 was the right starting point.

[Technical] We got a quote for the system migration from the first vendor: $380,000. Every subsequent vendor we interviewed, I was measuring against that number. When the third vendor quoted $260,000, it felt like a bargain. I didn't redo the analysis from scratch. I just felt the distance from $380,000.

[Political] The opposition party proposed a 15% cut to the transit budget. By the time the debate was over we'd "compromised" at a 6% cut. The final number felt like a reasonable middle ground. But we'd started from their number, not from an analysis of what the system actually needed. I don't know what we would have landed on if they'd never named 15%.

[Financial] I've held this mutual fund for twelve years. The high-water mark was about $340,000. It's sitting at $280,000 now. I tell people I'm down $60,000. My advisor points out that I originally invested $110,000 — I'm actually up significantly. But I can't feel that. The $340,000 peak is my reference point and everything is measured against it.

[Management] We benchmarked our senior engineer salaries against a report from two years ago that showed a median of $140,000 in our market. We built our compensation structure around that. Last month a candidate told me their current offer from a competitor was $178,000. I thought they were inflating the number. I later checked and the market had moved and we were significantly below it. That two-year-old $140,000 had shaped everything.

[Consumer] The used car dealer started by showing me a BMW at $34,000. Then he showed me a Toyota Camry at $22,000. The Toyota looked like a steal. I didn't think about whether $22,000 was a good price for a used Camry of that year and mileage. It just felt cheap after the BMW. I bought the Camry. I'm not sure it was the right call.

[Legal] Our attorney told us to expect the other side to demand a 60-day compliance timeline. When they actually asked for 45 days, our whole team felt like we'd won something. We hadn't done the analysis of what was actually realistic — we'd been priced against the number our attorney planted. We said yes to 45 days and then scrambled to make it work.

[Medical] The specialist said the procedure could run anywhere from three to five hours. It took four hours and fifteen minutes. My family in the waiting room was relieved — "it went faster than expected." But it went about as long as you'd expect for a procedure like this. The initial mention of five hours made four hours fifteen minutes feel short.

[Social] We were planning a birthday party and my sister said she'd been to a venue that charged $5,000. We started budgeting from there. We found a place for $2,800 and thought we'd done well. I don't know what a reasonable party budget actually was — we'd just measured down from that first $5,000.

[Family] My parents told me they paid $85 per month for their phone plan. I called to check mine and it was $95. I spent forty-five minutes negotiating it down to $82. I felt victorious. I later discovered my colleagues are all paying $60 for equivalent plans. I'd been negotiating against my parents' rate instead of asking what the market looked like.

[Educational] The professor mentioned in passing that his previous research grant was $800,000. When I was scoping my first independent study, I started my budget estimates near that number. My department coordinator looked at the draft and pointed out that projects like mine typically run $40,000–$60,000. I'd been inflating everything from the moment he mentioned $800,000.

[Technical] The first engineer we consulted said the infrastructure overhaul would take eighteen months. We planned around that. All subsequent estimates from our own team got calibrated against that number — two engineers independently said "maybe a bit under eighteen months." Our project manager later looked at the scope and said a realistic estimate was eight months. We'd been attached to that first number.

[Political] The campaign's polling consultant mentioned that in similar districts, a candidate typically needs to spend $1.2 million to be competitive. Everything after that orbited $1.2 million. When a cheaper digital strategy was proposed that similar campaigns had used for $400,000, the campaign team kept saying it "didn't feel like enough." The original number had shaped everyone's sense of what was adequate.

[Financial] My first salary out of college was $42,000. Five years later I was at $58,000 and felt well-compensated. A recruiter told me peers in my field were averaging $75,000–$85,000. I was shocked — I'd been mentally measuring everything against that first $42,000 and its incremental increases. I had no idea how far my reference point had drifted from the market.

[Management] We opened salary negotiations with the candidate at $95,000. She countered at $112,000. We went to $101,000 and she accepted. Our HR team felt good about staying close to our opening number. Our department head later told me the role was budgeted to $115,000 and we'd had the room to pay it. We'd anchored ourselves.

[Consumer] The contractor gave me an estimate for the kitchen renovation: $65,000. I spent three weeks trying to bring that number down. I got it to $55,000 and signed the contract. A friend who had done a similar renovation told me she paid $38,000. I'd been fighting around the contractor's number instead of starting from comparable projects.

[Legal] The employment lawyer quoted her retainer at $15,000 to handle the dispute. I thought that was high. After some back and forth, we agreed on $10,500. I walked out feeling I'd saved $4,500. A different attorney I consulted afterward said she would have handled it for $6,000. I'd been negotiating against the original quote's gravity the whole time.

[Medical] The nutritionist told me that some of her patients consume 4,000 calories a day before they work with her. She recommended I aim for 2,200. I've been eating about 2,400 and feeling like I'm nearly there. Before that conversation I had no idea how many calories I was eating. I'm not sure 2,200 was actually the right target for my body — I never questioned it after she mentioned 4,000.

[Social] We were discussing salary at a dinner party and someone said they'd read that the average household income in our city was $185,000. I started thinking about whether I was above or below that. Later I looked it up and the actual median was $72,000 — someone had confused mean and median or misremembered. But I'd spent thirty minutes recalibrating my sense of how I was doing relative to a number that was wrong.

[Family] My daughter's piano teacher mentioned she'd seen students take up to four years to reach grade 5. I've been mentally measuring my daughter's progress against that timeline. She's been playing two years and is at grade 3. I keep telling her she's ahead of schedule. I have no idea if four years was a representative benchmark or an outlier. It was just the first number I heard.

[Educational] The admissions office mentioned that most competitive applicants have around 250 hours of community service listed. I panicked — I only had 80. I spent the next month searching for opportunities. I later found out that 250 was the figure for applicants to very elite programs; my target programs typically saw 60–100 hours. I'd been chasing a number that wasn't even relevant to my situation.

[Technical] Our first vendor proposal for the cloud migration quoted 14 weeks. Every internal planning document I made after that used 14 weeks as the baseline. When our own architect said she thought 9 weeks was achievable, I pushed back: "That seems fast compared to the estimates we've seen." I was defending a timeline I'd never analyzed myself.

[Political] The city's infrastructure report said upgrading the water system could cost up to $80 million. The council started budgeting around $80 million. When a civil engineer presented a phased approach that would achieve the same goals for $44 million, some council members felt the lower figure meant corners were being cut. The $80 million had become the implied definition of adequate.

[Financial] I've been tracking this crypto asset since I first bought it at $0.80. It went up to $4.20 and I sold a portion. Now it's at $1.60 and I keep thinking of it as "down from its high" and waiting to buy back at a lower price. A friend who bought in last month sees $1.60 as her entry point and thinks it looks reasonable. We're looking at the same asset but through completely different numbers.

[Management] I got a quote for event catering at $180 per head. I spent a week negotiating it down to $155. I was proud of myself. When my finance colleague looked at the invoice she said corporate events like ours typically run $90–$110 per head in this city. I'd been so focused on lowering the caterer's price that I'd never looked at what catering actually costs.

[Consumer] The gym I wanted had a joining fee of $500. I pushed back and got it waived. I signed up feeling like I'd already gotten a good deal and didn't negotiate the monthly rate at all. The monthly rate was $95. Comparable gyms in my neighborhood are $55–$60 per month. I saved $500 on joining and will overpay hundreds in the first year.

[Legal] Our opposing counsel's opening demand was $3.8 million. My client's first reaction was to counter at $1 million. Everyone spent the next three months discussing numbers between $1 million and $3.8 million. An independent mediator told us that comparable verdicts in our jurisdiction typically run $500,000–$700,000. We'd been playing inside a range that had nothing to do with the actual market.

[Medical] The pharmacist told me the name-brand version of my medication costs $320 per month. I felt shocked. She then mentioned the generic was $28. I immediately said yes to the generic without comparing it to other generics or asking about discount programs. $28 felt so cheap against $320 that I stopped evaluating entirely.

[Social] My gym trainer mentioned that elite athletes do five hours of strength training per week. I started aiming for three hours, felt like a reasonable approximation. I've been doing two and feeling guilty. A trainer at another gym told me thirty to forty-five minutes three times a week is appropriate for my goals. The five-hour benchmark had been pulling me in the wrong direction all along.

[Family] We were trying to agree on a price for my parents' house before listing it. My brother said he read that houses in that neighborhood had sold for around $480,000 a year ago. We spent an entire evening arguing about a price near $480,000. Our realtor came back with a comparative market analysis showing the current range was $395,000–$420,000. We'd built a whole family disagreement around a stale data point.

[Educational] My dissertation committee mentioned that a strong submission usually runs around 90,000 words. I've been writing toward 90,000 words for a year. I've been cutting things that were genuinely important just to stay near that number. A professor from another department told me the expectation in my field is 60,000–70,000 words. I'd been writing to a benchmark from a different discipline.

[Technical] The first security audit we commissioned quoted a scope of thirty critical vulnerabilities to address. Our team organized the entire remediation roadmap around that number. The second audit, done by a different firm, found twelve. Instead of trusting the twelve, my first instinct was to question the second firm's thoroughness — the original thirty had become my mental baseline for what "secure" looked like.

[Political] The union's opening bargaining position was a 14% wage increase. Management's opening position was 2%. We all knew neither was the real number. But the final settlement landed at 7% — exactly halfway. Neither side had independently analyzed what a fair increase would be. The two opening numbers averaged each other out and that became the answer.

[Financial] I saw an apartment listed at $2,800 per month that was clearly overpriced for the neighborhood. I kept that $2,800 in my head. When I found a place for $2,100, I felt like I was saving $700 a month. I didn't check what similar units in that building actually rented for. The inflated listing had become my reference point for what was cheap.

# Escalation of Commitment

## Definition
The tendency to continue investing in a failing course of action beyond what rational analysis would justify, often increasing the level of commitment over time even as evidence of failure mounts. Closely related to the sunk cost fallacy, escalation of commitment specifically involves active increase of investment in response to negative feedback, rather than merely continuing a prior level of investment. It is driven by sunk cost reasoning, ego protection, and consistency bias — the desire to appear consistent with one's prior commitments. Escalation is particularly dangerous in organizational settings where decision-makers are publicly committed to a course of action.

## Examples
In finance, a fund manager who has publicly advocated for a large position in a company continues to increase the position as the price falls and the thesis proves wrong, compounding losses rather than exiting.

In technology, an organization that has committed to a failing ERP implementation continues funding additional development years past the point where the evidence of fundamental design flaws is clear, because the decision-makers are publicly identified with the project.

In politics, government military commitments demonstrate classic escalation: forces are increased in response to setbacks rather than withdrawn, because withdrawal would require acknowledging that the initial commitment was a mistake.

In construction, projects that overrun their initial budget and timeline receive additional funding in subsequent reviews based on "we're too far in to stop now" reasoning, even when the cost to complete exceeds the value of completion.

In relationships and partnerships, organizations continue dysfunctional vendor relationships or business partnerships long past the point where the costs clearly exceed the benefits, because the history of investment makes exit feel like abandonment.

## Indicators
- Puts in more time or money after receiving bad results, to justify what has already been spent
- Defends a failing course of action publicly despite mounting evidence that it is not working
- Treats stopping as giving up and continuing as being resilient, regardless of what the evidence shows
- Looks for information that supports continuing rather than reassessing whether the direction is still right
- Cannot separate the decision from personal identity, making it feel impossible to reverse course

## False Positives
- Persisting through a known difficult phase of a genuinely sound project is not escalation — the key distinction is whether evidence of fundamental failure is present and being discounted
- Increasing investment in a project where the initial setbacks are attributable to known, addressable issues is rational problem-solving
- Commitment to a long-term strategy through short-term adversity, when the long-term thesis is still sound, is disciplined investment, not escalation

## Related Biases
Sunk Cost Fallacy, Status Quo Bias, Confirmation Bias, Overconfidence Bias

## Story Patterns

[Management] We've been building this product for three years. The last two market validation exercises came back lukewarm. The last one came back negative. My board asked if we should pivot. I told them we're too close to breakthrough to stop now. I've gone back to the team and asked for another six months and another push on marketing. I know I am the one who stood up at every all-hands and told everyone this was our future. I can't be the person who says we were wrong.

[Financial] I averaged down on this stock three times now. I bought at $80, bought more at $55, and bought more at $32. It's at $19 today. My financial advisor tells me to stop putting more money in. She says the thesis has changed and I need to assess it on its current merits. I keep telling her the original thesis is intact and this is just market noise. I've put in $70,000. I'm not able to see it clearly anymore. I need it to come back.

[Technical] This software migration has been running 18 months over schedule. It was supposed to take a year, we're at 30 months, and we still have at least eight months of work. Every quarter I go to the executive team and ask for more funding and more time. I tell them we're in the final stretch. We've heard that from me before. I'm the architect who said the original plan was sound. I can't go back in that room and say I was wrong from the beginning.

[Legal] We've been in this litigation for six years. Legal fees are at $2.8 million. My lawyer has told me twice now that the remaining potential recovery doesn't justify continued litigation. Every time she says it, I tell her we've come this far. I've told my family we would win. I've told people at work this was a clear case. I can't stand down now. I'm going to see it through.

[Educational] I'm in the fourth year of a PhD program in a field that has almost no jobs. I know this. My advisor knows this. My department placed three graduates in the last five years. When my parents ask what I'll do with the degree, I talk about the research I'm passionate about and the small number of positions that do exist. I've spent four years here. I've told everyone this is my path. I can't go back now.

[Political] I publicly championed this urban development project eighteen months ago. I stood at a podium. I gave quotes to journalists. Now the construction bids came in 60% over the projected cost and the community opposition has grown significantly. A council colleague is asking me to vote against the next funding approval. I can't do that. I'm the one who made this project visible. If I vote against it now, everything I said looks like I didn't know what I was doing.

[Social] I've been renovating this house myself for two years. It was supposed to take eight months. It's now costing me double the original estimate and my wife wants to hire contractors to finish it. I told her we've come this far. I can't bring someone in now — I've spent two years saying I could do this. We have $40,000 in materials in this house. I keep starting earlier in the morning and working later into the night.

[Family] My parents started a business together fifteen years ago. It hasn't been profitable in three years. They've used most of their savings to keep it running. When I gently suggest it might be time to close it, my father says they've given everything to this business. He says it would mean admitting those fifteen years were wasted. I understand that. But I watch them drain what's left of their retirement.

[Technical] We chose a particular vendor for our enterprise infrastructure three years ago. It's been a poor fit — their support is slow, the product direction has diverged from our needs, and we've spent enormous time on workarounds. A migration would take about six months. I keep pushing for one more round of trying to make it work. My team says we should just migrate. I was the one who pushed for this vendor. I wrote the justification document. I'm not ready to write the one that says I was wrong.

[Management] We've been in this market for eighteen months and traction has been disappointing. My co-founder has started asking if we should regroup and try a different market. I keep saying we just need more time, better positioning, one more campaign. I've been the advocate for this market since the beginning. I've recruited team members specifically for this market's needs. If I say it was the wrong market, I have to answer for eighteen months of that decision.

[Financial] I bought a rental property that has been a difficult investment from the start — expensive repairs, difficult tenants, occupancy gaps. My accountant has shown me the actual return over five years, which is negative when you count my time. He suggests I sell. I keep saying I'm close to turning a corner, the market is appreciating, I just need to stabilize the tenant situation. I've been telling people real estate was the smart play for my portfolio. I can't sell at a loss on something I recommended.

[Educational] My university has been funding a research center that I founded eight years ago. The grant landscape has shifted and we've been operating on institutional funding for two years with little external sponsorship. The dean asked me to consider whether the center's mission could be folded into a larger department. I told her the center needs to remain independent. I named this center. My name is on the founding documents. I'm not ready to dissolve it.

[Political] I pushed hard for this city infrastructure program. It was my signature initiative in my first term. The independent review last year was damning — execution failures, cost overruns, questionable outcomes. My opponents have started citing it. I've been defending it in every public appearance since the review came out. I can't acknowledge the review's findings. This program was supposed to define what I stand for.

[Legal] Our company has been pursuing a patent infringement claim for four years. We've spent $1.8 million in legal fees. The external legal counsel we brought in last year said the claim had weaknesses she was surprised we hadn't settled on two years ago. My general counsel wants to accept the current settlement offer. I keep saying we've invested too much to walk away. I'm aware that's not a legal argument. I can't bring myself to take the settlement.

[Technical] Our game development project started as a one-year effort and we're now in year four. We've publicly announced it twice, delayed both announced windows, and the team is exhausted. The publisher is asking hard questions. I keep saying the vision we had in year one is still achievable and worth achieving. My team has rebuilt core systems twice. I've never seriously asked whether we should redefine the scope dramatically or stop. Too much has happened to say it out loud.

[Management] I've been working with a performance improvement plan for one employee for nine months. She's not meeting the objectives. My HR partner says we've been clear and consistent and the outcome isn't changing. I've kept extending the timeline. I was the one who hired her and advocated strongly for her at the time. Every conversation I have about this feels tied up in that original decision. I know what the data says. I'm not ready to act on it.

[Financial] I invested $50,000 in a friend's restaurant. It's been struggling for two years. He's asked for another $20,000. My wife says we need to stop. I keep saying I can't let him fail now when we're this invested. I know the restaurant industry statistics. I know this restaurant specifically has struggled to find its footing. I'm going to put in the $20,000. I'm the one who believed in him. I can't be the one who makes it fail.

[Social] I organized this community event series and it's been losing money and attendance is declining. Other organizers have suggested we restructure it or stop. I've been looking for a new sponsor, a new venue, a new angle. I told everyone this would be a long-term success. I put my credibility behind it. I'm not willing to be the person who ends it. I think we just need one more good event to turn the momentum.

[Educational] I pushed for my school to adopt a new curriculum last year. Implementation has been bumpy — teacher feedback has been difficult, test scores haven't moved in a positive direction, and two department heads have formally objected. An external reviewer suggested the curriculum may not be the right fit for our student population. I've been defending the implementation, saying we need more time for teachers to adapt. I was the one who championed this publicly. I'm going to keep pushing it.

[Technical] We've been trying to fix a fundamental architectural problem in our product for a year and a half by building around it. Every quarter I tell the board we're almost there. We have $3 million in cumulative development cost on this problem. A consultant we brought in last month said we need to redesign the core module — that building around the problem is making the product progressively less stable. I told him we'd consider it. We haven't moved on his recommendation. I'm not ready to describe two years of work as the wrong approach.

[Management] My team has been working on an internal data platform for two years and a half. I presented it at the executive kickoff as the future of our analytics. It's still not in production. The business users have found workarounds and some have stopped waiting for it. My manager asked last week whether we should consider adopting a third-party tool instead. I told him we're so close. I told him the custom platform will be more tailored to our needs. I can hear how those words sound. I keep saying them anyway.

[Political] I voted for this trade agreement three years ago and defended it loudly. The manufacturing impact in my district has been more significant than the projections suggested. My constituents are unhappy. I've been attributing the job losses to other causes — automation, broader economic shifts — rather than revisiting whether the agreement was as beneficial as I argued. I can't tell the people I represent that I was wrong about something this consequential.

[Financial] My business has been losing money for two years. I've put in $200,000 of my savings. My accountant has been gentle but increasingly direct: the unit economics aren't working and there's no clear path to profitability at our current scale. I've been raising money from friends and family to keep going. I keep telling them the market is turning. I know what the numbers say. I can't stop. I've been telling everyone this was going to work.

[Technical] I've been maintaining this open-source library for six years and it's become technically outdated. The community is small and aging. A much better alternative exists now. I've been putting in weekends keeping it running. My collaborator has been gently suggesting we archive it and recommend users migrate to the alternative. I can't do that. I built this. There are still users depending on it. I'm going to keep maintaining it. Even though I know the other library is better.

[Legal] I've been running this contract dispute through mediation for a year. Four sessions in, the mediator told both parties privately that the range of plausible outcomes was narrower than either side wanted. She recommended settling. My client didn't want to settle. Neither did I, honestly. I've been so invested in the theory of the case I've spent a year developing. I said we'd continue to the fifth session. I know how mediators describe cases that should have settled earlier. I'm becoming the story in her head.

[Management] I pivoted our company's product strategy eighteen months ago based on my conviction about where the market was going. The team I assembled, the sales material we created, the partnership deals I announced — all of it was built around that vision. The market has moved somewhat differently from what I projected. My team is asking hard questions in our planning sessions. I keep saying the direction is right and we're early. I'm aware I might just be the last person to see clearly that we need to turn.

[Social] I organized a community garden project two summers ago. I raised money from neighbors, brought in volunteers, built raised beds. Last summer half the plots went untended and the coordination has been exhausting. A neighbor suggested we scale back dramatically or stop. I said we needed to get through one more season and recruit better. I'm not ready to announce that the thing I was so enthusiastic about didn't work the way I said it would.

[Family] My adult children have been watching me run this rental property portfolio for fifteen years. I've been proud of building it. Two of the properties are genuinely problematic now — maintenance-heavy, low-yield, in areas where values have stagnated. My son-in-law, who is in commercial real estate, has been suggesting for two years that I sell those two and redeploy the capital. I keep saying I know these properties. I built this portfolio. I'll handle it. I can't let go of what I built.

[Educational] I pushed for the school to replace its entire mathematics curriculum last year. I attended conferences, brought in consultants, presented to the board. Implementation has been difficult and the teacher survey results are concerning. The board wants to revisit the decision. I've been presenting the implementation difficulties as a transition issue rather than a signal that the curriculum itself might have been the wrong choice. I was so publicly committed to this change. I can't go back to the board and say I was wrong.

[Technical] We've been trying to make this microservices architecture work for two years. We went from a monolith that worked to a distributed system that has more failure modes than anyone fully understands. Every time a new problem appears my team fixes it and I tell leadership we're through the hard part. My CTO is skeptical and asked last month whether we should consider a strategic retreat to a modular monolith. I can't do that. I was the one who made the case for microservices. We're going to make it work.

[Financial] I started a side business with my brother-in-law three years ago. We've put in $90,000 combined. The business has had three different business models in that time and none have achieved profitability. My sister has asked me to stop. My wife agrees. I keep saying we're close. The relationship with my brother-in-law, the money we've put in, the things I've told people about what we were building — all of it makes stopping feel impossible. I am no longer asking whether we should continue. I'm only asking how.

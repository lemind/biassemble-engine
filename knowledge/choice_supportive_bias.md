# Choice-Supportive Bias

## Definition
The tendency to retroactively attribute positive qualities to an option one has chosen while minimizing its negative qualities, and to attribute negative qualities to the options one did not choose. After making a decision, people selectively reconstruct their memory of the alternatives to make their chosen option appear better than it seemed at the time of decision. This post-hoc rationalization preserves self-esteem and cognitive consistency, but it distorts learning from decisions: people believe their choices were better than they were and miss opportunities to improve their decision process.

## Examples
In consumer behavior, people who have purchased a car recall the features of their chosen car more positively and remember the features of the car they rejected more negatively six months after purchase than at the moment of decision.

In hiring, managers who have hired a candidate recall at the six-month review that the candidate was clearly the best choice, forgetting that the decision was close and that the rejected candidate had significant strengths.

In finance, investors who have bought a stock construct a narrative where the purchase was obviously correct. They selectively recall the bullish analysis they encountered before buying and forget the bearish signals they also saw.

In politics, voters who chose a candidate reconstruct their memory of the election as one where the choice was clear, forgetting that they felt genuine uncertainty at the time. This reinforces party loyalty even when performance disappoints.

In academia, researchers who chose a particular methodology recall their reasoning for that choice as stronger than it was, making it harder to recognize when a different approach would have been better.

## Indicators
- Remembers a past choice as better than it actually seemed at the moment of making it
- Cannot recall the reasons that made the unchosen option look appealing before deciding
- Feels more confident that a past decision was right than the evidence available at the time justified
- Avoids reconsidering a past choice on the grounds that it was obviously the right call, even when new information has arrived
- Forgets having felt uncertain before a decision and now believes the choice was always clear

## False Positives
- Commitment to a decision to avoid second-guessing paralysis is a healthy psychological strategy in some contexts — not all post-decision conviction is choice-supportive bias
- When a decision genuinely was clear-cut and the chosen option genuinely performed better, recall of it as the better choice is accurate
- Learning from experience that one's decision-making process was sound in a particular case and refining it accordingly is appropriate, not a bias

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Sunk Cost Fallacy, Hindsight Bias, Self-serving Bias

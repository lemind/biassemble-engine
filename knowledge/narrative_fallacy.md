# Narrative Fallacy

## Definition
The tendency to construct coherent stories around sequences of events, attributing causality, intention, and meaning to what may be a series of random or unrelated occurrences. Humans are narrative creatures: the brain imposes story structure on information because stories are easier to encode, remember, and communicate than raw probability distributions or disconnected facts. The narrative fallacy leads to overconfident causal explanations, hindsight-inflated predictability, and selection of vivid explanatory stories over accurate statistical models.

## Examples
In finance, stock market movements receive causal narrative explanations every day ("markets fell because investors worried about inflation") even when the stated cause and the effect are uncorrelated and the explanation is post-hoc rationalization of random noise.

In business, the success of companies like Apple or Amazon generates retrospective narratives about visionary strategy and inevitable triumph, ignoring the many companies that followed similar strategies and failed, and the genuine uncertainty that surrounded these companies at each step.

In medicine, case histories are told as coherent diagnostic journeys with clear turning points, when the actual clinical course was characterized by uncertainty, conflicting evidence, and probabilistic reasoning rather than clean narrative logic.

In history, wars are explained through clear chains of cause and effect, attributing enormous consequences to the decisions of specific individuals, when counterfactual history and quantitative analysis suggest outcomes were highly contingent.

In technology, post-mortems of large failures often produce clean narratives of "how it happened" that obscure the complex interplay of independent contributing factors and make the failure seem more predictable and avoidable than it was.

## Indicators
- Confident causal explanations for outcomes that are better characterized as probabilistic or random
- Discomfort with "we don't know why this happened" as an answer
- Preferring a compelling story over a calibrated probability distribution
- Hindsight narratives that make outcomes seem inevitable
- Post-hoc rationalization that constructs cause after observing effect

## False Positives
- Genuine causal chains that are well-supported by evidence are not narrative fallacy — the fallacy involves imposing narrative on noise, not all causal reasoning
- Communicating findings in narrative form for clarity and accessibility is appropriate, as long as the underlying analysis is rigorous
- Expert pattern recognition in domains with genuine causal structure is not narrative construction — expertise can turn genuine causal patterns into reliable stories

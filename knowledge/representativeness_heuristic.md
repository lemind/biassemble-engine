# Representativeness Heuristic

## Definition
The tendency to judge the probability of an event by how closely it resembles a typical case or matches a stereotype, rather than by applying base rate probabilities. People assess likelihood by similarity to a prototype rather than by statistical reasoning. This produces the base rate neglect error: when a description matches a stereotype strongly, people ignore information about how common the category actually is. The classic demonstration is the conjunction fallacy — people rate a specific scenario as more probable than a general one when the specific scenario matches a stereotype better.

## Examples
In medicine, a physician assessing a young, fit patient with chest pain may underestimate the probability of a cardiac event because the patient does not fit the typical heart attack profile, despite cardiac events being statistically possible regardless of demographic presentation.

In finance, investors assess the quality of an investment by how much it resembles their mental model of a successful investment — a compelling story, a charismatic founder, a hot sector — rather than by base rate returns in the asset class.

In law, jurors who hear that a defendant "looks like a criminal" or matches a physical description associated with a crime type assign higher guilt probabilities independently of the evidence.

In hiring, interviewers assess candidates by how closely they match their mental model of a successful employee in the role, ignoring base rates for interview performance predicting actual job performance.

In statistics, the gambler's fallacy is a manifestation of representativeness: after a sequence of heads in coin flips, people expect tails because a random sequence "should look random," ignoring that each flip is independent.

## Indicators
- Judges how likely something is based on how closely it resembles a typical example, without consulting actual probabilities
- Rates a specific detailed scenario as more likely than the general category that contains it
- Ignores base rates when a vivid description is available because the description feels more informative
- Uses the stereotype of what something looks like to estimate whether it is that thing, overriding available statistics
- Sees a meaningful pattern in data because the data resembles a familiar template, even when the pattern is random

## False Positives
- Using reference class statistics to estimate probability is the correct use of base rates — the heuristic becomes a bias only when base rates are neglected in favor of representativeness
- Recognizing genuine patterns from expertise is different from applying stereotypes to probability judgment
- When a case truly is representative of a well-defined category with known base rates, similarity-based reasoning is accurate

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Gambler's Fallacy, Confirmation Bias, Stereotyping Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] This company has everything I look for — a charismatic founder, a hot sector, early product-market fit, and glowing press. It really looks like a winner to me. I didn't spend much time thinking about how often companies with this profile actually produce strong returns for investors. The story matches the template so well that I said yes without asking what the template's historical performance actually is.

[Medical] The patient is young and fit. He runs marathons. When he described chest tightness during exercise, I told him it was probably musculoskeletal. He doesn't look like someone who has cardiac issues. But chest tightness during exertion has a differential that doesn't depend on how fit you look. I went with my picture of what cardiac patients look like instead of running the appropriate workup.

[Legal] The witness identified my client confidently and specifically — height, clothing, a distinctive feature. She was certain. Eyewitness identification is compelling because it looks like direct evidence. I know the research. I know the error rates. But in the courtroom, confident specific identification feels like something I can't argue against the way I can argue against circumstantial evidence. The certainty of the witness looks like accuracy.

[Management] I gave the project to the candidate who gave the best presentation and seemed most like my image of a strong leader. She spoke assertively, projected confidence, gave concrete examples. The other candidate's examples were harder to evaluate but statistically better documented. I went with the one who looked the part. I should have looked harder at what the work would actually require and whether she'd done it before.

[Educational] My student's application essay was beautifully written — articulate, specific about her intellectual journey, references to exactly the kind of scholarship we value. She looked exactly like a strong graduate applicant on paper. I gave her a strong recommendation. She struggled significantly with the methodology and quantitative demands of the program. The essay prototype and the research aptitude prototype don't always overlap.

[Technical] The architecture this candidate proposed was elegant and showed real understanding of the domain. She talked about trade-offs confidently and in the right vocabulary. She looked exactly like a senior engineer in how she presented. My team lead pointed out she hadn't answered our question about failure modes. I thought the answer was implied by the quality of her overall presentation. Strong presentation style isn't the same as having tested the architecture.

[Consumer] I've been watching videos from a cooking channel where the chef looks exactly like what I imagine an authoritative culinary figure looks like — professional kitchen, clean technique, confident voice. I've adopted several of his techniques. Later a professional chef told me one of his common suggestions was contrary to how most professionals approach that specific preparation. Looking like expertise isn't the same thing as expertise.

[Political] The candidate sounded like a statesman. Measured, broad vocabulary, referenced history, spoke about trade-offs. He looked exactly like the template of "serious governance." I supported him without carefully examining his policy positions. A profile that looks like responsible leadership and actually being a responsible leader are different things that pattern recognition treats as the same.

[Social] My new neighbor drives a luxury car, dresses well, and speaks about his work confidently. I assumed he was financially successful. I've been making decisions about how to engage him socially based on that assumption. I later found out he's been struggling financially and the car was from before a difficult period. The presentation and the reality were not the same.

[Family] My uncle has been investing in the stock market for thirty years and speaks about it with great authority. He sounds exactly like someone who knows what he's doing. I've been following his investment suggestions. I recently compared his track record to the index. He's significantly underperformed over the past decade. Sounding knowledgeable and being a good investor are different things.

[Financial] I rejected the business plan because the founders seemed like nice people but not tough enough. They were thoughtful, collaborative, a bit quiet. They didn't match my picture of what startup founders look like. The product was strong and the market analysis was solid. Three years later that company is doing exceptionally well with the same team I passed on. My mental template for "successful founder" filtered out people who didn't fit the archetype.

[Medical] She described her symptoms and they fit a classic presentation of a condition I know well. I diagnosed her confidently. The treatment I prescribed didn't help. Further workup showed a different condition that had an atypical presentation. I'd matched her symptoms to the prototype of the condition it resembled rather than working through the differential. The match to the prototype felt like a diagnosis.

[Legal] The contract had all the marks of a professionally drafted agreement — the terminology, the clause structure, the formatting. I reviewed it quickly because it looked professionally done. My junior associate found two provisions that were fundamentally unenforceable and had probably been drafted by someone who understood the form but not the substance. The document looked like a carefully drafted contract. It wasn't one.

[Technical] The code looked exactly like clean, professional work — consistent style, good naming, thoughtful comments. I approved the PR without examining the logic carefully because the presentation was so good. A bug from that PR made it to production. The code looked like carefully thought-through work. The underlying logic had a flaw that the clean presentation didn't reveal and that I hadn't checked because the presentation had done the work of looking like quality.

[Educational] I gave this student's presentation the highest mark in the class because it was polished and confident. Later grading her written work I found her understanding of the core concepts was much shallower than the presentation suggested. Presentation skill is its own skill. It's not the same as conceptual mastery. The performance looked like understanding.

[Management] The consulting proposal was impressive — beautiful formatting, credentialed team, confident claims about similar engagements. It matched my template of what a high-quality proposal looks like. We hired them. The engagement produced deliverables that looked like the outputs of quality consulting work but had shallow analytical foundations that fell apart when we tried to use them to make decisions. The form matched the prototype. The substance didn't.

[Political] The activist group's materials were visually sophisticated and well-produced. Their messaging was polished and used the right frameworks. They looked like a serious, professionally run organization. I donated and got involved. Later I found out the organization had significant governance problems and most of the money raised wasn't going to the programs advertised. Professional presentation is not the same as organizational integrity.

[Social] My friend said the person she was setting me up with was "exactly my type" — he was into books, had a thoughtful manner, and worked in a field I found interesting. On paper and in the first ten minutes he matched the archetype of someone I'd get along with. The date was flat. Matching the template of what I imagine I'd like and actually being someone I connect with are different things that the first meeting made look like the same thing.

[Financial] I backed a founder because she fit the profile: Ivy League, previous startup experience, clear mission. The profile matched my mental model of who succeeds. The startup struggled significantly. Many people with exactly that profile have successful companies. Many also don't. The profile isn't a guarantee; it's just the most familiar archetype. I'd used the match to the archetype as a substitute for analyzing the specific business.

[Medical] The patient's blood pressure had been elevated at his last three check-ups. His lifestyle matches the typical profile of someone with hypertension — sedentary work, high stress, irregular sleep. I prescribed antihypertensives. A colleague suggested we rule out a secondary cause given some other findings in the workup. I said the presentation was classic primary hypertension. Classic presentation isn't diagnostic certainty. We should have done the workup.

[Technical] The startup described their technology as using "proprietary machine learning" to solve a problem. They had all the right language — model deployment, inference latency, training pipeline. They sounded exactly like a technical team. In due diligence, our engineer found that the "machine learning" was a decision tree with no learning component. The vocabulary had matched the archetype. The technology hadn't.

[Educational] I described the article to my seminar as a classic example of the methodological approach we were studying. It looked exactly like the prototype — right period, right methods, right theoretical framework. A student asked a pointed question and I realized I hadn't read the article carefully in several years. When I reread it, the methodology had differences from the prototype that made my description less accurate than I'd presented. I'd been going on the template match, not the text.

[Legal] The defendant's demeanor was flat and cooperative throughout the proceedings. He answered questions precisely, showed no visible distress, made eye contact. He looked exactly like what I imagine an innocent person trying to be credible looks like. I found him more credible than the facts probably warranted. The demeanor matched a template. Whether the template maps onto actual truth-telling is a separate question.

[Consumer] I chose this vintage because the bottle looked beautiful — distinctive design, high-quality label, sophisticated aging period stated on the back. It matched everything I associate with a bottle worth paying attention to. It was mediocre. The bottle was designed to look like exactly what I think premium wine looks like. Looking premium and being premium are things packaging is specifically designed to confuse.

[Family] My son's new girlfriend is very articulate and polished, references her studies intelligently, and generally matches my mental picture of someone who would be good for him. I've been enthusiastic about her. My wife is more reserved. She pointed out we've only spent a few hours with her and the profile match isn't the same as actually knowing what she's like. The template match made me feel like I already knew who she was.

[Management] The company we were considering acquiring had strong brand recognition, impressive revenue growth, and a compelling story. They looked exactly like an acquisition target worth a premium valuation. Our due diligence found that the revenue growth was driven by promotional activity that wasn't sustainable and the customer retention underlying the brand was weaker than the surface metrics suggested. They looked like the acquisition profile I was looking for. The fundamentals were different from the profile.

[Political] The foreign leader presented himself as a moderate reformer — pressed suits, fluent in economic concepts, referencing international norms. He fit my mental image of a pragmatic, reformist leader. Several analysts noted that his actual governance record differed significantly from his presentation. The presentation matched the template of a reformer. The record suggested a different conclusion.

[Technical] The open-source library had impressive GitHub stars, detailed documentation, and a large contributor base. It looked exactly like a mature, reliable library. We built our product on it. It had a critical bug that only appeared at scale and that the maintainers had been aware of for eight months but hadn't resolved. Looking like a well-maintained library and being one at the level we needed aren't the same thing.

[Social] My new acquaintance is deeply knowledgeable about environmental issues — she can cite statistics, knows the organizations, references the research. She looks exactly like what I imagine a serious, informed advocate looks like. I've been treating her positions as authoritative. A scientist friend who met her said her understanding of the science was strong on broad strokes but thinner than it appeared on specifics. Appearing deeply informed and being deeply informed can diverge.

[Financial] I looked at the startup's pitch deck and it matched every element of what a strong Series A pitch looks like. The narrative, the market slide, the traction metrics, the team slide — every section matched the archetype. I gave them a term sheet. Their actual business model had a customer acquisition assumption embedded in the metrics that was highly optimistic but wasn't visible from the deck's presentation. The deck had been assembled to match the prototype of a successful pitch.

[Educational] The student's resume had all the elements I associate with a high-performing candidate: relevant internships, strong GPA, extracurricular leadership. She matched the profile of candidates who had succeeded in the program before. She struggled significantly in the collaborative parts of the program that the resume didn't measure. Profile match predicted entry. It didn't predict performance on the dimensions the program actually required.

[Legal] The neighborhood had characteristics I associated with a certain type of property dispute — type of development, proximity to commercial zoning, similar prior cases. I went in expecting the claim to look a certain way. The specific situation was different enough that my pattern expectation led me to overlook an important feature of the case. Recognizing the neighborhood pattern had primed me for a conclusion before I'd examined the specific facts.

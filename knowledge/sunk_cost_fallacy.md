# Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Definition
The tendency to continue investing in a course of action because of previously invested resources — money, time, or effort — rather than evaluating whether continued investment is rational given current and future prospects. Sunk costs are by definition unrecoverable; rational decision-making requires ignoring them and evaluating only future costs and benefits. The fallacy occurs when past investment becomes the primary driver of future decisions, leading people to throw good money after bad, stay in failing relationships, or complete projects that should be abandoned.

## Examples
In finance, an investor who bought a stock at $100 that has fallen to $40 refuses to sell because "I've already lost so much." They continue holding or adding to the position to justify the original investment rather than evaluating whether the stock is a good investment at $40.

In technology, a company that has spent $5 million building a product continues funding it for two more years despite clear evidence of zero market demand, unwilling to write off the initial investment.

In medicine, a patient continues an ineffective treatment regimen because "I've already gone through three months of this, I can't stop now," ignoring that the treatment's past application provides no future benefit.

In academia, a PhD student continues toward a degree in a field with no job prospects because they have already spent four years in the program, even after their interests have changed.

## Indicators
- Continues a course of action primarily because of what has already been invested, not because of what the future holds
- Resists ending a failing project because of how much time, money, or effort has already gone into it
- Argues that quitting now would mean all the past effort was wasted, treating sunk costs as a reason to keep going
- Sits through a bad movie, meeting, or experience rather than leaving early, because walking out would mean acknowledging the time already spent was wasted
- Finds it emotionally impossible to accept that past losses are gone regardless of what happens next

## False Positives
- Completing a project because the marginal cost to finish is low and the output has residual value is rational, not a fallacy
- Staying in a situation because exit costs are high and genuine future value exists is cost-benefit analysis, not sunk cost reasoning
- Accumulated expertise or relationships from past investment can be legitimate future assets — not all value from the past is a sunk cost

## Related Biases

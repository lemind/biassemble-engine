# Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Definition
The tendency to continue investing in a course of action because of previously invested resources — money, time, or effort — rather than evaluating whether continued investment is rational given current and future prospects. Sunk costs are by definition unrecoverable; rational decision-making requires ignoring them and evaluating only future costs and benefits. The fallacy occurs when past investment becomes the primary driver of future decisions, leading people to throw good money after bad, stay in failing relationships, or complete projects that should be abandoned.

## Examples
In finance, an investor who bought a stock at $100 that has fallen to $40 refuses to sell because "I've already lost so much." They continue holding or adding to the position to justify the original investment rather than evaluating whether the stock is a good investment at $40.

In technology, a company that has spent $5 million building a product continues funding it for two more years despite clear evidence of zero market demand, unwilling to write off the initial investment.

In medicine, a patient continues an ineffective treatment regimen because "I've already gone through three months of this, I can't stop now," ignoring that the treatment's past application provides no future benefit.

In academia, a PhD student continues toward a degree in a field with no job prospects because they have already spent four years in the program, even after their interests have changed.

## Indicators
- Continues a course of action primarily because of what has already been invested, not because of what the future holds
- Resists ending a failing project because of how much time, money, or effort has already gone into it
- Argues that quitting now would mean all the past effort was wasted, treating sunk costs as a reason to keep going
- Sits through a bad movie, meeting, or experience rather than leaving early, because walking out would mean acknowledging the time already spent was wasted
- Finds it emotionally impossible to accept that past losses are gone regardless of what happens next

## False Positives
- Completing a project because the marginal cost to finish is low and the output has residual value is rational, not a fallacy
- Staying in a situation because exit costs are high and genuine future value exists is cost-benefit analysis, not sunk cost reasoning
- Accumulated expertise or relationships from past investment can be legitimate future assets — not all value from the past is a sunk cost

## Related Biases
Status Quo Bias, Overconfidence Bias, Escalation of Commitment, Loss Aversion

## Story Patterns

[Consumer] I paid $14 to see a movie and forty-five minutes in I realized I wasn't enjoying it. I stayed for the full two hours. I didn't want to waste the ticket money. My friend left at the same point and told me later she felt liberated. I sat through ninety minutes I didn't want to sit through to protect money I'd already spent and couldn't get back.

[Financial] I'm holding a stock that I bought at $95. It's at $44. I keep telling myself I'll sell when it gets back to $95. My broker asked why I'm holding it. I said I have too much of a loss to sell. She asked me whether, if I had cash today and no position, I would buy this stock at $44. I thought about it. No, I wouldn't. But I'm still holding.

[Educational] I'm in the third year of a law degree. I realized sometime in year two that I don't want to be a lawyer. The tuition is already spent. I keep going because I can't walk away from all that money. My advisor suggested I think about what I'm walking into rather than what I've already paid for. I know she's right. I'm finishing the degree because of the years and money behind me, not because of what's in front of me.

[Technical] We've been building a custom CRM for two years and $800,000. It's clear now that an off-the-shelf solution would have served us better and would cost a fraction of continued development. My board asked me to compare continuing development to switching. I keep framing it as "we can't throw away what we've built." The rational question is whether the future investment will create value. I can't separate that question from the investment we've already made.

[Social] I've been in this friendship for fifteen years. The friendship has been difficult for the last three — one-sided, occasionally hurtful, and draining. My therapist has asked me what's keeping me in it. My honest answer involves the fifteen years more than the current three. The years I've invested feel like reasons to continue rather than just context for evaluating whether to continue.

[Legal] We've been pursuing this litigation for three years and spent $1.2 million in legal fees. Our outside counsel believes the likely recovery has declined significantly. She recommends settling for whatever is on the table. I keep saying we can't walk away after everything we've put in. The investment we've made doesn't change the expected value of continuing. I know this. I can't make the decision like someone who's considering the case fresh.

[Management] We've been running a leadership development program for four years and it's been expensive and time-consuming. Evaluation data suggests it's having minimal impact on retention or promotion rates. My head of HR wants to redesign or discontinue it. I keep defending the program because of what we've invested in developing it. I've started asking myself: if I were starting today with no prior investment, would I create this program in this form? The answer is not clearly yes.

[Family] I've been in this relationship for eight years. We get along but we're not a good match in ways that have become clearer over time. My friends occasionally gently raise whether I'm happy. My main answer, when I'm honest with myself, usually comes back to eight years. The eight years don't add value to what the relationship is today. I know that. I'm still in it because of them.

[Educational] I wrote 70 pages of my master's thesis on a research question that I've now decided is framed incorrectly. My advisor agrees the framing needs to change. Changing the framing means discarding most of the 70 pages and starting fresh in a different direction. I keep trying to preserve the existing pages by adjusting rather than reframing. I've spent a summer on those pages. The pages want to survive even when they shouldn't.

[Consumer] I've been on a subscription meal kit service for a year. I don't really use it — maybe half the boxes go to waste. It costs me $100 per month. But I signed up when there was a deal and I told myself it would change how I cook. I haven't canceled because I keep thinking I'm on the verge of actually using it consistently. The money I've spent has become the reason I'm still subscribed to something I'm not using.

[Technical] We're using an analytics platform that we've spent a year integrating deeply into our workflow. The platform has been discontinued by the vendor and support ends in six months. We should be migrating now. I keep delaying the migration because the integration work was so substantial. We've put in a year. Moving away feels like abandoning what we built. The year we've invested doesn't change what the platform will be in six months.

[Financial] I renovated a rental property for $60,000. The market in that area has softened and I could sell it now for about what I owe plus the renovation cost, meaning I'd break even on the renovation but have my capital free. Or I can hold it and see if the market recovers. My accountant says the expected return from holding doesn't justify the carrying costs. I can't sell at break-even after all that work. The work behind me is making the calculation harder.

[Legal] I've been fighting a small-claims dispute over $800. I've now spent more than $800 on legal research, filing fees, and time. My attorney friend said to drop it — I've already lost economically regardless of outcome. I said I've come this far. The dispute is now about the time I've already spent as much as about the original $800. The sunk costs are pulling me forward into more sunk costs.

[Management] We brought a consultant in for $90,000. Three months in, the recommendations are thin and the process has been frustrating. My team wants to end the engagement. I've been reluctant because of the $90,000 we've spent. The question isn't whether the $90,000 was worth it — that money is spent. The question is whether more months and more money will produce value we'd pay for if we were starting fresh. I haven't asked it that way.

[Social] I've been going to a book club I don't enjoy for two years. The reading selections are never what I'd choose and the discussion frustrates me. I keep going because I organized the club and recruited the initial members. I feel responsible to what I started. The two years of my time feel like they'd be wasted if I stepped away. I know the two years are spent regardless. I'm still going next month.

[Technical] A feature I spent three months developing never got traction — users don't engage with it and it adds maintenance burden. My team lead has suggested deprecating it. I keep saying we should wait a bit longer and try different approaches to drive adoption. The three months I spent on it makes me reluctant to let it go. If I were evaluating a competitor's feature with this engagement profile, I would say deprecate it immediately. My investment has changed my assessment.

[Family] My parents built a summer house when my siblings and I were young. We're all adults now with different lives. Maintaining the house is expensive and no one uses it as much as we used to. My parents have asked us whether we should sell. Every one of us has been reluctant because of what the house represents and what went into building it. The cost of what was put into the house is influencing a decision that should be about what the house is worth to us going forward.

[Political] Our organization has been running a voter outreach program in a specific district for five years. The returns on investment have declined and the district's demographics have shifted. We have better opportunities in other districts. I've been keeping the program because of what we've invested in relationships and infrastructure there. The strategic question is where our next dollar should go, not how to justify where the last five years went.

[Educational] I've been teaching a topic that I began covering because there was demand for it ten years ago. The field has moved and the topic is less central now. My students ask about adjacent topics that would be more relevant to their careers. I keep the old material because I've developed strong notes and exercises for it. Years of teaching materials shouldn't be the deciding factor in what I teach.

[Financial] I started a small business that has been limping along for four years. Revenue covers expenses but doesn't pay me a salary. I've invested $40,000 and four years of time. My accountant says the business doesn't have a path to being profitable enough to be worth keeping. My answer is always that I've put too much into it to stop. The right question is what the next year would produce if I invested the same time and money elsewhere.

[Consumer] I've been in a gym membership at a facility that's inconvenient and mediocre because I prepaid for a year when I first moved here. The year ended eight months ago. I'm still going because I feel like I should get my money's worth. The prepaid year ended eight months ago. The current membership is month-to-month. There's no money to get worth from anymore. The original prepayment is driving a current decision as if it's still in play.

[Technical] We chose an infrastructure architecture three years ago that made sense then. The codebase has evolved and the infrastructure choice is now a source of friction. Migrating would require significant work. My team keeps saying "we've lived with this long enough to know how to work around it." Working around an architecture is not the same as it being the right architecture. Three years of working around it is evidence that it needs to change, not a reason to preserve it.

[Social] My spouse and I have been planning a trip for months. We've booked flights and a hotel and spent significant time on the itinerary. As the trip approaches, we both realize we're exhausted and don't want to go. We're going anyway because of everything we've spent and planned. We could cancel most of it — some fees apply but most is refundable. We haven't looked into cancellation because the planning we've done feels like something we'd be abandoning.

[Legal] I've been corresponding with a difficult neighbor about a property line issue for eighteen months. The legal position is murky and a lawsuit would cost more than any realistic resolution is worth. I've spent so much time on this correspondence that I feel I can't just drop it. An attorney friend said: tell me the best outcome and what it's worth. Then tell me what it would cost to get there. Don't tell me what you've already spent. When I followed her framing, the answer was obvious.

[Management] The partnership I'm in hasn't been working well for two years. My partner and I have fundamentally different views about direction and we've been through two rounds of trying to realign. We've built this together for six years. I can't imagine dismantling six years of work. My lawyer says the question isn't what we've built together; it's whether continuing the partnership will create value that would be better than the alternatives. The six years are making it impossible to evaluate the present clearly.

[Technical] I wrote a significant portion of our codebase and I feel personally attached to the architecture decisions I made. When my team proposes refactoring a core module, I look for reasons to preserve the existing approach. I find the code and the decisions defensible. A neutral review would probably find it could be substantially improved. The author inside me and the engineer inside me are in conflict and the author keeps winning.

[Financial] I paid $2,000 for a professional certification program. I completed two of the four modules and then life got busy. The modules I haven't completed are available for six more months. I keep forcing myself to work through them even though I no longer need this certification for my career path. The $2,000 is driving my weekend time. If the course were free and I had the same time constraints, I wouldn't be spending my Saturdays on this.

[Educational] My graduate student has been working on a particular research direction for two years that has hit a dead end. The original framing was wrong and the data hasn't produced anything publishable. I've been encouraging her to persist because of the two years she's invested. A colleague from another program looked at the project and said the cleanest move was to pivot. Two years invested doesn't mean two more years is justified. I'm using her past investment as the reason to continue rather than evaluating the future prospects clearly.

[Consumer] I bought an exercise bike that I use infrequently. It takes up significant space in my apartment. I've been offered a reasonable sum for it by a neighbor. I haven't sold it because I spent a lot on it and feel like selling it means admitting the purchase was a mistake. The purchase was already made. The bike is already taking up space. Selling it would free the space and recover some of the cost. The remaining attachment is to a feeling about the original purchase, not to the bike's future value to me.

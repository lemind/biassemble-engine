# Projection Bias

## Definition
The tendency to overestimate how much future preferences, feelings, and states will resemble current ones. People project their current emotional state, desires, and attitudes onto their future selves, failing to account for how circumstances, moods, and preferences change over time. Projection bias produces decisions that optimize for current states rather than future states: people overbuy at the grocery store when hungry, underestimate how they will adapt to changed circumstances, and make long-term plans based on current transient preferences.

## Examples
In consumer behavior, people who shop for groceries when hungry purchase more high-calorie food than they intended and more than they will want when they are satiated. The current state of hunger is projected onto anticipated future consumption.

In finance, investors who have recently experienced a crash project their current risk aversion into the future and make allocation decisions that systematically underexpose them to risk during the recovery phase, when their long-term risk tolerance would support higher equity exposure.

In real estate, buyers who tour houses in summer overestimate the enjoyment they will derive from features like pools and patios — current mood in a season of warmth is projected onto future experience across all seasons.

In medicine, patients asked to make advance decisions about medical procedures (do-not-resuscitate orders, organ donation) in good health significantly change their stated preferences when given the same choices while ill, because health state projection fails to anticipate how values shift when circumstances change.

In career planning, professionals make long-term career decisions based on their current interests and values, underestimating how substantially interests, priorities, and personal circumstances change over a ten-year horizon.

## Indicators
- Makes long-term plans based on current preferences without considering that those preferences will change
- Buys more food than needed when shopping hungry because it feels like the current level of hunger will persist
- Expects a major life change to feel as significant one year from now as it does today
- Cannot vividly imagine wanting something different in the future than what feels important right now
- Makes a commitment based on the current emotional state that turns out to be wrong when that state changes

## False Positives
- Some preferences genuinely are stable across time and circumstances — projecting stable preferences into the future is accurate forecasting, not bias
- When planning for the near term where circumstance changes are minimal, current-state projection is approximately correct
- Accurate self-knowledge about which of one's preferences are stable versus state-dependent mitigates projection bias and is worth cultivating

## Related Biases
Optimism Bias, Planning Fallacy, Recency Bias, Status Quo Bias

## Story Patterns

[Consumer] I went grocery shopping after a long run when I was very hungry. I bought enough food for about three times what I'd actually eat over the next week. Everything in the store looked appealing in a way it doesn't normally. I got home and half of it has been sitting untouched for four days. I'd been planning for how hungry I felt at the store, not for how hungry I'd be at every meal for the next week.

[Financial] I've had a rough quarter at work and I'm feeling anxious about money. I moved a larger portion of my portfolio to cash last month. My financial advisor says my actual financial situation hasn't changed and neither has my long-term risk tolerance. She's probably right. In the moment, the anxiety feels like information about risk. It might just be information about my mood.

[Legal] I signed up for a more aggressive litigation strategy when I was furious at the other party right after their opening filing. A month later I'm not as furious, the litigation is going to be expensive and time-consuming, and I'm questioning whether I'd have made the same decision if I'd waited a week before responding to their filing. The anger felt like the permanent state of things. It turned out to be the first few weeks.

[Management] I promised my team a significant reward if we hit the Q3 target — an offsite trip I knew they'd love. In Q2 when I made the promise, we were all energized and the target felt motivating. By Q3 we hit the target but I'm now under budget pressure and thinking the trip looks expensive. The team is expecting it. I made a commitment based on how the situation felt in Q2 and now Q3 has different pressures. I should have thought more carefully about whether I'd still value the same reward six months later.

[Social] I signed up to run a half marathon with a friend when I was in a great fitness phase and feeling strong. The event is in four months. My training has lapsed significantly. The enthusiasm I felt when I signed up was real but it was the enthusiasm of a person who was already running four times a week. I should have asked whether I'd be equally committed to training three months later when the initial excitement wore off.

[Technical] I committed to a side project when I had a week off and was feeling very productive. I said I'd build a complete prototype in my spare evenings over the next two months. I had five or six spare evenings and then life got busy. The project is twelve percent done. My spare-time capacity during a vacation week was not what spare-time capacity looks like in a normal working month.

[Political] When I was unemployed, I made very confident statements about what I would and wouldn't accept in a job. I said I'd never take a job without creative independence and that salary wasn't the main driver. Six months later with savings declining I accepted a job with limited creative independence and salary was basically the whole driver. My unemployment-self was projecting its priorities onto a future self whose situation had changed considerably.

[Educational] I enrolled in a professional certification program when I was on a learning kick and deeply interested in the subject. The program runs eighteen months. By month seven I was much less engaged with the subject — my professional interests had moved in a different direction. I'd signed up for eighteen months based on how interested I was at month zero. That interest level wasn't stable.

[Family] I planned a large family vacation for next August when everyone was enthusiastic about it in December. It's now May and scheduling it has become complicated, one family member is going through a difficult period, and the enthusiasm has dissipated. In December the whole thing felt obviously worth the effort. From May it's starting to feel like a logistical burden. I'd made a plan for a group of people in December moods that we're not all in anymore.

[Consumer] I subscribed to four different streaming services in one week when I was on a binge-watching phase. Eight months later I use two of them regularly and two I haven't opened in months. I couldn't have told you this would happen at the time of subscribing. All four seemed obviously useful given how much I was watching that week. That viewing pace wasn't a permanent feature of my life.

[Financial] I took a much larger mortgage than I needed because houses with large square footage appealed to me strongly when we were house shopping with a baby. Now our kids are older and out most of the day, my spouse and I rattle around in an oversized house. The things that felt important to have — playroom, large yard, mudroom — were things I valued as the parent of a very young child. I didn't think about whether I'd want the same features in ten years with teenagers.

[Medical] I agreed to a twelve-week intensive physical therapy schedule when I was very motivated right after the injury. The therapy requires six sessions per week. By week five the intensity of motivation had moderated, the sessions are genuinely disruptive to my work schedule, and I've been canceling more sessions than I should. I'd committed to a schedule designed for someone who felt the urgency I felt in week one. That urgency has changed.

[Management] I told my team in the planning session that I wanted to do a full retrospective every sprint. It sounded right when we were invested in improving the process. Six months in, retros are rushed and people find them less useful. The commitment I made in a high-energy planning session didn't match how the team would feel about the time investment when sprints got busier. I should have piloted it rather than committing to it indefinitely.

[Technical] I designed the system to have very granular logging because I was deep in a debugging problem when I built it and really felt the need for detailed visibility. After that problem was resolved, the logging has been generating noise in production that the team finds more annoying than useful. The decision I made from inside a hard debugging problem was calibrated for that specific problem, not for routine operations.

[Political] I made commitments in my campaign platform when I was in an energized campaign mindset surrounded by enthusiastic supporters. In office, the constraints are different — budget realities, legislative coalitions, implementation complexities. Some promises I made feel obviously right from inside a campaign. They're more complicated from inside a budget conversation. I hadn't fully modeled what it would feel like to govern rather than campaign.

[Social] I agreed to be on three volunteer committees when I was in a yes-saying phase and feeling very community-oriented. Four months in I'm overwhelmed and the committees feel burdensome. I'd said yes to everything because that's what my state at the time wanted to do. I hadn't thought about whether my available time and energy would be the same four months later.

[Consumer] I bought a very elaborate coffee brewing setup when I was extremely into specialty coffee. It's a beautiful setup. I use it about twice a month. The enthusiasm I felt during the peak of my coffee interest was treated as a permanent feature of my preferences. It was a phase. The elaborate setup required investment and routine that my post-phase self wasn't willing to sustain.

[Family] I signed my kids up for three activities each when they expressed strong interest in all three in September. By February each of them is enthusiastic about one and struggling through the other two. The enthusiasm at signup was genuine but a nine-year-old's September enthusiasm for four months of weekly commitment is not a stable reading of what they'll want in January.

[Educational] I took on a role as department chair when I was feeling deeply engaged in departmental politics and governance issues. Two years in I'm burned out on the administrative work. The governance issues that felt urgent and important at the time of accepting the role have been addressed or proven intractable, and the ongoing administrative work is less interesting than the role had looked from outside. My interest in the chair role was partly state-dependent.

[Technical] I wrote a very detailed design document for a feature when I was deeply engaged in planning it and had all the context in my working memory. The document is thorough but assumes the reader is in the same context I was in when I wrote it. Someone picking it up fresh finds it confusing in places because I wrote it from inside the thinking, not from the perspective of someone who needed to enter the thinking. What was clear from inside my head didn't translate to clear on the page.

[Financial] I agreed to mentor three junior colleagues when I had recently received a grant that gave me more time than usual. I'm no longer on the grant. My available time has returned to normal. I'm now spread across the mentoring commitments in a way that isn't serving anyone as well as it should. I'd said yes based on my grant-phase schedule. That schedule ended. The commitments didn't.

[Management] I drafted an aggressive product roadmap in Q1 when we'd just had a great quarter and morale was very high. The roadmap assumed the team's current energy level and output rate. By Q3 the team was dealing with typical mid-year fatigue, two key people had left, and the roadmap was clearly not achievable. I'd built the plan for the Q1 team's state. Plans need to work across different states.

[Social] I booked a very intense travel itinerary for a trip I planned when I was very excited and energized. The trip is in three months. Looking at the schedule now, I'm already tired thinking about it. I'll be jet-lagged at the start, we've scheduled something every day, and the pacing assumes enthusiasm levels that I'll probably have on day one but not on day eight. I planned for how I felt booking the trip, not for how I'll feel mid-trip.

[Legal] I gave a very definitive opinion letter when a client caught me in a high-confidence moment after winning a related matter. The opinion was more definitive than the legal uncertainty warranted. The client relied on it substantially. When circumstances shifted the risk picture, my earlier certainty created a gap. I'd written from the confidence of that specific day without calibrating for how I'd assess the same question in different conditions.

[Technical] I set my system's alert thresholds when I was monitoring it intensively right after launch. At that time, conservative thresholds felt right — I wanted to know about everything. Two years later, the alerts are almost entirely noise and the team has stopped responding to them. The thresholds were calibrated for a high-vigilance phase that naturally ended. I set them as if the launch-phase vigilance would be the permanent operating mode.

[Family] My partner and I made a big decision about having a third child when we were both in a very loving, connected phase of our relationship after a family vacation. We were both energized by family life and the idea felt joyful. Looking back, we hadn't fully accounted for what we'd feel like in ordinary phases — when we were tired and stretched and already maxed out. The decision was made from inside a peak, projected forward as if that peak was our baseline.

[Educational] I committed to a guest lecture series at a partner institution when I was enthusiastic about the collaboration and the topic was very fresh for me. Three years in, the topic has moved on in my research and the commute to deliver the lectures feels less worth it. I'd committed to a series without modeling what it would feel like to honor the commitment once the initial enthusiasm had run its natural course.

[Consumer] I adopted a new workout routine when I had two weeks off over the holidays and was feeling very healthy and motivated. The routine required forty-five minutes every morning before work. Back at work, forty-five minutes before work is a completely different proposition. I had projected my holiday-self's schedule and energy onto my work-self. They're different people living in different constraints.

[Management] I agreed to co-author a book when a colleague proposed it at a conference where I was feeling inspired and intellectually energized. Writing a book requires sustained discipline over a year or two of evenings and weekends. My conference self had that energy. My twelve-months-later self has a different relationship to evenings and weekends. I'm behind on my chapters. I agreed from inside a high point.

[Financial] I made an aggressive savings target for the year when I was in a frugal phase and optimistic about my ability to cut spending. By July I'm behind on the target and I've been making spending decisions that are inconsistent with January's plan. January-me had underestimated how much I'd value certain things once February, March, and April came around with their own pressures and contexts. January-me made the plan. The rest of the months live it.

[Technical] I set an ambitious code quality target for the codebase when I was fresh off a painful debugging experience that made code quality feel like the most important thing. Six months later, feature pressure has compressed the time we spend on code quality review. The target had been set from inside the debugging frustration. The normal development state has different priorities. I'd calibrated the target for my worst day and tried to hold it during normal days.

[Political] I made a public commitment to a reform position when I was in a deeply idealistic period, inspired by a particular speech and a sense of political possibility. Two years later the reform effort has stalled, the political calculus has shifted, and the commitment is more difficult to maintain than it felt at the time I made it. I'd made a long-term public commitment from inside a peak of idealism. I hadn't modeled how I'd feel about the commitment during ordinary political fatigue.

[Social] I invited my whole extended family to an annual holiday gathering at our house when we had just done a large renovation and were very proud of the space. Four years of hosting later, the gathering has become a source of significant stress. I had projected how I felt about hosting from inside a new-house excitement period. That excitement was real. It was not permanent.

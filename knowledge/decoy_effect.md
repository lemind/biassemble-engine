# Decoy Effect

## Definition
The phenomenon where a consumer's preference between two options changes upon the introduction of a third option (the decoy) that is asymmetrically dominated — clearly inferior to one of the original options but not the other. The decoy shifts preference toward the option it is dominated by, without adding genuine value to the choice set. It violates rationality axioms (specifically the independence of irrelevant alternatives) because the addition of a new, inferior choice should not change preferences between the original options. The decoy effect is deliberately exploited in pricing strategy, product line design, and menu engineering.

## Examples
In retail, a magazine subscription offers: online-only for $59, print-only for $125, and combined print+online for $125. The print-only option is the decoy — it makes the combined option appear to be exceptional value, shifting buyers toward the more expensive combined package.

In consumer electronics, a three-tier product line is designed so the middle tier is positioned as slightly less capable than the premium tier at a price just below it, making the premium tier feel underpriced and increasing premium purchases.

In restaurants, menu designers place a very expensive item at the top of a menu not to sell it but to make the second-most-expensive item appear reasonably priced by comparison. The expensive item is the decoy.

In real estate, a property slightly inferior in size and location to a target property at a similar price makes the target property appear to be better value, increasing conversion on the target.

In finance, fund comparisons that include a poorly-performing fund as a third option can shift investor preference between the two genuinely competitive funds toward the one that most dominates the decoy.

## Indicators
- Changes a preference between two options when a third, clearly worse option is added to the set
- Picks an option primarily because it looks better than the obviously inferior alternative next to it
- Gravitates toward a mid-tier choice because the expensive option makes it feel like a bargain
- Finds that an option which felt overpriced or unappealing suddenly looks reasonable once a clearly worse option is placed next to it
- Selects a combined or premium option that didn't seem compelling until a dominated option made it look like exceptional value

## False Positives
- A third option that genuinely provides useful comparison information for making a choice is not a decoy — it adds relevant signal
- Adjusting preferences when a new option reveals information about the true value distribution is rational, not decoy-driven
- Adding an inferior option to a lineup that happens to shift preferences is only the decoy effect if the preference shift is irrational relative to the information content of the new option

## Related Biases
Anchoring Bias, Framing Effect, Status Quo Bias, Representativeness Heuristic

## Story Patterns

[Consumer] I went in to buy a streaming subscription. There was a basic plan at $8 and a premium plan at $16. I was leaning basic. Then I saw they'd added an ad-supported plan at $7 — worse than basic in almost every way but similar price. Suddenly the $8 basic felt like a real value compared to the ad-supported option. I bought the basic. I'm not sure I would have without seeing that third option.

[Financial] The fund comparison showed three options. Option A: 6% projected return, medium risk. Option B: 9% projected return, higher risk. Option C: 9% projected return, very high risk with more volatility. After seeing Option C, Option B started looking like the sensible risk-adjusted choice. I picked B. Looking back, Option C wasn't a real competitor — it just made B look conservative by comparison.

[Management] We were choosing between two consulting firms. One was $180K for the engagement, the other $240K. I was leaning toward the $180K firm. Then the sales rep added a "streamlined" version of their proposal at $220K that did noticeably less. Suddenly the $240K full proposal looked like much better value — look how much more you're getting for just $20K more. We went with the $240K. That streamlined option did something to how we evaluated the full package.

[Medical] My dentist showed me three options for a crown. The basic was $900, the mid-grade ceramic was $1,400, and the premium full porcelain was $1,500. I'd been planning to ask about the basic option but after seeing the premium was only $100 more than mid-grade, I started thinking about whether I should just go for the top option. I ended up with mid-grade feeling like I'd made a rational compromise. I'm not sure I would have considered mid-grade at all without that third price.

[Consumer] I was choosing a gym membership. Month-to-month: $65. Annual prepaid: $580. Then they showed me a "semi-annual" option: $340 for six months. That worked out to $57/month, almost as expensive as month-to-month for half the commitment. Suddenly the annual plan at $48/month felt incredibly efficient. I signed the annual. The semi-annual option I'd never use had repositioned the annual as the smart choice.

[Legal] Three billing structures were on the table: hourly at $450, a monthly retainer at $3,500, and a "lite" retainer at $3,200 covering fewer services. The lite retainer barely saved money and left me with less coverage. The full retainer suddenly felt like the right value. I went with the retainer. I'm pretty sure I would have stayed hourly if I hadn't seen the lite option sitting right next to it.

[Technical] We were evaluating three cloud storage plans for the company. 1TB at $20/month, 5TB at $60/month, and 2TB at $55/month. The 2TB option — expensive per-gigabyte and only modestly more space than 1TB — made the 5TB plan look like a no-brainer value. We went with 5TB even though 1TB had been sufficient for two years. The middle option had done something to my sense of what good value looked like.

[Social] A catering company gave us three wedding menu options: simple buffet at $45 per head, plated dinner at $120 per head, and a "comfort dining" option at $110 per head. The comfort dining was less formal than the plated dinner but almost the same price. After seeing that, the plated dinner seemed almost reasonably priced — we're only talking $10 more for a clearly better experience. We went with plated. I'm not sure we would have without that third option showing us where the price ceiling was.

[Family] We were looking at three summer camps for our daughter: a local day camp at $800, an overnight regional camp at $2,400, and an overnight specialty arts camp at $2,200 that had a significantly narrower program. The arts camp wasn't right for her interests. But seeing its price made the regional camp — $200 more, broader program — look like exceptional value. I recommended the regional camp. I wonder if the arts camp was on the list specifically to make the regional camp look good.

[Educational] A test prep company offered three packages: basic at $299, premium at $699, and a "focused" option at $650 that covered fewer subjects. The focused option was almost as expensive as premium with less coverage. I looked at premium differently after seeing focused — premium was only $49 more for the full curriculum. I chose premium. I had been leaning toward basic.

[Consumer] The phone upgrade options at my carrier: keep my old plan at $45/month, upgrade to a new plan at $75/month, or choose a "value bundle" at $70/month with slightly fewer data. After seeing the value bundle, the $75 plan looked like barely more money for noticeably more. I upgraded. The $5 difference seemed small. The $30 gap from my original plan felt different before the value bundle appeared.

[Management] Three project management software tiers: starter at $12/user/month, professional at $28, and a "growth" plan at $26 with fewer integrations than professional. The growth plan seemed to exist only to make professional look reasonably priced. Once I saw it, I couldn't argue against professional when it was only $2 more. We moved to professional. The growth plan made the decision for us without us noticing.

[Financial] My wealth manager presented three portfolio models: conservative, balanced, and "active balanced." Active balanced had higher fees and similar projected returns to regular balanced with more volatility. I would have picked conservative or maybe regular balanced. But having active balanced in the picture made regular balanced seem like the measured, sensible middle choice. I went with balanced. I think I was positioned to go there.

[Consumer] I was choosing between two laptop models at the store. Budget model at $799, flagship at $1,399. A floor representative pointed out a mid-tier model at $1,199 that was slightly less capable than the flagship in almost every way for only $200 less. Suddenly the flagship looked like a much better deal — the $200 gap felt small against the performance difference. I bought the flagship. I hadn't been planning to spend that much.

[Legal] We were presented with three settlement structures. Option A: $180,000 lump sum. Option B: $240,000 structured over three years. Option C: $235,000 structured over four years — a worse payment schedule and barely more money than Option B. Option C made Option B look almost equivalent to a lump sum, just a little more. I recommended my client take Option B. I'm not sure Option B was the right choice or whether Option C had put it in an artificially favorable light.

[Technical] Three hosting tiers: basic at $49/month, business at $149/month, enterprise at $139/month with more restricted support hours. The enterprise plan was priced just below business but was clearly less capable. Business suddenly felt like the most premium option at almost the same price. We signed up for business. The enterprise tier seemed designed to make business feel like a relative bargain.

[Social] A charity auction had three sponsorship levels: community ($500), champion ($1,500), and advocate ($1,400 with fewer table seats and less recognition). The advocate tier seemed like a trap — almost identical in price to champion but clearly worse. Champion became the obvious answer. I paid $1,500. I might have considered community if I hadn't seen champion made to look so comparatively reasonable by the advocate option sitting next to it.

[Consumer] Wine list at a restaurant. House pour at $12/glass, reserve section starting at $22, and a mid-range option at $20 that the sommelier described as "good value" — but it was only $2 less than the reserve. After hearing that, the $22 reserve bottle looked like an obvious step up for barely more. I ordered the reserve. I would have ordered the house pour without that middle option.

[Family] We were comparing three moving companies. Company A: $2,100. Company B: $3,400. Company C: $3,200 with fewer included hours and no packing material. After seeing Company C, Company B looked nearly equivalent in price but clearly superior in service. We booked Company B. I'm not sure we would have gotten there without seeing how Company C was positioned.

[Educational] Three textbook options from the campus store: used copy at $45, new physical copy at $130, and a digital license at $120 that expires after 180 days. After seeing that the digital option was $10 less than the physical copy but didn't give you ownership, the physical new copy seemed like real value. Several students in my class said they bought new. I bought new. Nobody seemed to question whether $45 used was actually fine.

[Management] Our office furniture vendor showed us three desk configurations: basic at $400, executive at $900, and a "professional" option at $850 that had the same surface area as basic with a slightly better finish. After seeing professional, the executive felt like only $50 more for a meaningfully better desk. We upgraded to executive for the new office. I'd been expecting to buy basic desks.

[Technical] The SaaS vendor gave me three annual plan options: starter at $1,200, professional at $4,800, and standard at $4,500 with slower customer support and fewer user seats. Standard was almost professional's price but noticeably worse. Professional started looking like the smart choice — barely more cost, clearly better. I chose professional. I had planned to ask whether starter would work.

[Political] A campaign showed voters three proposed tax brackets. Option A raised taxes for high earners modestly. Option B raised them significantly. Option C raised them significantly but with a complex mechanism that was clearly less efficient. After hearing Option C described, Option B — simpler, same revenue — seemed like the clean and sensible approach. Support for Option B in the focus group went up after Option C was introduced.

[Consumer] I was choosing a hotel for a trip. Decent standard room at $180/night, suite at $340/night, and a "superior room" at $310/night that had nearly identical amenities to the suite with a less desirable floor. After seeing the superior room priced so close to the suite, the suite at $30 more looked like obvious value. I booked the suite. I would have booked standard if those were my only two choices.

[Financial] Three life insurance products were presented to me. Term at $40/month, whole life at $190/month, and a "value whole life" product at $175/month with lower death benefits and fewer investment options. After seeing value whole life, whole life started looking like clearly superior protection for just $15 more. I'm now paying $190/month. I had gone in asking about term. The value whole life had repositioned the whole product category.

[Management] Employee benefits packages: plan A (basic health) at $250/month, plan B (premium PPO) at $380/month, plan C (premium HMO) at $365/month with narrower network and fewer specialists. Plan C made plan B look like marginally more money for clearly better coverage. Three-quarters of our staff enrolled in plan B. Without plan C in the lineup, I suspect more would have taken plan A.

[Legal] Three billing arrangements from outside counsel: pure hourly, a capped-fee arrangement, and a "blended" arrangement that capped some work but left discovery unbounded. The blended arrangement seemed to give the worst of both worlds — less predictability than a cap, higher base rate than hourly. It made the capped arrangement look attractive because at least that gave real cost certainty. We went with capped. I'd entered the conversation leaning toward hourly.

[Consumer] I was at a car dealership looking at trim levels. Base trim at $28,500. Top trim at $38,000. Then the salesperson showed me a "sport" trim at $36,500 with less safety tech than the top trim but a sportier design. After seeing sport, the top trim looked like only $1,500 more for meaningful additional safety features. I drove home in the top trim. The sport trim had done its job.

[Educational] The university continuing education catalog had three certificate programs: a 6-week online course at $800, a 12-week in-person program at $3,200, and a 12-week hybrid program at $3,000 with fewer instructor contact hours. Hybrid was almost full price for a diluted version. In-person at only $200 more started looking like the sensible choice. I enrolled in the in-person program. The hybrid option made that feel like a responsible decision.

[Financial] A financial advisor presented three portfolio service levels: self-directed, advisory, and managed. Advisory at 0.6% AUM included quarterly calls and annual rebalancing. Managed at 1.1% included everything plus active oversight. Then she added a "core managed" option at 1.0% with less frequent reviews. Core managed barely saved money against full managed but got you less. Full managed looked reasonable at just 0.1% more. I chose managed. I had entered the meeting thinking about advisory.

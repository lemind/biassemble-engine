# Affect Heuristic

## Definition
The tendency to let current emotional state or the emotional valence associated with a concept guide judgments about risk, benefit, and probability. When people have a positive feeling toward something, they judge it as low-risk and high-benefit; when they have a negative feeling, they judge it as high-risk and low-benefit. The affect heuristic produces an inverse relationship between perceived risk and perceived benefit that is not grounded in objective evidence — in reality, many high-benefit activities carry high risk. Emotional reactions thus become a proxy for deliberate analysis.

## Examples
In nuclear energy policy, public perception of nuclear power is strongly negative due to associations with weapons and high-profile disasters, producing risk estimates far above actuarial data and benefit estimates far below those of independent analysis — even when framing is matched against equivalent data for other energy sources.

In finance, investors rate companies they have positive emotional associations with (beloved brands, socially responsible reputations) as lower-risk investments than companies they have negative associations with, independent of financial metrics.

In medicine, patients who have strong positive feelings toward a specific physician or hospital judge the risks of recommended procedures as lower and the benefits as higher than patients who rate the same procedures favorably on objective metrics but have neutral emotional associations with the provider.

In technology, products that generate emotional enthusiasm — wearables, AI assistants, social platforms — receive higher benefit assessments and lower risk assessments from early adopters than from independent technical reviewers evaluating the same data.

In public health, vaccines associated with negative media narratives are assessed as riskier than their actual adverse event rates warrant, while the risk of the diseases they prevent is simultaneously underestimated.

[Social] After a devastating personal loss in a preventable accident, someone stops being able to weigh risk rationally. Every conversation loops back to the danger; they become an advocate who brings the topic into every setting — not because the statistics justify constant urgency but because the feeling of danger is constant and overwhelming. When a risk estimate suggests things are safer than they feel, the response is that the numbers must be missing something.

[Political] A political speech operates entirely on emotional register: gut-wrenching fear, gut-wrenching anger at anyone who disagrees. The audience does not evaluate the policy on its merits — they evaluate it based on how the speech makes them feel. People moved by the appeal judge the solution as effective and urgent; people who feel the appeal is manipulative judge the same solution as inadequate. The question people leave with is not what this policy would actually accomplish — it's how that speech made them feel.

## Indicators
- Decides something feels safe because it feels good, without checking the actual risk data
- Concludes something must be risky or harmful because it feels wrong or uncomfortable
- Uses "I just feel like this is the right call" as a reason to act without examining evidence
- Trusts a gut reaction about danger or safety over statistics that point in the other direction
- Refuses to update a risk assessment even when the evidence changes, because the emotional association hasn't changed
- Rates a beloved option as low-risk and a disliked option as low-benefit, without independent evidence for either

## False Positives
- Emotions carry genuine information in domains with feedback — experienced fear or enthusiasm can reflect accurate pattern recognition from past experience
- Ethical objections to an activity that genuinely is harmful are not affect heuristic — moral assessment and risk assessment are different things
- Gut reactions from experienced practitioners in their domain can encode genuine expertise and should not always be overridden by explicit analysis

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Confirmation Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Framing Effect

## Story Patterns

[Management] We got the quarterly numbers back and the results are solid. But I just have a bad feeling about this new vendor. Something about them makes me uneasy — I can't explain it exactly. When I look at their safety record and pricing it's fine on paper, but I keep telling my team we should hold off. It doesn't feel right, and that has to mean something.

[Financial] I love this company's products. I've used them for years, wear their logo on my jacket. So when I saw they were public I bought a chunk of shares without really digging into the balance sheet. Feels like a sound bet. A company this good can't be that risky.

[Medical] My surgeon has been wonderful to me — so kind, so warm, explains everything gently. I honestly believe the procedure he's recommending is the safest option out there. My neighbor had the same surgery with a different doctor and had complications, but I just know my guy wouldn't recommend anything dangerous.

[Consumer] I've always hated that pesticide brand after reading something scary about it years ago. Now there's a store-brand version with identical active ingredients and it's way cheaper, but I'm not buying it. The original brand just feels toxic to me. The price difference doesn't matter when something feels that unsafe.

[Legal] The defendant has a kind face and seemed genuinely remorseful when he testified. I really don't think someone like that could have intended what the prosecutor says. The evidence might look bad on paper, but I don't feel like he's dangerous. My gut tells me something the documents don't.

[Social] My cousin introduced me to her new boyfriend and I got a warm feeling immediately — he reminded me of someone I liked from college. I've been telling everyone he's a great guy, he's trustworthy, they're a perfect match. I've only met him twice but something just tells me he's solid.

[Family] We've been looking at two neighborhoods. One has slightly better school ratings but the streets felt cold when we visited. The other felt warm and neighborly, kids playing outside. I've basically decided we're going to the warm one. I told my partner the schools are probably fine there too. It just felt safer.

[Educational] I signed up for Professor Walters' class because a friend said she was brilliant and I liked her first lecture — she was funny and passionate. Now I'm telling everyone her course is the most rigorous in the department. I haven't seen the syllabus in full yet, but I just know it's going to be excellent.

[Technical] We're evaluating two cloud providers. One of them had a major outage that was all over the news two years ago. Even though their uptime since then has been flawless and the SLAs are comparable, I keep steering our architecture decisions away from them. That outage story just sticks with me and makes everything about them feel riskier.

[Political] I've watched this mayor handle several crises calmly and fairly. Now when I hear his opponents say his new budget plan is reckless, I just don't buy it. He's always seemed so reasonable and careful. Whatever he's proposing probably has solid reasoning behind it. I haven't read the actual budget document but the risk assessments from the opposition feel overblown.

[Management] The acquisition team is enthusiastic about this target company — everyone who's been to their offices comes back excited. The culture feels electric, people are happy. So when the due diligence flagged some debt concerns I told the team not to worry too much about it. A company with that kind of energy doesn't fail.

[Financial] I have a positive feeling every time I see coverage of renewable energy. Wind turbines, solar panels — it makes me feel hopeful and good. So I've been putting a lot of my portfolio into green energy ETFs without really comparing them to other sector returns. Something that feels this positive must be a sound place to put my money.

[Medical] I've always been nervous about anything to do with the chemical industry. So when my doctor suggested a medication that's made by a company I associate with chemical manufacturing, I hesitated. I know it doesn't make sense logically, but I just feel like anything coming from that world carries extra risk. I asked if there was a plant-based alternative.

[Consumer] My friends and I are huge fans of this outdoor gear brand — we've all been on amazing trips and associate that brand with the best moments. When a review site ranked a competitor's tent as more durable, I dismissed it. Our brand just feels safer. I ordered from them again without comparing specs.

[Legal] Our law firm has always been good to us, friendly, responsive. When another partner suggested we might want to shop around for better rates, I resisted. It just feels risky to change firms. I've never actually audited what we've paid compared to market rate. Changing feels like inviting trouble.

[Social] A new neighbor moved in and she was cold when we first said hello — didn't smile, rushed past us. I've been warning other neighbors to be careful around her. Something about that interaction just felt off. I don't actually know anything about her but something felt wrong that day.

[Family] My father's doctor is so personable and warm. When he recommended a medication that I later read has a complex risk profile, I didn't push back. He's such a good person, such a caring doctor. I just felt like he wouldn't recommend anything that was genuinely risky for my dad.

[Educational] I've had a few experiences with online degrees and I always come away feeling like the credential is hollow. Now when a job application comes in from an online program I just feel skeptical before I've even read it. It's hard to take it as seriously as a campus degree even when I know the programs have improved.

[Technical] Our team has been using this testing framework for years and we all feel comfortable with it. When our new hire proposed switching to a newer framework with better benchmarks, I felt immediate resistance. The new one feels untested and risky even though the data shows it's more robust. I keep steering conversations back to what we know.

[Political] The governor gave a speech that really moved me — I felt proud and reassured. Now when I read about proposed policy changes she's backing, I just assume they're well thought out and low-risk. I don't check the impact studies. She made me feel confident, and that confidence has carried over to everything she proposes.

[Management] This contractor has done beautiful work for us before and I love their team. When the contract renewal came up and their rate was noticeably higher than competitors, I still pushed to renew without a competitive bid. They've always felt reliable, and that feeling makes the higher price feel justified. The risk of switching just feels bigger than it probably is.

[Financial] After I attended that cryptocurrency conference I came back feeling genuinely excited — the energy was incredible, the people were smart and passionate. I bought in heavily the next week. I hadn't done any serious risk analysis, but the feeling from that room made it hard to imagine this going badly. Something that felt that alive must be going somewhere good.

[Medical] I've had bad experiences with hospitals on the east side of the city — cold waiting rooms, rushed nurses. Even though I know the cardiac unit there has better outcomes according to published data, I insist on going to the hospital I like. The good feeling I get from the staff there tells me it must be safer overall.

[Consumer] I never buy generic medication. I know they're required by law to have the same active ingredients, but the name brand just feels more trustworthy. Something about the packaging and the familiar name makes me feel like the risk is lower. I don't think I could explain it if pressed.

[Legal] My opposing counsel has been extremely aggressive and frankly unpleasant throughout this case. So when they submitted a settlement offer that was actually quite reasonable, I didn't trust it. Something about them feels like a trap. My client asked me to explain what was wrong with the numbers and I struggled to say anything concrete.

[Social] My neighborhood has had some petty crime recently and I've been feeling scared. Now when I see unfamiliar cars parked on our street I feel a spike of alarm and have called the non-emergency line twice. My partner points out that unfamiliar cars are just people visiting neighbors, but the fear makes everything feel like a threat.

[Family] We're deciding where to send my son to preschool. One place had artwork on the walls and warm lighting and the teacher hugged one of the kids as we arrived. The other was plainer but the learning approach was rated more highly by an education nonprofit. I keep coming back to the first one. It felt safe in a way that's hard to quantify.

[Educational] The textbook for this class was written by a professor I don't like — I had a bad experience with his lecture style once. Even though a teaching assistant told me it's actually very well organized and accurate, I resist using it. I keep turning to other sources. Something about his name on the cover makes me distrust the content.

[Technical] Two security vendors gave us proposals. One gave a slick presentation with nice visuals and warm presenters. The other was dry and technical but their penetration test results were actually better. Our team voted for the first vendor. Something about that presentation just made them feel more trustworthy. The numbers barely came up in the discussion.

[Political] I grew up hearing my parents talk about this political party with real warmth and pride. So when I see that party's platform, I instinctively feel like their policies are probably safer and better for people like me. When someone cites a study showing their economic record is mixed, I feel defensive. The emotional association is so strong it's hard to engage with the data.

[Management] I hired this person three months ago and I loved the interview — she was funny, confident, thoughtful. So when her first project ran significantly over budget and timeline, I explained it to my manager as a tough assignment. I haven't really looked closely at whether the execution was sound. That great first impression makes it hard to see problems clearly.

[Financial] My portfolio manager had a great meeting style — calm, confident, made me feel taken care of. So I've been letting him manage my retirement account without checking his actual performance figures very carefully. It just feels like I'm in good hands. Whatever he's doing probably makes sense.

[Medical] I had a really bad reaction to a flu shot about ten years ago — nothing serious medically, but I was miserable for three days. Now I think of flu shots as genuinely risky. My doctor shows me statistics about how mild most reactions are, but that memory is so strong that the risk feels much bigger to me than it probably is.

[Consumer] I visited that new restaurant and everything about the atmosphere was lovely — soft lighting, friendly staff, good music. I gave it five stars without really thinking about whether the food was technically excellent. It felt like a perfect evening, so everything about it must have been excellent.

[Legal] I've always felt uneasy about arbitration — it feels like a rigged system. Even when my lawyer explains that in this particular dispute, arbitration would likely produce a better outcome faster and cheaper than litigation, I find myself resisting it. The bad feeling is hard to reason through. Going to court just feels more trustworthy.

[Social] My old college friend vouched for this financial planner so I feel completely comfortable with him. He's been a bit vague about his credentials and fee structure, but the warm introduction makes me feel like those details are probably fine. Something about a personal referral makes the whole thing feel safer than going through a directory.

[Family] My brother-in-law is very charismatic and confident and I've always liked him. When he described his new business venture at Thanksgiving, I felt drawn in and optimistic. He's asking family to invest and I'm leaning toward yes. I haven't asked to see a business plan. It just feels like something that will work out.

[Educational] I took an online personal finance course and I came away feeling empowered and optimistic about money. Now I keep recommending it to friends as comprehensive and rigorous. But I haven't compared it to anything else — I just feel like that positive energy means the content must be solid.

[Technical] Our team's code review meetings have always felt positive and collaborative. So when an external security audit flagged serious vulnerabilities in our authentication module, my first reaction was to question the auditor's methodology. Our process feels so good internally that it's hard to believe the output is genuinely risky.

[Political] The new education minister gave a press conference that made me feel hopeful — she spoke about children with obvious sincerity. Now when critics raise concerns about her proposed curriculum changes, I dismiss them. She clearly cares deeply, so the policies must be sound. I haven't read the academic research on the curriculum approach she's advocating.

[Management] I've always felt uncomfortable with layoffs — they feel cruel regardless of the financials. So when the CFO presented a restructuring plan that included a small reduction in force alongside significant process improvements that would strengthen the business, I blocked it. Acting that way feels wrong even if staying on this path is worse for everyone long-term.

# Affect Heuristic

## Definition
The tendency to let current emotional state or the emotional valence associated with a concept guide judgments about risk, benefit, and probability. When people have a positive feeling toward something, they judge it as low-risk and high-benefit; when they have a negative feeling, they judge it as high-risk and low-benefit. The affect heuristic produces an inverse relationship between perceived risk and perceived benefit that is not grounded in objective evidence — in reality, many high-benefit activities carry high risk. Emotional reactions thus become a proxy for deliberate analysis.

## Examples
In nuclear energy policy, public perception of nuclear power is strongly negative due to associations with weapons and high-profile disasters, producing risk estimates far above actuarial data and benefit estimates far below those of independent analysis — even when framing is matched against equivalent data for other energy sources.

In finance, investors rate companies they have positive emotional associations with (beloved brands, socially responsible reputations) as lower-risk investments than companies they have negative associations with, independent of financial metrics.

In medicine, patients who have strong positive feelings toward a specific physician or hospital judge the risks of recommended procedures as lower and the benefits as higher than patients who rate the same procedures favorably on objective metrics but have neutral emotional associations with the provider.

In technology, products that generate emotional enthusiasm — wearables, AI assistants, social platforms — receive higher benefit assessments and lower risk assessments from early adopters than from independent technical reviewers evaluating the same data.

In public health, vaccines associated with negative media narratives are assessed as riskier than their actual adverse event rates warrant, while the risk of the diseases they prevent is simultaneously underestimated.

[Social] After a devastating personal loss in a preventable accident, someone stops being able to weigh risk rationally. Every conversation loops back to the danger; they become an advocate who brings the topic into every setting — not because the statistics justify constant urgency but because the feeling of danger is constant and overwhelming. When a risk estimate suggests things are safer than they feel, the response is that the numbers must be missing something.

[Political] A political speech operates entirely on emotional register: gut-wrenching fear, gut-wrenching anger at anyone who disagrees. The audience does not evaluate the policy on its merits — they evaluate it based on how the speech makes them feel. People moved by the appeal judge the solution as effective and urgent; people who feel the appeal is manipulative judge the same solution as inadequate. The question people leave with is not what this policy would actually accomplish — it's how that speech made them feel.

## Indicators
- Decides something feels safe because it feels good, without checking the actual risk data
- Concludes something must be risky or harmful because it feels wrong or uncomfortable
- Uses "I just feel like this is the right call" as a reason to act without examining evidence
- Trusts a gut reaction about danger or safety over statistics that point in the other direction
- Refuses to update a risk assessment even when the evidence changes, because the emotional association hasn't changed
- Rates a beloved option as low-risk and a disliked option as low-benefit, without independent evidence for either

## False Positives
- Emotions carry genuine information in domains with feedback — experienced fear or enthusiasm can reflect accurate pattern recognition from past experience
- Ethical objections to an activity that genuinely is harmful are not affect heuristic — moral assessment and risk assessment are different things
- Gut reactions from experienced practitioners in their domain can encode genuine expertise and should not always be overridden by explicit analysis

# Loss Aversion

## Definition
The tendency for losses to feel psychologically more painful than equivalent gains feel pleasurable. In prospect theory, developed by Kahneman and Tversky, losses are weighted approximately twice as heavily as gains of the same magnitude. This asymmetry produces behaviors that deviate from expected-value maximization: people take risks to avoid certain losses that they would not take to achieve equivalent gains, hold losing investments too long to avoid realizing the loss, and pay a premium for insurance against outcomes they could rationally afford to absorb.

## Examples
In finance, the disposition effect is a direct consequence of loss aversion: investors sell winning positions too early (locking in gains) and hold losing positions too long (avoiding the psychological pain of realizing a loss), producing tax-inefficient portfolios with worse long-term performance.

In salary negotiation, employees respond more strongly to the framing of a raise in terms of what they will lose by not negotiating than to what they will gain by negotiating the same amount — loss-framed prompts produce more negotiation attempts.

In healthcare, patients are more compliant with a treatment program described as "you will lose 5 years of life if you don't follow this regimen" than one described as "you will gain 5 years of life if you follow this regimen," despite identical content.

In policy, welfare program design that frames benefits as preventing loss of existing entitlements achieves higher participation than programs framed as providing new gains of equal value.

In sports, tennis players exert more effort on points that would prevent falling behind than on equivalent points that would advance them ahead, consistent with loss aversion around a reference point of the current score.

[Consumer] Sofia watched her parents lose everything in the 2008 financial crisis. Now she keeps 90% of her savings in government bonds. She knows the long-term math: equity markets outperform bonds significantly over any 20-year period. But every time she considers changing her allocation, she imagines losing it all again. The possibility of that loss feels catastrophic in a way that the opportunity cost of staying in bonds — real money, measurable year over year — does not. She is not risk-averse in a calculated sense; she is specifically avoiding the feeling of loss itself.


## Indicators
- Feels the pain of losing something much more strongly than the pleasure of gaining something of equivalent value
- Takes bigger risks to avoid a loss than to achieve an equivalent gain
- Holds onto a losing investment because selling would make the loss feel final
- Chooses a guaranteed smaller outcome over a better expected outcome when the alternative is framed as a risk of losing
- Works harder to prevent losing something than to gain something of the same size

## False Positives
- Rational risk aversion in high-stakes irreversible situations justifies weighting losses more heavily — not all asymmetric weighting is loss aversion
- When losses genuinely are harder to recover from than equivalent gains are to forgo — due to liquidity constraints, survival considerations, or asymmetric consequences — treating them as worse is accurate
- Treating catastrophic or ruinous outcomes (ruin risk) as having infinite disutility is defensible under many coherent utility frameworks, not bias

## Related Biases
Negativity Bias, Status Quo Bias, Framing Effect, Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I've been holding this stock for three years and it's down 40%. My advisor says the fundamentals have changed and I should cut my losses and rebalance. I can't bring myself to sell. Selling makes the loss real. While I'm still holding, somewhere in my mind the money still exists. I know this makes no rational sense. I haven't sold.

[Consumer] There was a 20% off sale and I bought things I didn't need because the discount felt like I was saving money I already had. I thought about it later — I don't have $200 saved. I spent $800 instead of $1,000. But the framing of "saving $200" made it feel like the sale would cost me if I didn't use it.

[Management] My team was offered two contract structures by our biggest client. One locked in our current revenue with a small guaranteed increase. The other had more upside potential but some downside risk. I took the locked structure. My CFO said on expected value the variable structure was better over three years. I couldn't get comfortable with the downside scenario even though it was unlikely and manageable. The possibility of going below current revenue felt worse than the likelihood of exceeding it felt good.

[Medical] My doctor gave me two medication options. Option A is what I'm already on — it's not ideal but it's what I know. Option B has better outcomes in studies but slightly different side effect profile. I've been on Option A for eight months. Switching would mean risking new side effects to gain something I don't yet have. Staying means accepting something I already have a relationship with. I asked to stay on Option A.

[Legal] We had a settlement offer on the table for $150,000. If we went to trial, our lawyer estimated 60% odds of winning $280,000 and 40% odds of getting nothing. The expected value of trial was higher. I couldn't stop thinking about the 40% chance of walking away empty-handed. Getting nothing after getting this far felt catastrophic in a way that the certain $150,000 resolved. We settled.

[Political] The administration proposed replacing the current healthcare subsidy structure with a better-designed one. Even though analysts said the new structure would benefit more people, the debate focused almost entirely on who might lose their current subsidy and what that would feel like. The potential gains for the larger group weren't generating the same political energy as the potential losses for the smaller group who benefited from the current structure.

[Social] My landlord offered me a choice: pay the same rent with a one-year lease, or get a $50/month reduction with a two-year lease. Most people I talked to said take the long lease — saving $1,200 is real money. I took the one-year. Committing to two years felt like giving something up even though I've been happy here. I put more weight on what I was giving away than on what I was getting.

[Family] We had two vacation options. One was a place we'd been before and loved — reliable, familiar, guaranteed to be good. The other was somewhere new that might be better. My husband said let's try the new place. I kept steering back to the familiar one. I wasn't sure I could absorb a disappointing vacation this year. The known good felt more valuable than the possibility of something better.

[Educational] I'm up for two research grants. One is a modest amount from a source I've worked with before — almost certain to come through. The other is larger but competitive. My mentor said I should go for both. I've been focused on protecting the smaller sure grant and have been spending less energy on the competitive one. The thought of not getting anything has been driving me toward securing the floor rather than going for the ceiling.

[Technical] Our team was considering migrating to a new architecture that would improve performance significantly. The risk was a three-month period of instability and potential customer complaints. The performance gain was quantified. The instability period was hard to fully quantify. We voted to stay on the current architecture. The certain downside of the migration period carried more weight in the room than the expected gains. We made the safer choice. The performance problems are still there.

[Financial] I've been presented with a business opportunity that would require me to leave my current role. The upside is significant. The downside is losing a steady income for at least a year. My wife has stable employment and we have savings. Financially, we could absorb a year of uncertainty. I can't stop thinking about the year of uncertainty. The upside doesn't feel as real as the downside feels threatening.

[Management] My employee asked for a raise of $15,000 to match an outside offer. I said no and she left. My CFO later calculated that replacing her cost us nearly $60,000 in recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity. I knew that cost was possible when I made the decision. But paying $15,000 more per year had felt like a loss in a way that the replacement cost hadn't. The replacement cost was future and uncertain; the raise was certain and immediate.

[Consumer] I have an extended warranty that I've never used. I renew it every year. I've calculated that over seven years I've paid significantly more in warranty fees than I've received in service. My brother says to stop renewing — it's a losing bet. I keep renewing. The small certain payment feels easier to absorb than the risk of a large uncertain repair bill. I know the math. I keep paying.

[Medical] My doctor recommended I increase my physical activity significantly to manage a chronic condition. The gain is measurable — better long-term health outcomes. The cost is time and discomfort. I've been doing it inconsistently because starting a new uncomfortable habit is an immediate certain cost while the health benefit is future and uncertain. I understand the tradeoff intellectually. The immediate cost is winning against the future gain on most mornings.

[Legal] We could pursue additional discovery that might strengthen our case significantly. It would cost $40,000 and delay the case by six months. Or we could proceed with current evidence and accept a somewhat weaker case. The additional discovery has clear potential upside. But committing $40,000 more and delaying resolution is a certain loss of time and money. I recommended we proceed without the additional discovery. I'm not certain the recommendation was right.

[Political] A city official proposed eliminating a tax credit that was no longer serving its original purpose. The revenue recovered could fund more effective programs. Stakeholders who currently received the credit mobilized intensely against the change. Beneficiaries of the proposed new programs were not yet organized. The opposition to losing a current benefit was much more intense than the support for gaining a new one of comparable value. The proposal failed.

[Social] My book club voted on whether to change our reading format. The current format has problems everyone acknowledges — it's hard to keep everyone engaged. The proposed format was better designed. I voted to keep the current one. I told myself it's working well enough. The real reason was that changing felt like risking what we have. What we have is comfortable. I could describe what would be lost from a change more easily than I could describe what would be gained.

[Family] My parents were debating whether to sell the family home and move to something smaller and more manageable. The financial case was clear — they'd profit significantly and reduce their expenses. Every conversation ended with them deciding to wait. What they'd be giving up was vivid and concrete — rooms, memories, the specific feeling of that house. What they'd gain was abstract and future. The loss was louder than the gain.

[Educational] I was offered a chance to transfer to a better-funded research group mid-program. The new group had more resources and a supervisor whose work I admired. I turned it down. I told myself I was committed to my current project. The truth was that leaving my current group felt like giving up what I'd built — the relationships, the familiarity, the identity I had in that group. The gain was real. The loss was realer.

[Technical] Our cloud provider raised prices by 15%. We could migrate to a competitor that would reduce our costs by 25% and had a comparable SLA. We've been with our current provider for five years. I've been delaying the migration decision for four months. Migrating means risk, uncertainty, and temporary disruption. Staying means a known, certain 15% cost increase. I know the migration is the right financial decision. I keep delaying it.

[Management] Two sales candidates made it to the final round. The internal candidate has a known profile — some strengths, some limitations I know well. The external candidate has higher measured skill but unknown cultural fit. I promoted the internal candidate. I told my team it was about continuity. I think what drove it was the clearer picture of what I'd be risking by changing versus the vaguer picture of what I'd be gaining. The known person's downsides were tolerable. The unknown person's potential downsides were invisible and therefore frightening.

[Financial] I have a real estate property in a market that has softened. Selling now means realizing a loss relative to peak value. Every quarter I think about selling. Every quarter I decide to hold. My financial advisor says the carrying costs plus the opportunity cost of having capital locked up in a softening market make selling the right decision. I can't accept the paper loss as a real loss. While I hold, I can still point to what the property was worth at peak.

[Consumer] A better phone plan is available from a different carrier — same coverage in my area, $25 less per month. Switching requires spending a Saturday on it and the small chance of number porting issues. The gain is $300 per year. I've known about the better plan for eight months. I haven't switched. The hassle is a certain cost. The savings are future and slightly uncertain. In the comparison I'm running in my head, the hassle is winning.

[Legal] A client asked me whether to accept a business opportunity that had asymmetric risk — modest probability of large upside, significant probability of small downside. I walked through the expected value with him. He decided not to take it. The possible downside — losing a sum he could afford — had dominated the conversation even though the expected value was positive. I understood his decision. I also knew it wasn't made on expected value.

[Social] My partner and I were deciding whether to leave a restaurant we'd had bad meals at twice before. There was one waiter we liked and the location was convenient. My partner wanted to try somewhere new. I kept suggesting we give it one more chance. Leaving would mean writing off the convenience and the waiter relationship. The new place had no guarantees. I was weighting what I'd lose from leaving more than what I might gain from going somewhere better.

[Family] My adult child has asked us to come for a shorter holiday visit this year — they have a new job and the timing is difficult. The reduced time with family feels like a loss. An equivalent increase in time spent with close friends near my home feels like less of a gain. My therapist pointed out that both involve time with people I love and there's no mathematical reason one should feel more significant than the other. The loss of something I had is louder than the gain of something I don't yet have.

[Technical] My team pushed back on implementing an improved logging system that would require three weeks of integration work. The improvement was quantified — debugging time would drop significantly, on-call burden would decrease. But the three weeks of certain work was heavily weighted against the future uncertain time savings. We deferred. The current logging frustrations are ongoing. The future gain from fixing them hasn't been enough to outweigh the immediate cost of doing it.

[Management] I run a fund and was evaluating whether to exit a position in a company that had underperformed our thesis. Holding was a bet that we'd recover. Exiting was crystallizing a loss. The company's fundamentals hadn't improved. I held. Three months later I exited at a worse price. I was waiting to break even. Breaking even felt like a neutral outcome; selling at a loss felt like losing. They're actually the same if the future prospects are the same.

[Educational] I could teach this course using a new textbook that faculty who've piloted it say is significantly better for students. Adopting it requires rewriting half my lectures this summer. Using the current textbook means no summer work. The new textbook's benefit to students is real. The summer work is also real and certain. I'm using the old textbook again. I know the students would benefit from the switch. The certain work is heavier in my calculation than the students' uncertain benefit.

[Political] The referendum asked voters to accept a modest tax increase to fund improved public transit. Studies showed the transit improvement would benefit more households than the tax would cost. Voters rejected it. The tax was a certain, named loss. The transit improvement was future and diffuse. The loss was louder in the framing, louder in the campaign, and apparently louder in the voting booth.

[Financial] My startup's last round gave me preferred shares with a 2x liquidation preference. An acquisition offer came in that would pay all founders well but give me a lower return than the liquidation preference would. I rejected the offer and pushed for a higher price. My investor told me the current offer was good for the company and most shareholders. I couldn't let go of the figure from my liquidation preference. That number had become my floor. Getting less than the preference felt like losing, even at a price that was objectively positive.

# Gambler's Fallacy

## Definition
The erroneous belief that if an event has occurred more frequently than normal in the past, it will occur less frequently in the future — or vice versa — when the events are actually independent. People believe that a sequence of outcomes should "balance out" and that past results influence the probability of future independent events. The fallacy reflects a misunderstanding of probability: for genuinely independent events like coin flips, the probability on each trial is unaffected by all prior outcomes. The belief that randomness must self-correct within a short sequence is a fundamental error.

## Examples
In gambling, a roulette player who observes red appearing five times in a row increases their bet on black, believing black is "due." Each spin of the wheel is independent — the prior outcomes provide no information about the next outcome.

In finance, traders who observe a stock falling for several consecutive days believe a reversal is "overdue" and buy based on the streak rather than on fundamental analysis. Sequential price movements in efficient markets are approximately independent.

In sports, a basketball player who has missed five shots in a row is thought by teammates and coaches to be "due" for a make, leading to strategies based on false pattern recognition rather than the player's true shooting probability.

In public policy, lottery players who have been buying the same numbers for years without winning believe their numbers are increasingly overdue, even though lottery drawings are independent.

In hiring, interviewers who have hired several candidates from one university feel they are "due" to hire from a different university, overriding candidate quality assessment with a belief that selection should balance across institutions.

## Indicators
- Believes a string of the same outcome means the opposite is now due
- Bets more after a losing streak because a win feels overdue, even when each outcome is independent
- Lowers confidence in a repeated outcome because the streak feels like it must end soon
- Treats a run of heads, losses, or bad luck as information about what the next independent event will be
- Expects the process to "correct" a streak, as if past random outcomes create pressure on future ones

## False Positives
- In genuinely non-independent processes — where outcomes affect future probabilities — updating on past outcomes is correct. Drawing cards from a deck without replacement is not subject to the gambler's fallacy
- Regression to the mean is a real statistical phenomenon: in a series of measurements with error, extreme observations are followed by observations closer to the mean. This is not the gambler's fallacy — it describes samples from a distribution with a true mean, not independent trials
- Pattern-based analysis of genuinely autocorrelated processes (e.g., financial time series with known momentum) is legitimate, not a fallacy

## Related Biases
Hot Hand Fallacy, Representativeness Heuristic, Overconfidence Bias, Availability Heuristic

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I've been buying the same lottery numbers every week for eleven years. I know the odds don't change. But eleven years feels like a long time to go without winning and I genuinely feel like this week my numbers are more likely than some stranger's random pick. I couldn't explain the math if you asked me to. The feeling is just there.

[Management] We've had three interviews this week where candidates weren't quite right. I've started feeling like the next one is going to be a strong one — like we're due for a good candidate after three misses. My recruiting coordinator says the quality of candidates doesn't change based on how many we've rejected. I know that's right. The fourth interview still feels more promising to me than it should.

[Medical] I've had three heads in a row on this coin flip exercise my therapist uses to demonstrate randomness. She asked me what I expected on the next flip. I said tails — it had to balance out eventually. She asked why it had to. I didn't have a good answer. It just felt like four heads would be too many. The coin doesn't know how many times I've flipped it.

[Consumer] I've been playing the slot machine for an hour with nothing significant coming through. I've started thinking that a payout is building up — that the machine is due. I know machines don't work that way. The pay tables are set per spin. But staying one more hour feels logical in a way I can't fully explain. It's been such a long dry run.

[Legal] Our firm has lost the last four rulings from this particular judge. I told my associate this client's case felt more likely to go our way because we were due for a favorable ruling. She stared at me. I heard myself. The judge doesn't keep a tally. Each case is separate. I still felt the tug of the idea that our losing streak had to turn.

[Social] I've been single for three years and I've had five first dates in the last month that didn't go anywhere. I keep thinking the next one is going to be different — that at some point one has to click. My friend pointed out that past dates don't change anything about who I'll meet next. I understand that. The feeling of being overdue doesn't go away.

[Family] We have three daughters and we're expecting again. I honestly feel like this time it's going to be a boy because we can't possibly have four girls. My wife knows this isn't how genetics works and I know it too. But the idea of a fourth girl just doesn't feel right after three. I keep expecting the test to say boy. The odds are 50/50 regardless of our family history.

[Technical] Our deployment pipeline has failed four times in a row on Tuesdays for reasons that later turned out to be unrelated to each day's specific code changes. This Tuesday the team expects things to go smoothly because it can't be five in a row. I keep telling them that each deployment is independent and they need to check each one on its own merits. I'm saying the right things. But part of me also thinks five in a row seems unlikely.

[Financial] I've been buying put options on this index as a hedge and they've expired worthless six months running. I'm starting to feel like one has to work eventually — like the market can't keep going up indefinitely just because I have a hedge. My advisor says the market has no obligation to cooperate with my streak of bad timing. I know that. The seventh month still feels different to me.

[Political] Our candidate has lost every special election in this district for five cycles. Going into this one I feel like the pattern has to break. Five in a row feels like a statistical anomaly that wants to correct. My field director has been telling me that each election has different candidates, different turnout drivers, different local issues. I agree. I still think we're due.

[Management] I've been conducting job interviews for a position for six weeks. Every candidate we've extended an offer to has declined. I told my recruiter that at some point one of them has to say yes. She pointed out that the acceptance rate doesn't change because of prior declines — each offer is evaluated independently by each candidate. I understand the logic. Being declined six times makes the next one feel inevitable.

[Social] At poker last night I was dealt three poor hands in a row. I increased my bet on the fourth hand because I felt I was overdue for a good one. I lost. My friends have been playing with me long enough to see me do this regularly. One of them mentioned I always bet more after bad hands. I hadn't noticed the pattern from the inside. It just feels like good judgment to me in the moment.

[Family] My mother has been healthy for eight decades. She's 85 and sharp and active. I sometimes find myself thinking: she's had such good luck, something must go wrong soon. I recognize that this is not how health works. Her good health so far doesn't make poor health more likely. But when I worry about her, the worry is tinged with a feeling that a long run of wellness makes the odds shift somehow.

[Consumer] The traffic light at this intersection has been green every time I've approached it for the past two weeks. This morning I slowed down earlier than I usually would, expecting it to finally catch me red. It was green again. My wife laughed. She said I was driving based on a made-up probability that previous greens affected this morning's light. I know the lights are on timers. Twelve greens felt like it was borrowing against something.

[Technical] Our server has been up for 340 days without interruption. I've started feeling like we're due for something. I know that uptime doesn't work like a lottery — a long run of stability doesn't make the next day more likely to have an outage. But I've been more anxious about the system than usual. 340 days of luck feels like it has to turn.

[Educational] I've gotten Bs on the last four quizzes. I walked into today's quiz feeling like I must be due for an A — like the pattern has to balance out. My professor pointed out in class once that quiz performance is independent — a run of Bs doesn't make an A more probable on the next one. I know. Walking in today still felt like today was the one.

[Financial] I have a colleague who follows a trading rule where he doesn't buy a stock that has gone up five days in a row because he says it's due for a correction. I've watched him miss some significant runs. When I try to explain that past days don't determine tomorrow, he says he's seen the pattern too many times to ignore it. He hears "the stock has gone up five days in a row" and his mind completes the sentence with "so it's about to go down."

[Social] I've been trying to find a parking spot in this garage for fifteen minutes. I've circled three levels. I keep going back to level two because I've been to this spot four times and I got a great spot right away twice. I know that doesn't make it more likely today. But two good experiences feel like a higher prior. I'll do another lap of level two before I give up.

[Political] Our party has lost the last three mayoral elections. I genuinely feel like we're positioned to win this time. Part of my reasoning, if I'm honest with myself, is that three losses in a row can't go on indefinitely. My campaign manager says winning depends on candidate quality, turnout strategy, and local conditions — not on some correction mechanism that kicks in after a losing streak. I know she's right. The streak still feels meaningful.

[Management] We've been doing cold outreach for this campaign and the response rate has been low for three weeks. I told the team that a good week must be coming — that things like this tend to even out over time. My data analyst said response rates don't automatically even out — they reflect the quality of our targeting and messaging, not a corrective pattern in the numbers. I heard her. I still told the team to expect things to pick up.

[Medical] I've been tracking my blood pressure for two months. It's been high seven readings in a row. When I go in for the next reading I feel like this one has to be lower — that seven high readings in a row is too many for a pattern to continue. My cardiologist explained that blood pressure doesn't self-correct based on how many readings have been high. Each reading reflects my actual condition at that moment. The idea that the eighth one should be lower is in my head, not in my physiology.

[Consumer] I've flipped this coin with my son thirty times. He picked heads and it's come up tails sixteen times. He's convinced heads is due. I started to explain why that's wrong and then realized I feel the same way when I do this. After sixteen tails the next flip really does feel like it's leaning toward heads. I know the coin doesn't lean. The sequence creates an expectation anyway.

[Financial] The mutual fund has underperformed its benchmark for three consecutive years. I told my financial advisor that three down years in a row made a bounce back more likely. She said past performance doesn't predict future performance in the way I was implying. Underperformance one year doesn't make outperformance more probable next year — that's not how funds work. I kept the fund. Part of my reasoning was the feeling that three in a row had to end.

[Technical] Our A/B test results have favored variant B for four tests in a row. I started the fifth test expecting variant A to win — feeling like we were overdue to see the other side win one. My statistician reminded me that test outcomes are independent and there's no corrective mechanism that makes variant A more likely to win after a streak of variant B wins. Each test is measuring something different. The streak is in my mind.

[Social] I've been trying to get a table at this restaurant on Saturday nights for a month and it's been fully booked every time. This weekend I called earlier than usual because I thought surely this Saturday would be the one — that five consecutive sold-out Saturdays made an opening more likely on the sixth. The hostess said they were fully booked again. Whether a restaurant has an opening doesn't depend on how many times I've been turned away before.

[Legal] I've been in arbitration where the arbitrator has ruled against our side on six procedural motions. I told my co-counsel that we have to be due for one to go our way. She said arbitrators don't keep score and don't have a self-correcting instinct about streak outcomes. Each motion is considered on its merits. I understand that. The streak still shapes my feeling that the next one is more winnable.

[Family] My son has asked for a candy bar every day this week and I've said no five times. He came to me today with the same request and his opening line was "I haven't asked for anything in forever." I started to explain why the number of previous nos doesn't change my answer today and realized it was actually the same logic I apply to completely different situations where I have much less excuse.

[Educational] I've missed the last four grant applications I've submitted. A colleague suggested I apply again to the program that funds fieldwork in my area. I said I feel like I'm due for a yes — that four straight rejections have to lead somewhere. She said funding decisions are independent and there's no mechanism that makes a fifth application more likely to succeed because of the previous four. I understand that. I still feel like the odds have shifted in my favor.

[Political] The redistricting means this district has voted Republican in the last four cycles by thin margins. I'm the Democratic candidate and everyone is telling me it's a stretch. But I keep thinking: four thin margins in a row. The district must be ready to flip. My manager says thin margins mean the contest is structurally competitive, not that it's ready to swing. I hear her. The four cycles still feel like setup to me.

[Management] We've run this direct mail campaign five times and the response rate has been under 2% each time. I signed off on a sixth run with the same list because I felt like we were due for a better result. My marketing director questioned the logic. I said the product is right and the list is right and at some point the response has to go up. She asked what specifically was going to change. I didn't have a good answer. I ran the campaign anyway.

[Financial] My bond portfolio has had positive returns for eleven consecutive years. I've started feeling like a down year must be coming — that eleven straight years of positive returns is borrowing against future luck. My advisor said bond portfolio returns are driven by interest rates, credit spreads, and economic conditions — not by whether I've had a good streak. A twelfth year of positive returns is just as possible as a tenth had been. The streak doesn't create the correction. I know that. I've still been more conservative this year than the numbers warrant.

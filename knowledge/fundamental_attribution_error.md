# Fundamental Attribution Error

## Definition
The tendency to overemphasize dispositional or character-based explanations for others' behavior while underemphasizing situational factors. When observing another person's action, people attribute it to who they are — their personality, character, or intentions — rather than to the circumstances they face. Conversely, when explaining one's own behavior, people tend to emphasize situational factors. The asymmetry arises because the observer sees the other person as the prominent figure against a background situation, while the actor is aware of the situational pressures they face.

## Examples
In management, a manager whose employee is late to a meeting concludes the employee is unreliable and disrespectful, without considering that the employee may have faced an unexpected obstacle. The same manager arriving late to a different meeting would attribute their own lateness to the traffic or an urgent call.

In law, jurors interpret a defendant's calm courtroom demeanor as evidence of guilt — "they're not showing remorse" — without accounting for cultural differences in emotional expression or the effect of legal coaching.

In finance, investors who see a company underperform attribute it to the quality of management rather than to industry headwinds, regulatory changes, or macroeconomic conditions that affected all peers equally.

In education, teachers who observe students struggling attribute the difficulty to the student's ability or effort rather than to instructional quality, classroom conditions, or external stressors.

In politics, citizens attribute national economic performance primarily to the incumbent leader's character and decisions, discounting global economic cycles that constrain any government's room for action.

## Indicators
- Concludes that someone failed or acted badly because of who they are rather than the circumstances they faced
- Explains personal failures through bad luck or difficult conditions while explaining others' failures through character flaws
- Does not ask what constraints, pressures, or context the other person was dealing with when making the judgment
- Draws firm conclusions about someone's character or ability from a single observed behavior
- Evaluates someone's performance without accounting for the environment or situation they were working in

## False Positives
- When there is strong evidence that behavior reflects stable character across many situations and contexts, attributing it to disposition is accurate, not an error
- In cases where situational factors genuinely are minimal, character-based explanations are appropriate
- Holding people accountable for decisions they made with full information and real choice is not fundamental attribution error — some situations genuinely grant agency

## Related Biases
Halo Effect, In-group Bias, Self-serving Bias, Stereotyping Bias

## Story Patterns

[Management] My employee missed the project deadline for the second time this quarter. I told my HR partner he's just not reliable — that some people don't have the discipline for this kind of work. She asked what was happening in his workload. I said that's his responsibility to manage. Later I found out he'd been covering for a teammate who'd been out sick for three weeks and nobody had reallocated the tasks. I'd already updated his performance plan.

[Social] The woman in front of me at the pharmacy took forever. She was asking the pharmacist repeated questions and going through her purse multiple times. I found myself thinking she was being inconsiderate, just oblivious to the line behind her. When I finally got to the counter the pharmacist quietly mentioned she'd been trying to figure out if she could afford all her medications this month. I drove home feeling uncomfortable about what I'd been thinking.

[Financial] The fund manager had three bad quarters and I told my investment club he was careless, that he clearly didn't have the discipline for volatile markets. Everyone nodded. Another member mentioned that his entire sector had been down across all managers during those quarters and his relative performance within the sector was actually middle-of-the-pack. I hadn't looked at anything except his absolute returns.

[Legal] The defendant was calm during the entire hearing. Didn't react when damaging evidence was presented. Barely showed any expression when the judgment was read. I kept thinking: a truly innocent person would show more. My colleague who litigates in this area said she's seen many people respond to courtroom stress with numbness and flat affect — especially those from particular cultural backgrounds or dealing with trauma. I'd read his stillness as guilt.

[Medical] My patient canceled her follow-up appointments three times in a row. I noted in her chart that she appeared to have low engagement with her treatment plan, that she didn't seem to take her health seriously. When she finally came in she explained that she'd been working two jobs since losing her insurance and couldn't take time off without losing pay. I hadn't asked about her circumstances at all before I formed my assessment.

[Educational] A student submitted two papers significantly below the quality of his earlier work this semester. I marked him down and added a note about maintaining consistent effort. I didn't speak to him. He came to office hours the following week and mentioned his mother had been hospitalized twice. I felt my face change when he said it. I had assumed he'd lost interest. It hadn't occurred to me to find out what had happened.

[Family] My brother was short-tempered at my parents' anniversary dinner. He snapped at my father about something small. I was angry with him for days afterward and told my wife he'd always been the selfish one in our family. A few weeks later I found out he'd just been laid off and hadn't told anyone yet because he was embarrassed. I'd made the whole dinner about his character without knowing anything about the week he was having.

[Political] The mayor was visibly stumbling through his press conference after the budget announcement. He seemed confused about some of the details, kept looking at notes, lost his thread twice. I told my neighbor he was in over his head, that you could see in moments like that who really understood policy. I didn't know he'd come directly from a family medical emergency and hadn't slept in thirty hours. I criticized his competence based on one performance under circumstances I knew nothing about.

[Consumer] The cashier at the grocery store was curt and barely made eye contact. I said something to my wife on the way out about how people in service jobs should be more professional if they want customers to keep coming. My wife said she'd noticed the cashier had a wrist brace and looked like she'd been crying. I hadn't noticed either thing. I'd turned her manner into a statement about her character.

[Technical] Our QA lead had a rough sprint — missed several bugs that made it to staging. I told the engineering director in our one-on-one that she might not be right for the role, that attention to detail at this level isn't something you can train. The director asked if I knew she'd been handling all the load herself while we looked for a second QA person for four months. I hadn't thought about capacity constraints. I'd been assessing her character from an output level that wasn't fully under her control.

[Management] I observed a sales rep on his calls for an afternoon and his close rate was poor. He seemed hesitant, not particularly compelling. I wrote a note to myself about whether he was suited to the role. My sales manager mentioned afterward that those were his first outbound calls and that I'd observed his practice day. I'd been evaluating his underlying ability from a moment of deliberate learning. I should have watched experienced calls and compared.

[Social] My elderly neighbor rarely waves back when I wave from across the street. A few months ago I started assuming she was unfriendly or dismissive. I mentioned it to another neighbor who told me she had macular degeneration and probably couldn't see well enough to recognize me at that distance. I'd been interpreting her visual limitations as a personality verdict for almost a year.

[Educational] One of my students consistently participates less in class discussions than the others. I mentioned to a colleague I thought she lacked confidence in the material. My colleague, who'd taught her the previous semester, said she was one of the more capable students she'd had in years but came from a cultural background where speaking up to challenge professors was seen as disrespectful. I'd read her silence as intellectual caution rather than cultural norm.

[Financial] My company's stock underperformed the index for four consecutive quarters. The board brought in a consultant who suggested the CEO might need to be replaced — that leadership was clearly failing. I agreed when they asked me. It wasn't until the annual industry analysis was presented that I understood that our sector had contracted significantly across all major players and our relative performance was actually stronger than most peers. I'd read four bad quarters as a story about one person.

[Legal] The driver who caused the accident I was in seemed completely calm afterward. No visible distress at all. When we were dealing with the insurance claim, I kept telling my attorney that his composure right after the accident showed he wasn't remorseful. She said composure after an accident is consistent with shock, anxiety disorders, or just a different nervous system response. I'd built my narrative about him from a moment of trauma.

[Social] A colleague from a different department is quiet at team socials and rarely stays long. I've heard a few people describe him as arrogant — like he thinks he's above spending time with the rest of us. I found out recently he's a single parent with pickup and daycare cutoff times he doesn't publicize. What we'd read as aloofness was a hard constraint he'd chosen not to explain to a room full of people he barely knew.

[Family] My adult daughter has been quiet and distracted at family dinners for a few months. I told my wife she seemed disengaged from the family, like she was pulling away. I was starting to feel hurt. I eventually asked her directly and she told me she'd been dealing with a difficult situation at work that she hadn't wanted to burden us with. My interpretation of her behavior had entirely missed what was actually happening.

[Management] I asked a senior employee for a report by Thursday. He delivered it on Friday. I was frustrated and told him he had difficulty meeting deadlines — that I'd noticed a pattern. He came to my office and explained that a critical database had been unavailable for maintenance during the time he'd been planning to run the final queries. He'd sent me an email about it on Wednesday. I'd missed the email. I had been describing a pattern from one incident I hadn't fully understood.

[Political] The candidate stumbled during her first major debate — gave a rambling answer on a housing policy question, lost her thread, looked uncertain. I told my husband she seemed out of her depth. A policy reporter I follow wrote that it had been her first major public event since a family death the previous week, and that her team had recommended she withdraw but she'd insisted on showing up. I'd dismissed her as unqualified from a specific and brutal personal moment.

[Technical] I was reviewing a colleague's code and found some patterns I considered sloppy — inconsistent naming, some redundant logic. I mentioned in the review that he might want to think more carefully about maintainability. He messaged me afterward and said those sections were intentionally structured to match a specific legacy integration spec that he'd linked in the ticket. I'd never looked at the ticket. I'd attributed carelessness to constraints I was unaware of.

[Legal] My neighbor left their dog outside for extended periods last winter. I told people at the block association meeting that they didn't care about the animal, that some people just shouldn't have pets. Another neighbor mentioned that the owner had broken their hip and their adult child had been handling the dog's care remotely for several weeks. I'd constructed a character assessment from a situation I had no context for.

[Family] My teenage son has been getting lower grades this semester and has been spending more time alone in his room. I told my wife he was slacking, that he'd gotten lazy about school since he started spending so much time gaming. When I finally sat down and talked to him, he told me he was being excluded by his friend group and had been struggling with it for months. I had been reading the withdrawal as attitude rather than asking what was happening.

[Educational] A faculty member was late to two consecutive departmental meetings and seemed distracted when she was there. I mentioned to the department chair that she didn't seem fully committed anymore. The chair told me privately that the faculty member's father had died overseas and she was managing an estate remotely across a twelve-hour time difference. I hadn't known about the death. I'd been observing symptoms and inferring causes that had nothing to do with commitment.

[Social] My friend was unusually short with me on the phone last week. Short answers, not much engagement. I called our mutual friend and said I felt like something had shifted between us, that maybe she was growing more distant. Our mutual friend said she'd just had a difficult conversation with her doctor that morning. I'd taken a bad day personally and made it a story about her feelings toward me.

[Consumer] I watched someone leave a restaurant without tipping. I mentioned it to my partner that some people are just cheap, that they benefit from service workers and don't care. My partner suggested there could be many explanations. I said sometimes behavior tells you something about a person. I have no idea if the person had a difficult experience, was a foreign visitor unfamiliar with the tipping convention, or was having a financial crisis. I made a confident character judgment from one observed behavior.

[Management] My director seemed cold and unresponsive during our team all-hands. He gave short answers, didn't engage with the team's energy, left as soon as it was over. I told a peer I thought something had shifted in how he saw the team — that he might be planning some changes. He later told the team at the next meeting that he'd been dealing with a personal crisis the week of the all-hands and apologized for not being present. I'd built organizational anxiety from a bad personal week.

[Political] A city official voted no on a popular community park proposal. I called her out on our neighborhood social media group — said she obviously didn't care about green space or community needs, that it was a disgrace. She posted a response explaining that the project's funding mechanism would have impacted maintenance budgets for three existing parks in underserved areas. I'd made her a villain from one vote without any understanding of her reasoning.

[Financial] A startup founder I know has been slow to pay contractors. I've heard people say he's shady, that he doesn't respect the people who work for him. A mutual friend mentioned he's been waiting on a delayed funding round for seven months and has been personally covering contractor invoices from his own account to bridge the gap. I'd been hearing a character story about someone managing a structural financial crisis.

[Technical] My intern's pull requests were consistently messy in the first two months. I told her in a mid-internship check-in that she needed to take more pride in her work. She looked surprised and said she'd been working entirely off a codebase she'd received on day one with no documentation and no orientation. I'd never set up a proper onboarding. I'd been reading the output of a confusing environment as a reflection of her work ethic.

[Social] Someone in my running club started skipping group runs without explanation. A few of us speculated that she'd gotten too good for the pace and didn't want to hold back. I nodded along with that. She eventually came back and mentioned she'd been dealing with a mental health episode. I'd been part of a group narrative about her character that had nothing to do with what was actually happening.

[Legal] The opposing party's representative took a long time to respond to our discovery requests. I complained in our case notes that they were being obstructionist, typical of the organization. Our case manager mentioned that their in-house counsel had been on medical leave and they were working with reduced legal staff. I'd attributed a structural staffing problem to strategic bad faith.

[Educational] One of my PhD students missed three lab meetings in a row. I told a colleague he was becoming disengaged, that I was reconsidering whether he was right for the program. My colleague asked if I'd talked to him. I hadn't. When I did, he explained that he was supporting his family financially and had taken a second job that conflicted with the meeting times. He'd been afraid to tell me. I'd been making a program-fit judgment about someone managing a very difficult situation he hadn't told me about.

[Management] A team member had a poor performance review cycle. Low ratings on collaboration and responsiveness. I had built an impression that she was difficult to work with. A peer manager told me she'd been dealing with harassment from one of the people who'd given her a low collaboration rating, and she'd been withdrawing from that person to protect herself while she figured out how to raise it. I'd taken the output of a harmful interpersonal situation and turned it into a trait assessment.

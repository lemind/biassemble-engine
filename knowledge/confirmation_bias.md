# Confirmation Bias

## Definition
The tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in a way that confirms or supports one's prior beliefs or values. People discount evidence that contradicts their existing views and overweight evidence that supports them. This bias operates both consciously and unconsciously across all domains of reasoning. It is reinforced by selective exposure to information sources that share one's worldview and by motivated reasoning, where the desired conclusion drives the evaluation of evidence rather than the evidence driving the conclusion.

## Examples
In finance, an investor who believes a stock will rise reads only bullish analyst reports and dismisses bearish ones, even when the bearish case is better supported. They interpret ambiguous earnings data as positive signals.

In medicine, a diagnostician who suspects a particular diagnosis orders tests that would confirm it while skipping tests that might rule it out. A physician convinced a patient has anxiety may attribute physical symptoms to stress rather than ordering imaging.

In politics, a voter who supports a party reads news outlets that align with that party and dismisses opposing outlets as biased, regardless of the quality of individual articles.

In academia, a researcher who has published a theory interprets ambiguous experimental results as supporting their theory and attributes contradictory findings to methodological flaws in the contradicting studies.

[Political] A politician explains that critics are simply afraid of success: "Anyone who disagrees just hasn't looked at the facts — or they have, and they don't want you to know." Every piece of criticism is treated as proof of bad faith rather than as an argument to engage with. Statistics that support the current policy get cited in speeches; statistics that challenge it get dismissed as partisan or distorted. Every press statement features statistics that support the current policy; statistics pointing the other way are never mentioned or are called partisan before the numbers are examined. Disagreement is not a signal to reconsider — it is evidence that the other side doesn't want the truth to come out.

## Indicators
- Reads only the sources, articles, or experts that already agree with the current view
- Explains away contradictory evidence as unreliable, biased, or methodologically flawed rather than engaging with it
- Interprets ambiguous information as support for what is already believed
- Remembers evidence that fits the existing view more clearly than evidence that challenges it
- Asks questions designed to get agreement rather than to find out whether the current view is wrong
- Spends more time building the case for the current position than seriously examining the case against it

## False Positives
- A scientist who forms a hypothesis and then tests it is not exhibiting confirmation bias — the bias requires selectively ignoring contradictory results, not merely starting with a hypothesis
- Updating beliefs incrementally as supporting evidence accumulates is rational Bayesian reasoning, not confirmation bias
- An expert who quickly dismisses implausible claims is exercising domain knowledge, not confirmation bias — not all skepticism is selective
- Preferring well-sourced information over poorly-sourced contradictory information is quality filtering, not bias

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Anchoring Bias, Projection Bias, In-group Bias, Dunning-Kruger Effect

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I've been bullish on this energy company for a year. When an analyst published a bearish report on them last month, I read about a third of it and then closed it. It seemed overly negative, cherry-picking a few quarters of data. Then I found three other analysts who were still positive and read all three of those in full. I feel more confident in my position than I did before the bearish report came out.

[Medical] I'm pretty sure my chronic fatigue is from mold in my apartment building. I've been reading everything I can find about mold illness and it fits perfectly. When my doctor says the building inspection showed normal spore counts, I think she's not familiar with the latest research. I've shared four articles with her. She hasn't engaged with them the way I feel she should.

[Management] I was already skeptical about the merger when the board proposed it. Since then I've been collecting every piece of evidence that supports my concern — the integration failure rates, the cultural mismatch data, the warnings from people who've been through similar deals. My assistant said something positive about the merger last week and I pointed out three things she was missing. I haven't seriously engaged with the business case for it.

[Political] I believe this administration has been bad for working families. When the jobs report came out strong last month, I read several analyses saying the numbers overstate the reality. When manufacturing output was down the month before, I sent that data to everyone I know. I notice I spend more time looking for things that confirm my view than seriously sitting with the evidence that complicates it.

[Educational] I have a theory about why reading scores are declining in my district and it involves screen time. I've been building the case for it in my research proposal. When I came across a large meta-analysis that found the relationship was much weaker than commonly claimed, I spent about twenty minutes on it and then went back to the sources that supported my hypothesis. I wrote "methodologically limited" in my notes about the meta-analysis. I haven't looked at it again.

[Technical] I think our performance problems are caused by a specific bottleneck in the database layer. I've had the database team running diagnostics for two weeks. They've found the layer is performing normally. I've asked them to run more specific tests. Meanwhile the networking team mentioned they saw something unusual in traffic logs but I haven't followed up on that. I want to make sure we've fully exhausted the database angle first.

[Legal] I've become increasingly certain my neighbor violated our property easement. Every time I walk past that fence I notice something else that seems out of place. I've been photographing it for weeks. A surveyor friend looked at my photos and said the encroachment was very minor and probably not actionable. I'm going to get a second opinion. I've been researching property lawyers who specialize in easement disputes. I'm not ready to hear that there's nothing here.

[Social] I think my friend has been pulling away from our group. Every time she doesn't respond quickly to a message, every time she comes late to something, every time she seems distracted — I notice it. Three of us got together last weekend and had a great time. I mainly registered that she left a little early. I'm not sure I'm tracking the whole picture.

[Family] I believe my mother-in-law has always preferred my husband's brother. Every family event I look for moments that confirm it. When she gives the brother attention, I file it. When she's attentive to us, I think about other explanations. My husband says I misread things. I think he's been in this dynamic so long he doesn't see it clearly.

[Consumer] I had a bad experience with this airline three years ago and I've never flown them since. When people praise them, I ask about their experience more carefully and usually find something to question. Last week a colleague said they had the most comfortable long-haul flight of her life on that airline. I told her she got lucky and mentioned a news story from eight months ago about delays on their routes. I don't fly them.

[Financial] I've been saying for two years that this housing market is overvalued. Every new data point I see either confirms it or gets explained away. When prices dropped slightly in Q1 I said "here it comes." When they recovered in Q2, I said it's a dead cat bounce. My sister-in-law bought at the beginning of this year and her house is up 11%. I haven't updated my position. I just keep waiting.

[Management] I decided early that a particular junior team member wasn't going to work out. Since then I notice every mistake he makes and mention it to my HR partner. When he does something well, I move on quickly. My HR partner asked me whether I was tracking his wins as carefully as his misses. I said I was. I don't think I was.

[Medical] I'm worried my son has ADHD. I've been reading about it intensively. Everything I see in him I interpret through that lens. His teacher said he's actually very focused in class and the concerns I raised weren't things she had observed. I thought: well, the classroom structure is more accommodating than home. I asked for a formal evaluation. I may be right. But I'm not sure I've been genuinely open to a different explanation.

[Political] I follow seventeen accounts on social media. Fourteen of them share my political perspective. When I see a headline that challenges my view, I spend a moment looking for what's wrong with the source before I read the article. When I see a headline that confirms my view, I share it without reading past the first paragraph. I think I'm staying informed. I'm not sure I am.

[Technical] We've been convinced for a while that the mobile app's poor retention is caused by our onboarding flow. We've redesigned it twice and retention hasn't moved. My product lead keeps proposing A/B tests of onboarding variations. A user researcher suggested the problem might be the core value proposition. I found her hypothesis vague. I keep investing in onboarding. It feels like the clearest lever.

[Legal] I've been arguing to my client that the clause in question clearly supports our interpretation. He showed me a legal brief from the other side that raised something I hadn't considered. I read it and I think their argument has a flaw. I've been building my counterargument for three days. I haven't genuinely entertained the possibility that their interpretation is correct. I tell my client we're on solid ground.

[Social] My neighbors have been parking in what I consider my part of the shared driveway. I've been documenting it with photos and times. My wife pointed out that we also use more than our share of the shared space sometimes. I said our situation is different. I'm not sure I've thought about that fairly.

[Family] I've been saying for years that my brother's business partner is taking advantage of him. Everything my brother reports back gets filtered through that view. When things go well, I think the partner got lucky or took more credit than he deserved. When things go poorly, I say I told you so. My brother has asked me to stop. I think he's too close to the situation to see it.

[Educational] I believe the traditional essay format constrains student thinking. I've been building a case for portfolio-based assessment in my department. When a peer sent me a study showing traditional essays still predict performance better than alternatives, I read the abstract and noted that it was from 2018. I've been focusing my research on more recent work. I found two papers that support my view. I used those in my proposal.

[Financial] I'm bullish on this small biotech. I got excited about it after a podcast interview with the CEO. Since then I've been in a Reddit forum where most people share my view. When someone posted a skeptical analysis last week, several people jumped on it and found holes in the methodology. I upvoted the critiques. I haven't done my own critical analysis of the bull case I've been following.

[Management] I was skeptical about adopting the new HR software from the start. Now that we're six weeks into the rollout and there are complaints from the team, I feel like my early concerns were validated. My COO pointed out that complaints are normal in any software transition and the completion rates are actually tracking higher than comparable rollouts at similar companies. I haven't fully absorbed that data. The complaints feel more real to me than the benchmark.

[Medical] I've been taking this supplement for three months and I feel better. I'm sure it's working. My wife pointed out that I also started sleeping more regularly three months ago. I said the sleep changes came after I started noticing the supplement's effects. I'm not sure that's accurate — I can't really reconstruct the timeline. But I want to keep taking the supplement.

[Political] I believe the neighborhood council is not properly representing our community's interests. I attend every meeting looking for evidence of this. When the council makes a decision I agree with, I don't think much about it. When they make one I disagree with, I bring it up to neighbors, write about it, raise it at the next meeting. My wife says I seem to go in having already decided. I say I just know how to read what's happening.

[Technical] I formed an opinion early in the codebase review that the previous team had made poor architectural decisions. Every problem I've found since has gone into that mental folder. I've found some good decisions in the codebase too — I noted them, moved on. My manager said I might be presenting the inherited system to new engineers less fairly than the evidence warrants. I said I'm just being honest about what I found.

[Consumer] I bought the expensive blender because I'd already decided premium kitchen equipment was worth it. I asked friends who had the same one if they liked it. They did. I found some one-star reviews saying the motor burned out after a year. I noted that those users might have overloaded it. I haven't treated the positive and negative reviews with the same level of scrutiny. The blender is good and I feel fine about the purchase.

[Legal] I've been building the case that our former contractor violated non-disclosure terms. My partner asked me to look at it from their perspective — what arguments would they make? I started writing out their case and found it stronger than I expected. I haven't revisited my own position since that exercise but I also haven't stopped pushing for litigation. I think I just need to find the right angle on their counterarguments.

[Social] I was warned by a mutual friend that a new acquaintance could be manipulative. I've been watching for it ever since. Every conversation I have with her, I'm cataloguing things that seem off. A few times I've thought she seemed genuinely warm and direct and then I've told myself that could be part of the pattern. I don't know if I'm seeing something real or making evidence fit what I was told to expect.

[Family] I've been convinced for two years that my parents gave my sister more financial support than me over our lifetimes. I keep track of evidence for this — gifts I found out about later, help with her down payment, things my mother mentioned in passing. When my mother helped me with a car repair last year, I thought about it briefly and moved on. I haven't done the accounting honestly. I just feel certain.

[Educational] I have a strong view that standardized testing disadvantages students from lower-income backgrounds. Every study I read on the topic, I evaluate through that lens. Studies that support my view get shared with my department. Studies that complicate the picture get noted as methodologically problematic or as measuring the wrong outcome. My graduate students have started reflecting my view back to me in their papers. I'm not sure we're doing the research justice.

[Financial] I think my employer's stock is undervalued and I've been buying it in my 401k. Every positive development I hear about the company reinforces this view. When a sector analyst published a note last month saying our segment faces structural headwinds, I said he was missing some internal operational improvements I knew about from working here. I haven't seriously engaged with his argument about the structural headwinds. I know this company better than he does.

[Management] I became convinced early in the strategy process that the right move was to double down on our enterprise segment. Since then I've been presenting data to leadership that supports this direction. When a board member asked why we weren't exploring the SMB opportunity more seriously, I had three reasons ready. I hadn't spent serious analytical time on the SMB case. I'd spent it building the enterprise argument.

[Technical] I believe our mobile app crashes are caused by memory management issues in a specific third-party library. I've had the team investigating that library for ten days. They've found it's within normal parameters. I've asked them to look more carefully at edge cases. Meanwhile our QA lead says she sees a pattern in the crashes that points somewhere else. I'll look at her analysis after we close out this investigation. I want to be thorough on the library angle first.

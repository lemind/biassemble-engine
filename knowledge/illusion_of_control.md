# Illusion of Control

## Definition
The tendency to overestimate one's degree of influence over outcomes that are largely or entirely determined by chance. People behave as if they can control or predict random events, particularly when they involve active participation, familiar stimuli, or repeated exposure. The illusion of control was identified by Ellen Langer in 1975 through experiments showing that people valued lottery tickets they had chosen themselves more than equivalent tickets chosen randomly. It leads to excessive trading, risky decisions, and confidence in skills that are actually inapplicable to the outcome.

## Examples
In finance, day traders believe that chart analysis and technical trading patterns allow them to predict short-term price movements that are largely random at that timescale, producing excessive trading activity that destroys returns through transaction costs.

In gambling, casino players blow on dice before rolling, choose their own slot machines, or avoid touching a slot machine that has recently paid out — all behaviors that reflect belief in personal influence over random outcomes.

In sports management, coaches believe their in-game tactical adjustments have precise causal effects on game outcomes, when a large fraction of variance is attributable to random variation in player performance and officiating.

In medicine, patients who choose their own treatment from a menu of equivalent options report higher satisfaction and attribute better outcomes to their agency, even when outcomes do not differ from patients who had treatments assigned.

In technology operations, SREs who developed a monitoring system believe they can predict system failures from pattern observation, when the failures are the result of cascading rare events with genuinely low predictability.

## Indicators
- Performs a ritual, routine, or superstitious behavior believing it will influence an outcome that is actually random
- Takes credit for positive random outcomes as if personal actions caused them
- Believes a random process can be predicted or tilted through careful observation or personal technique
- Prefers to pick a lottery number or draw a card personally rather than have it assigned, believing the choice affects the result
- Feels in control of a situation whose outcomes are driven primarily by chance

## False Positives
- In domains with genuine skill components, confidence in one's ability to influence outcomes is warranted — illusion of control requires that the outcome is predominantly random and the belief in control is demonstrably false
- Perceived control that reduces anxiety and improves performance through effort and persistence (self-efficacy) is beneficial and not illusory when it is channeled toward genuinely controllable inputs
- Statistical process control and systematic monitoring that genuinely improve process stability are real control, not illusion

## Related Biases
Overconfidence Bias, Hot Hand Fallacy, Optimism Bias, Self-serving Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] I always choose my own lottery numbers — specific dates, patterns I've collected over the years. My coworker joined a workplace pool using randomly generated numbers and suggested I join too. I said no. My numbers have meaning. I've thought about them. I know it doesn't change the probability, but choosing randomly feels like giving something up. Something about selecting them myself makes it feel like mine.

[Consumer] I press the elevator button repeatedly even though I know a second press doesn't make it arrive faster. I do it at every elevator and I've never stopped. My son pointed it out once and I laughed. I don't consciously believe it helps. My hand does it anyway. Pressing it more times makes the wait feel more active, like I'm participating in the outcome.

[Technical] I run a specific set of system checks before a critical deploy — checks we've had in the runbook for years. My team knows some of them are redundant given the automated tests we've added. I keep them anyway. The morning I skip one of them something might go wrong and I'll know I skipped it. The ritual has become part of how I manage the anxiety of a deploy. I need to feel like I've done everything.

[Social] Before big conversations I do a very specific preparation routine — certain notes reviewed in a certain order, music, always the same mug of tea. I've done it before every important negotiation and presentation for fifteen years. Does the tea affect anything? No. But on the days I can't do the routine, I feel off. I feel more exposed. The routine gives me a sense that I've done what can be done to influence the outcome.

[Political] I vote in every election, including the ones where I'm aware my single vote is statistically negligible. I understand the math. But I won't skip a vote because not voting feels like relinquishing something. On election day I always watch the results more carefully when I've voted in that race. I feel more connected to the outcome. Like my participation makes me more entitled to observe.

[Medical] I read extensively about my father's condition. I've joined forums, connected with specialists online, tracked his treatment responses carefully in a spreadsheet. My sister says I can't control the disease progression. I know she's right. But researching is how I feel like I'm doing something. Feeling informed makes the helplessness more manageable. I don't tell myself the research is changing his outcome. I just need to feel like I haven't surrendered to it.

[Management] Before every major client presentation, I reorganize my notes in a very specific way — color-coded, sequenced, two printed copies. I've had great presentations when I forgot the second copy and bad ones when everything was perfect. I know the ritual doesn't predict the outcome. But I haven't stopped doing it. The preparation ritual and the quality of the presentation feel linked in a way I can't fully argue against.

[Family] My mother prays every night for our family's safety. My father points out with gentle logic that statistically, prayer's effect on accident rates is not measurable. She doesn't argue with him. She says she'll feel responsible if something happens and she didn't pray that evening. The prayer isn't about outcome probability. It's about making sure she's done her part. The act of doing it is the control.

[Financial] I'm a buy-and-hold index investor by strategy. But I check my portfolio balance every morning. My financial advisor says this accomplishes nothing and increases anxiety. I know she's right. I do it anyway. Looking at the number gives me a feeling of monitoring that feels like stewardship. As if looking at the number is a form of tending to it, even though I'm not acting on what I see.

[Technical] Our on-call rotation has a specific start-of-shift checklist that includes several items that the monitoring systems handle automatically. I've kept the manual items in the checklist even though they duplicate automated checks. The engineers on rotation feel better after completing the manual check. There's something about doing it by hand that makes the system feel more monitored than if we only trusted the automation. The automation works fine. We still do the manual check.

[Educational] I have a particular chair I always sit in during exams. I've moved universities twice and I still find the equivalent chair — end of a row, near a window if possible. I know the chair doesn't affect my performance. My thinking is identical in any seat. But walking in and finding that chair makes me feel prepared in a way that other seats don't. I've sat for exams in worse chairs and done fine. I still look for that position every time.

[Consumer] I always pick my own scratch cards from a stack rather than taking the one the cashier reaches for. I examine the edges, I pick based on some intuition about which one feels right that day. I've tried to explain it and I can't. I know the results are fixed at printing. My choosing process has no mathematical effect on the outcome. But I feel like I would regret letting someone else choose for me and then losing.

[Social] I'm superstitious about talking about good news before it's confirmed. I've been offered a job I want but I won't tell anyone until I have the written offer in hand. Something about saying it out loud feels like it could unmake it. I know this is irrational. Telling my husband that I got an offer doesn't change whether the offer is real. But I don't tell him until the paper comes.

[Legal] I arrange my case files in a very specific order before trial. Different attorneys have different rituals; mine involves a particular sequence for documents and a specific pen I've used on every trial I've won. I've won cases when I forgot the pen and lost cases when I had it. I still bring it. It's part of feeling ready. Part of feeling like I've done everything within my power.

[Family] My husband wears his lucky shirt to every major meeting. He knows the shirt doesn't affect the meeting outcome. He says it's not about luck — it's about feeling like himself. When he wears it and the meeting goes well, the shirt gets credit. When it doesn't go well, he says something else went wrong. The shirt can only win.

[Political] I always watch the election returns with the same group of friends in the same living room with the same snacks in roughly the same seating arrangement. We've had mixed results — our candidates win some and lose some. We still keep the ritual. There's something about doing what you always do in a situation you can't control that makes the lack of control more bearable.

[Technical] I tap the deploy button exactly once. I have superstitions about this in a way I can't fully explain. I've watched colleagues double-click and I've felt briefly uncomfortable. I know the button doesn't care. I know what I'm doing is exactly as effective as a double-click — meaning the action is taken once. The ritual tap is mine.

[Financial] Before big market days I have a morning routine that involves reviewing my positions, reading one specific newsletter, and eating a particular breakfast. On the days I skip parts of it the market goes both ways, exactly as it does on days I complete it. I know this. I've never tested whether my breakfast affects the S&P 500. I've also never stopped believing in the morning before.

[Management] Our team holds the same kick-off meeting format for every new project. We've modified the agenda over the years but the sequence has a ritual quality that I've preserved. A new manager suggested we skip the opening check-in because it takes fifteen minutes. I resisted. That check-in feels important for setting the tone in a way that I believe affects how the project runs. My evidence is that projects with the full kick-off go better. My colleague says that correlation is probably about team cohesion, not ritual. I keep the check-in.

[Medical] Every day before my surgery rotation I lay out my tools in a specific order and pause for a moment before I start. Other surgeons have different routines. I've seen surgeons with no routine at all who are excellent. But I've been doing this since residency and my outcomes have been good and I am not changing the routine. I don't think the order of the tools affects my hands. I do think the moment before I start changes something in me.

[Social] I read my horoscope every morning. I know it's not predictive. When it says I'll have a challenging social interaction, I notice I'm slightly more prepared to encounter one — slightly more patient with ambiguous conversations. When it says good energy, I feel slightly more open. Whether the horoscope causes these or my reading of it changes my morning slightly, I can't say. I keep reading it.

[Consumer] I always put the same foot forward when I step into an athletic competition. Always the right. I developed this in high school track and I haven't stopped. I've beaten and lost to opponents regardless of what foot I stepped in with. The foot doesn't communicate with the outcome. I'm not ready to find out what happens if I change it.

[Technical] My typing has a specific sound in my home office — mechanical keyboard, no headphones, fan running. I've written the best code I've produced in this specific environment. I've written good code in other environments too. But when I'm working on something hard, I want to be in that room, at that desk, with that keyboard. The environment isn't changing the logic of the code. Something about it makes me feel like I'm working at capacity.

[Educational] I always sharpen two pencils before an exam even though I write primarily with a pen and only use a pencil for scratch work. I've been doing this since middle school. I've never used both pencils in a single exam. The second pencil has never been needed. I still can't start an exam without them both being sharp and present on the desk.

[Legal] I arrange the folders on my desk a specific way before client calls. The arrangement has no effect on the call. The client can't see my desk over the phone. I've had calls go badly when the desk was arranged correctly and very well when it was chaotic. I still rearrange the folders before every call. The arranging is for me. It's the act of saying I've done what I can do.

[Family] My father taps the roof of the car every time he parks under something like an overpass or a tunnel. He says it's for good luck. I asked him once what the tap does. He said it probably doesn't do anything. I watched him two minutes later tap the roof when he drove under a parking structure. The tap is not about the car. The tap is about him needing to do something.

[Financial] I ring a specific bell on my desk when I close a trade. I've done it for years. A junior trader asked me if it was a tradition. I said it was just my thing. She started doing it too. Within a month three traders on our floor had their own bells. None of us think the bell changes the trade. But closing trades in silence feels incomplete now.

[Political] Before casting an important vote, I read the bill one more time even if I've already read it three times. I know my reading number four isn't going to reveal something four through three didn't. But reading it again makes me feel like the decision was careful in a way that matters to me. The extra read is for my conscience, not for the vote.

[Technical] Before a critical client demo, I reboot my demo machine and run through every step of the demo once. If anything were going to go wrong it would have gone wrong in the dry run. But even after a clean dry run I sometimes reboot again before we actually start. Two reboots before a demo is clearly more than needed. The second one is about my anxiety, not the system stability.

[Medical] I wear the same scrubs on days I have difficult surgeries. They've been washed hundreds of times. There's nothing special about the fabric. But I feel like myself in them in a way I don't in the others. I've worn other scrubs and performed well. I still choose the familiar pair when the stakes feel high. The scrubs are one more thing I can control in a day where almost nothing is.

[Consumer] I always use self-checkout when I buy lottery tickets. I don't want the cashier to pick which ticket comes out of the roll. I need to press the button myself. I need to choose the denomination, the game, and I need to be the one who initiates the transaction. The ticket is predetermined before I touch the terminal. I know this. I still need my hands to be the ones that make it happen.

[Social] Before an important conversation with my father — the kind where I'm going to say something hard — I walk around the block once. Just once. Not twice. I've been doing this for years. I have no evidence that the walk affects how the conversation goes. It affects how I arrive at the conversation. I feel ready after the walk in a way I don't feel without it. That's probably real. Whether it influences the outcome is a different question.

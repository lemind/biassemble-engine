# Self-Serving Bias

## Definition
The tendency to attribute positive outcomes to one's own character, skill, and effort, while attributing negative outcomes to external factors beyond one's control. Success confirms one's ability; failure is caused by bad luck, unfair circumstances, or other people's actions. The self-serving bias protects self-esteem and maintains a positive self-image, but it prevents accurate self-assessment, inhibits learning from failure, and generates conflict in groups where multiple parties each believe they were right and others were at fault.

## Examples
In finance, traders attribute winning trades to skill and their research process, and attribute losing trades to market manipulation, unexpected news events, or bad luck — even when the same decision framework was applied to both.

In sport, athletes who win credit their preparation and execution. Athletes who lose cite injuries, officiating decisions, weather, or opponent tactics. Studies of post-game interviews show this pattern consistently across team and individual sports.

In education, students who do well on an exam credit their studying and intelligence; students who do poorly blame the exam questions, the professor, or insufficient sleep the night before.

In management, executives attribute company success to their strategic decisions and leadership, and attribute failures to market conditions, regulatory changes, or the performance of their teams — while direct reports attribute outcomes in the opposite direction.

In negotiation, each party believes their position is fair and reasonable, and attributes the failure to reach agreement to the other party's unreasonableness rather than to the incompatibility of positions.

## Indicators
- Takes personal credit for successes while attributing failures to external factors, circumstances, or other people
- Claims a causal role in a good outcome without examining what actually drove it
- Attributes a bad outcome to bad luck, others' mistakes, or difficult conditions rather than to personal decisions
- Cannot accurately reconstruct their own contribution to a failure because memory reshapes it toward self-protection
- Disagrees with someone else's account of a shared event because that version assigns less credit or more blame

## False Positives
- When success genuinely resulted from skill and effort, attributing it to those factors is accurate, not self-serving bias
- When failure genuinely resulted from external factors outside one's control, attributing it to those factors is accurate — not all external attribution is self-serving
- High confidence in one's contribution after consistently strong performance over time is calibrated self-assessment, not bias

## Related Biases
Fundamental Attribution Error, Choice-supportive Bias, Overconfidence Bias, Hindsight Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] My three best trades this year were because I did the research carefully and spotted what others missed. My two losing trades were because of unexpected regulatory news that nobody could have anticipated. I've been telling myself this story all year. My friend who reviews my portfolio pointed out that I'd also missed some signals on the losing trades. I haven't fully engaged with that observation.

[Management] Our product line had a strong year. I've been talking about the strategic decisions I made that drove the results. When my CFO mentioned that a favorable competitive exit in our main market helped significantly, I said of course that mattered but our positioning was ready to capitalize on it. I've been describing a strategic win. It was partly strategic and partly fortunate timing. In my telling, luck isn't part of the story.

[Educational] I got a strong evaluation from my teaching supervisor. I told my partner it validated the approach I've been developing for three years. When the evaluation form came back, the comments were mostly positive but also mentioned that student participation was lower than expected in the second half of the course. I read the participation comment and moved past it. The overall positive rating is what I took from it.

[Social] My friend group has a disagreement about something that happened last summer. I've described the situation to several people outside the group. Everyone I've described it to has said it sounds like I was right. I've started wondering if the fact that everyone I describe it to agrees with me might be because I'm describing it in a way that makes my position sound obviously correct.

[Legal] We won the motion I'd argued for. At the debrief I said my research and framing of the issue had been the deciding factor. My co-counsel said the judge's ruling had cited a procedural point she'd raised in her brief rather than the substantive argument I'd led on. I said the procedural point and the substantive argument worked together. I was the one who had been most visible in the argument. I wanted credit to flow toward my contribution.

[Technical] My code went through and caused no issues for the first three months. I've been using this as evidence that my approach to system design is sound. Last week a failure in that code caused a two-hour outage. I've been focused on the edge case nature of the scenario that triggered it rather than on what the failure says about the original design. The three successful months felt like skill. The failure feels like an unusual scenario.

[Medical] I correctly diagnosed a case that two previous doctors had missed. I've told the story several times as an example of careful clinical reasoning. The part I leave out is that I had access to test results the previous doctors hadn't ordered. I did order those tests. But my diagnosis wasn't pure reasoning — it was reasoning plus information the others didn't have. The story I tell is about my clinical instinct, not about my access to additional data.

[Political] My party lost the election. I've been explaining to my colleagues that we had the right message but the media environment was unfair and voter suppression was a factor. Some of those things are real. I've been less willing to engage with the analysis suggesting our candidate had specific campaign weaknesses. I know those weaknesses exist. Engaging with them feels different from explaining them away with the other factors.

[Consumer] I left a glowing review for a restaurant where I had an excellent meal. I left a one-star review for a restaurant where my meal was fine but the server was slow. My reviews are not representative of my actual distribution of experiences — I review enthusiastically when I'm happy and critically when I'm annoyed. My reviews reflect my feelings more than the restaurants' quality.

[Family] My sibling and I had a financial disagreement about our parents' estate. I've been explaining the dispute to my spouse as a case of my sibling being unreasonable and refusing to see what was fair. My sibling told their spouse a completely different version where I was the unreasonable one. One of us is a more accurate narrator than the other. I'm fairly confident it's me but I don't have a reliable way to check.

[Management] My team missed our quarterly target. I explained it to my director as a consequence of the sales team's delayed pipeline and a competitor move that came out of nowhere. Both of those things were real factors. My director asked how much of the miss was attributable to execution decisions we made. I said we'd been working well given the constraints. I hadn't fully engaged with the question of what our execution had contributed.

[Financial] I made a good call on selling an asset before it declined. When I talk about it I say I saw the warning signs and acted on them. What I don't usually mention is that I sold because I needed liquidity for another purpose and the timing happened to be good. The decision wasn't primarily driven by the analysis I describe. But the outcome looked like the analysis vindicated.

[Technical] A project I led was delayed by three weeks. I've been explaining it to stakeholders as a result of the third-party API documentation being incomplete. The documentation was incomplete. But I'd also underestimated the complexity of the integration work and hadn't built contingency into the schedule. In my account, the external factor is doing most of the explanatory work. My schedule wasn't carrying much of it.

[Educational] My students got good exam results this semester. I've been attributing it to the new teaching methods I introduced. My colleague in the same department, teaching a similar course with different methods, got similar results. The exam improvement might reflect my methods. It might also reflect the cohort being stronger this year or the exam format being more favorable. I haven't examined those alternatives.

[Legal] I lost a motion that I thought was clear. I've been telling people the judge made an error. She might have. But the attorney who reviewed my brief said there was a gap in the argument that an experienced judge would likely notice. I've been emphasizing the judicial error rather than engaging seriously with the gap in my own work.

[Social] I had an argument with a close friend and I told a mutual friend about it. She listened carefully and at the end said "I can see why you were frustrated but I think you might have also said something that landed hard." I said I'd been pretty measured. Looking back at the exchange, I'm not entirely sure I was as measured as I described. The version I told was the version I felt I'd been, not necessarily what happened.

[Management] Our team had a successful quarter after a difficult one. I described the improvement to the CEO as evidence that the changes I'd implemented had taken hold. My operations director mentioned that a market tailwind had also helped our numbers significantly. I acknowledged that in the meeting. In my written update to the board I described the quarter as a result of operational improvements without mentioning the tailwind. The operational improvements were real. The attribution wasn't complete.

[Financial] I left my job to start a business. When the business succeeded, I talked about the vision I had and the risk I took. When I think back honestly, I was also partly pushed out of my job by a restructuring and partly pushed toward the business by a co-founder who really drove it in the early months. The success story I tell has a protagonist who's fully in control of a decision that was partly made for me.

[Technical] A major feature I designed has been well-received by users. I've been presenting it at conferences as a case study in user-centered design. The feature went through three significant redesigns based on user feedback before it became the thing users liked. My early designs got the same feedback: confusing and hard to use. In the conference presentation, I describe the process as iterative design done right. I'm less specific about how wrong my first version was.

[Social] My relationship ended and I've been telling the story to friends. In my version I gave a lot and he didn't meet me where I was. Two friends who know us both have gently suggested the picture was more complicated. I've been resistant to that reframing. It's possible I'm telling a story that has me as the more generous and reasonable party because I need to believe that. I don't know. I do know that two people who care about me have suggested my account is one-sided.

[Management] I hired a person who didn't work out. I've explained it to my leadership team as a case where he misrepresented himself in the interview. He may have. But I also moved quickly through the process and didn't do the reference calls as carefully as I usually do because I was pressed for time. The explanation I've given externally focuses on his behavior. It doesn't include my process shortcut.

[Educational] I published a paper that got good citations. The research had been a collaboration with a graduate student who did most of the data collection. In conference talks I describe the research in first person — "I found," "my analysis showed." The student is credited in the paper. In the conference presentation, her contribution becomes invisible in the way I describe the work.

[Financial] My startup failed. I've been explaining to investors and friends that the market timing was wrong — the category wasn't ready. That's partly true. But a colleague who worked at a competitor during the same period pointed out that the competitor found a segment of the market that was ready. We might have been poorly positioned rather than early. The market timing explanation removes my product and strategic decisions from the failure story.

[Technical] Our system had an outage. The post-mortem report focused on a configuration change that a junior engineer had made. That change triggered the failure. But I had reviewed and approved the change procedure the day before. In the post-mortem I didn't explicitly note my review and approval. The narrative focused on the trigger without noting that I'd been in the causal chain.

[Political] I backed a policy that turned out to be well-received. I've been describing it in my public materials as a case of principled leadership despite initial opposition. What's less in the story is that I had been very uncertain about the policy until polling shifted and then moved to support it publicly. The principled leader story requires me to have been ahead of the polls. I wasn't.

[Legal] I settled a case on terms that were better than I expected. I've been describing it to clients as a result of aggressive negotiation strategy. The settlement was better than expected partly because the other side's key witness became unavailable for reasons unrelated to our strategy. I benefited from their misfortune. The negotiation strategy story is cleaner and more flattering than "we got lucky on a witness."

[Consumer] I left a negative review for a product that I might have used incorrectly. The instructions were unclear and I had a bad experience. The possibility that I misunderstood the instructions is in my review as a one-line caveat. The headline of the review is a product failure. I was frustrated and that's the lens through which the review is written.

[Social] My running group hit our collective goal this year and at the end-of-year celebration I said it was because we held each other accountable and pushed each other to show up. The member who missed the fewest sessions and who had organized the most group runs was sitting next to me. She was the person who had done more than anyone else to create the accountability structure I was describing. I'd described the group's success in a way that distributed the credit across us while she'd done the most of it.

[Family] My children have done well academically and professionally. When people ask what we did right, I talk about our parenting philosophy — the value we placed on education, independence, and resilience. What I mention less is that they were fortunate to grow up in an environment with significant advantages — good schools, stable home, economic security. The parenting philosophy is real. It doesn't fully account for the advantages they started with.

[Management] I took a strong position in a management meeting and my CEO eventually agreed with me. Afterward I described the meeting to my team as an example of standing your ground and making the case. What I didn't describe is that a peer had made a very similar point before me and been dismissed, and that the CEO was already visibly reconsidering before I spoke. I was the last voice before she shifted. I didn't cause the shift as much as I arrived at the shift.

[Technical] I found a bug in a colleague's code during review. I flagged it and it turned out to be significant. I've mentioned this in a few subsequent conversations as an example of careful code review. What I haven't mentioned is that I had also reviewed the same module two weeks earlier and hadn't caught the bug at that pass. I found it on the second review. The first pass hadn't been careful enough to catch it. The story I've been telling starts at the review where I found it.

[Financial] A company I'd invested in was acquired at a premium. I've told the story as one of conviction and patience — I'd held through difficult moments because I believed in the thesis. What I don't include is that I had considered selling three months before the acquisition announcement when the price dipped and I was nervous. I didn't sell because I got busy and forgot to follow up, not because I held from conviction. The acquisition turned my inattention into a story about patience.

# Recency Bias

## Definition
The tendency to weight recent events and information more heavily than older information when making judgments, forecasts, or decisions. The most recent data points feel most relevant, displacing longer-term patterns, historical base rates, and evidence from earlier periods. Recency bias is a specific manifestation of the availability heuristic — recent events are more accessible in memory and therefore feel more representative. It leads to trend-chasing, overreaction to short-term fluctuations, and underweighting of historical distributions.

## Examples
In finance, investors who experience a strong bull market for two to three years project that trend forward and reduce their risk estimates, ignoring historical cycles. After a market crash, the same investors dramatically reduce equity exposure, anchoring on recent losses rather than historical recovery rates.

In sports management, coaches and selectors who overweight recent performance in team selection ignore career performance distributions, dropping in-form players during brief slumps and selecting out-of-form players after a single strong game.

In human resources, performance reviews conducted annually show strong recency bias — employee performance from the last month disproportionately influences the annual rating despite eleven months of prior data.

In weather forecasting interpretation, people who experienced an unusually hot summer overestimate the probability of another hot summer the following year, even when historical data shows it was an anomaly.

In politics, voter approval ratings spike and drop with the most recent news event, overriding slower-moving assessments of policy performance.

## Indicators
- Extrapolates a recent trend into the future without checking whether historical patterns suggest it will continue
- Judges how risky something is based on what has happened in the last few months rather than over the long run
- Rates a person's overall performance primarily on what they did in the most recent period, discounting a longer track record
- Makes an investment or financial decision in direct response to recent price movements rather than long-term value
- Dismisses historical data when recent experience points in a different direction, without examining whether the change is real

## False Positives
- Recent data genuinely may be more relevant in rapidly changing environments — updating on new information is rational when the data-generating process has changed
- A doctor who updates a treatment plan based on a patient's recent test results is not exhibiting recency bias — they are responding to new evidence
- Weighting recent information more in explicitly non-stationary systems (e.g., a company undergoing rapid transformation) is appropriate model calibration

## Related Biases
Availability Heuristic, Anchoring Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Overconfidence Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] The market has been up for eighteen months and I've gradually moved more of my portfolio into equities. Risk feels low right now. I'm not selling anything because everything is going well. My financial planner sent me a rebalancing recommendation and I told her the current allocation feels right to me. It feels right because the recent period has been good. I haven't thought carefully about the historical distribution.

[Management] My best salesperson has closed three deals in a row and I'm treating him as my top performer in every planning conversation. I've been assigning him the highest-value prospects. My VP of sales pointed out that his year-to-date close rate is actually in the middle of the team — the three-deal streak is recent but the full-year data is more informative. I've been responding to the streak, not the track record.

[Technical] The new framework I used last month worked beautifully on a straightforward project. I'm now advocating for using it for a complex, high-stakes project. My team lead asked me to do a more systematic comparison against our established tools. I said the new framework had clearly demonstrated what it could do. I'd seen it on one project. My positive experience was fresh and vivid but it wasn't a complete picture of the tool's performance range.

[Educational] I graded this student's final paper and it was excellent — clear argument, strong evidence, well-organized. My overall mark for the semester was heavily influenced by that final paper. My rubric says the final paper is 40% of the grade. But my subjective sense of the student's performance was weighed toward what I'd read most recently. Earlier papers that were weaker got less weight in my impression than they should have.

[Political] The administration had two very visible policy successes last quarter. In every polling question about their overall effectiveness, I find myself thinking of those two successes first. My colleague reminded me that the prior two years had significant policy failures that are in the record. The recent successes haven't undone the earlier record. But they've become the data my mind reaches for when evaluating effectiveness.

[Medical] My last three experiences with this hospital system have been frustrating — long waits, billing errors, one communication failure with a specialist. I'm now considering switching systems for my family's care even though my experience over the prior decade was very positive. The three frustrating recent experiences are more present to me than the decade of good care. I'm letting last quarter change a long-term assessment.

[Consumer] I've been buying this brand of running shoes for eight years. Last year's model was uncomfortable and I returned it. Now I'm looking at other brands even though one bad model in eight years is not much evidence that the brand has changed. The most recent experience is dominating my evaluation. I'm shopping like someone who has only had one experience with this brand, not eight years of experience with mostly one bad year.

[Management] At the annual performance review, my assessment of each team member was heavily influenced by what I'd seen in the last two months. My HR partner kept asking me to look at the whole year. I said I was considering the whole year but when I reviewed my notes, most of the specific examples I cited in reviews were from the last quarter. Eleven months of prior data were being summarized by what was most recent and most accessible.

[Financial] I put extra money into emerging markets six months ago when returns there had been strong for several quarters. My advisor had been recommending emerging markets for years based on long-term valuation analysis. I hadn't done it until the recent returns made it feel obviously right. I was buying because prices had recently gone up, which is not the reason my advisor had been recommending the allocation.

[Technical] We adopted a new DevOps practice after it worked exceptionally well at our last company. The practice had been effective there. But the last company had a specific team structure and codebase architecture that made the practice fit naturally. I'm carrying the recent positive experience into a different context without examining whether the conditions are the same. My last experience is driving my recommendation for the current context.

[Social] My closest friend and I had a difficult few months where communication was strained and we had two arguments. I've been telling my partner that the friendship has fundamentally changed. She asked how the friendship had been before those two months. I said it had been good for years. She suggested two months might not indicate a fundamental change in a years-long friendship. The recent period was prominent in my assessment but it wasn't representative.

[Family] My mother called me every day for a week during a period when she was anxious about a health test. I started feeling that her calling frequency had permanently increased and becoming frustrated. Then her test came back fine and she returned to her normal calling pattern. I'd taken one week's pattern and been building an assessment of a trend. The week was uncharacteristic. I was responding to it as if it were characteristic.

[Educational] I've been reading a lot of papers on one particular theoretical approach lately because it's come up in several recent seminars. I'm now recommending it to my students as a cutting-edge perspective. My colleague mentioned that this approach had its high point about ten years ago and more recent literature has moved in different directions. My sense of "current" was based on what I'd been reading recently, not on what was actually current in the field.

[Financial] Three of my angel investments have shown traction in the past six months. I've been feeling very confident about my investment judgment and I've agreed to make three new investments in the coming month. My co-investor asked me whether my judgment had improved or whether six months of a favorable market environment had lifted several companies simultaneously. I didn't have a clear answer. My confidence was based on recent outcomes.

[Management] Our customer satisfaction scores declined for two consecutive months. I responded by initiating a customer success overhaul that involves significant changes to our entire approach. My director asked if I'd looked at the two months in the context of the prior year. The prior year showed consistent strong scores. She said two months might be noise or seasonal variation. I was treating two months of decline as a trend.

[Technical] Our last deployment cycle was clean — no incidents, no rollbacks, everything went smoothly. I went into this sprint planning without adding the usual buffer time for deployment risk. I said we were in a good rhythm. My ops lead said she wanted to keep the standard buffer regardless of recent deployment history. I said things had been going well. She said recent clean deployments don't reduce the probability of incidents in the next cycle.

[Consumer] My neighborhood has been very quiet for the past three months — no incidents, peaceful evenings, I've started leaving my car unlocked again. My neighbor mentioned a car break-in down the street last month. He said these things come in cycles and this might be a lull. I've been making security decisions based on recent quiet. The three-month quiet isn't a permanent condition; it's a recent period.

[Political] The party performed well in the last two special elections. Party strategists have been adjusting their national message to match what worked in those two races. A longtime political analyst said special elections in unrepresentative districts might not signal what will work broadly in the general. The strategists are optimizing for recent outcomes rather than asking whether those outcomes generalize.

[Financial] My real estate portfolio has appreciated significantly in the past two years. I've been telling my children that real estate is the safest investment. I've owned properties for twenty-five years. There were years when values declined significantly and some of those years were very difficult. The recent appreciation is making me describe the asset class as if it's low-risk. The full twenty-five year experience is more complex than the recent two-year period.

[Management] Our team has missed two deadlines in a row and I've been speaking to my director about the team's reliability. She asked me to pull the data for the full year. For the full year, we've hit eight deadlines and missed two. She said that was a strong performance record. I'd been building my narrative from the most recent two months and proposing process changes based on them. The two missed deadlines were a more accurate picture of recent performance, not overall reliability.

[Technical] We had a serious security incident two months ago. Since then I've been treating every security decision with extreme caution, adding approval layers, running extra audits, pushing back the timeline on anything with security implications. My security lead said the incident had been addressed and our posture was sound. I haven't been able to recalibrate. The incident is very present and it's calibrating my current risk sense beyond what the current threat level probably warrants.

[Educational] I had several excellent students in a row this academic year. I've been describing the cohort to colleagues as unusually strong. One colleague asked how this cohort compared to the one three years ago that I'd also praised. I said this one was stronger. She pointed out I'd described the three-years-ago cohort in exactly the same terms at the time. I might just be influenced by the recency of my current students.

[Social] My partner and I have had a great month — several romantic evenings, good communication, a trip we both loved. I've been telling people our relationship is in a better place than it's been in years. My therapist asked how we'd been six months ago. I said it had been fine. She said a great month after a fine stretch is a good sign but it might not indicate a permanent shift. I was letting one excellent month define a long relationship's trajectory.

[Financial] I've shifted away from value investing toward growth investing because growth has outperformed for the past five years. My original strategy was built around a longer historical pattern where value periodically outperforms. I've been treating five years of growth outperformance as evidence that the historical alternation is no longer happening. Five years is not a short period but it's not the full historical record either.

[Management] My team's new hire has impressed me in his first six weeks. He's been proactive, delivered quality work, and integrated well. I've been describing him to peers as one of the best hires we've made. My HR partner asked me to wait for at least a quarter before making that judgment. She said early performance is often high because new employees are on good behavior and haven't yet encountered the kinds of pressures that test long-term performance. I'm responding to six weeks.

[Technical] The system has been running well since we upgraded the infrastructure eight months ago. I've been using this as evidence that the upgrade was worth the cost and complexity. My CTO asked me to look at the prior year's uptime data before the upgrade. It was also very good. The recent strong performance might not be attributable to the upgrade at all. I was using the most recent period to validate a decision rather than doing a fair comparison.

[Consumer] I switched to a new coffee shop after my regular one had three bad experiences in one month — slow service, wrong orders, a rude interaction. I've been going to the new one for two months and it's been fine. My wife asked if I was sure about giving up the old place based on one bad month after years of good service. I said things had clearly gone downhill. One bad month in years of service might not be a trend. I responded to it as if it were.

[Legal] I've been pessimistic about this case since we lost two consecutive motions. My co-counsel thinks the overall case is still strong. I've been saying we should settle. She wants to assess whether the losses indicate a fundamental weakness or just two unfavorable motion outcomes. I've been letting two losses weight my whole case assessment more than two data points should.

[Family] I was patient with my children every day last week — I felt proud of my parenting that week. I've been describing myself to my spouse as having made a significant improvement in my patience. She gently asked what the week before had been like. I said it had been harder. She said one good week might not indicate a new pattern. I wanted the recent week to mean something lasting. She was asking me to be more careful about what one week shows.

[Financial] I've been working with a financial advisor for four years. The last year has been below benchmark. I'm considering switching advisors. My accountant asked me how the prior three years had performed. Above benchmark. He said the full four-year record was positive and one underperforming year might be market conditions or sector rotation. I've been evaluating four years of performance from the perspective of one recent year.

[Technical] Our user acquisition metrics have been excellent for the past three months. I approved an increase in paid acquisition spend based on these metrics. My data analyst said three months of strong metrics in a growth phase wasn't enough to confidently project forward — growth curves in our category typically have periods of acceleration followed by softening. I had been extrapolating the recent trend in a straight line.

[Political] I changed my opinion of a political leader after a series of recent positive news stories. My previous assessment had been formed over years of observations. My partner asked whether I was updating on the leader's overall record or on the past three months. I said the recent period showed a real change. He said three months of positive coverage following years of mixed performance should produce a smaller update than I was making.

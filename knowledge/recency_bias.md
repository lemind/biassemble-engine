# Recency Bias

## Definition
The tendency to weight recent events and information more heavily than older information when making judgments, forecasts, or decisions. The most recent data points feel most relevant, displacing longer-term patterns, historical base rates, and evidence from earlier periods. Recency bias is a specific manifestation of the availability heuristic — recent events are more accessible in memory and therefore feel more representative. It leads to trend-chasing, overreaction to short-term fluctuations, and underweighting of historical distributions.

## Examples
In finance, investors who experience a strong bull market for two to three years project that trend forward and reduce their risk estimates, ignoring historical cycles. After a market crash, the same investors dramatically reduce equity exposure, anchoring on recent losses rather than historical recovery rates.

In sports management, coaches and selectors who overweight recent performance in team selection ignore career performance distributions, dropping in-form players during brief slumps and selecting out-of-form players after a single strong game.

In human resources, performance reviews conducted annually show strong recency bias — employee performance from the last month disproportionately influences the annual rating despite eleven months of prior data.

In weather forecasting interpretation, people who experienced an unusually hot summer overestimate the probability of another hot summer the following year, even when historical data shows it was an anomaly.

In politics, voter approval ratings spike and drop with the most recent news event, overriding slower-moving assessments of policy performance.

## Indicators
- Forecasts that extrapolate recent trends without regression to historical means
- Risk assessments that track recent events rather than long-term distributions
- Performance evaluations dominated by last-period outcomes
- Investment decisions made primarily in response to recent price movements
- Neglecting historical base rates when recent experience contradicts them

## False Positives
- Recent data genuinely may be more relevant in rapidly changing environments — updating on new information is rational when the data-generating process has changed
- A doctor who updates a treatment plan based on a patient's recent test results is not exhibiting recency bias — they are responding to new evidence
- Weighting recent information more in explicitly non-stationary systems (e.g., a company undergoing rapid transformation) is appropriate model calibration

# Base Rate Neglect

## Definition
The tendency to ignore or underweight statistical base rate information (prior probabilities, population frequencies) in favor of specific descriptive information about an individual case. When case-specific details are vivid and narrative, people use them to override or ignore the prior probability that should anchor any probabilistic assessment. Base rate neglect is central to diagnostic reasoning failures in medicine, risk assessment errors in law, and miscalibrated confidence in forecasting. It was identified by Kahneman and Tversky as a core feature of the representativeness heuristic.

## Examples
In medicine, the "base rate of disease" failure: a positive result on a test with 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity for a disease with 1% prevalence is still more likely a false positive than a true positive (positive predictive value ≈ 16%), but physicians and patients routinely assume a positive result means the patient has the disease.

In law, jurors presented with a compelling eyewitness account assign very high guilt probabilities, ignoring that eyewitness testimony is among the least reliable forms of evidence with known false identification rates of 25–40%.

In finance, analysts building earnings models for a company focus heavily on firm-specific factors while ignoring base rates: most firms that grow at 30% per year for three years revert toward industry average growth, regardless of story quality.

In hiring, an interviewer's vivid impression of a candidate dominates the actuarial base rate for interview-to-performance correlation, which is typically modest. Structured interviews have higher predictive validity than the unstructured impressions they replace.

In risk assessment, security analysts briefed on a specific threat scenario overestimate its probability, underweighting the base rate of that threat type occurring in similar geopolitical contexts.

## Indicators
- Treats a vivid, detailed scenario as highly likely without asking how often situations like that actually occur
- Hears a positive test result and concludes the diagnosis is nearly certain, without factoring in how rare the condition is
- Accepts eyewitness identification as near-definitive without considering how often eyewitnesses are wrong
- Makes a confident forecast without consulting how often comparable forecasts in similar situations have been right
- Hears a confident, plausible-sounding story and raises the probability estimate without asking what the actual base rate is

## False Positives
- When base rates are genuinely unavailable for a novel phenomenon, relying on case-specific information is the only available evidence
- Updating appropriately from base rates using genuine case-specific evidence (Bayesian inference) is correct — the neglect only occurs when base rates are discounted rather than properly combined with case evidence
- Expert judgment in domains with well-calibrated intuition can perform close to base-rate-anchored inference without explicit calculation

## Related Biases
Representativeness Heuristic, Availability Heuristic, Overconfidence Bias, Confirmation Bias

## Story Patterns

[Medical] The test came back positive. My doctor said the test is 97% accurate. I've been in a complete spiral for two weeks assuming I definitely have this condition. My doctor tried to explain something about how rare the disease is and false positives but I was so upset I couldn't really absorb it. A positive result feels like it means you have it. That's what 97% accurate sounds like to me.

[Legal] The eyewitness pointed at my client and said without any hesitation: that's the man. She was confident, specific, recalled specific details. I'm watching the jury and they're clearly moved. I've tried to tell them that eyewitness identification has serious reliability problems. But I can see it on their faces — she looked right at him. That confidence feels like proof. No amount of general statistics about eyewitness error is landing against that specific certainty.

[Financial] The analyst gave a very detailed, compelling account of why this company is about to break out. He had proprietary data, management interviews, specific product pipeline analysis. It was a forty-page report. I bought a large position. Later I found out that the analyst had a strong track record of compelling writeups that frequently didn't pan out. I should have asked how his picks had actually performed. The detail of the analysis made me feel like the probability was high without ever checking the base rate.

[Management] My candidate for the senior role had a remarkable interview — specific examples, thoughtful questions, excellent communication. I've been telling the team she's clearly the best we've seen in years. My HR partner quietly asked if I'd reviewed the structured interview scoring rubrics we use because they even out some of those impressions. I said I trusted my read. I hadn't thought about how often a great interview actually predicts great job performance.

[Technical] Our load test looked great. All the key scenarios passed, response times were well within spec, and the engineers who built the system were confident. We launched. Within the first week we had significant performance problems. I kept saying: but the load test passed. The team pointed out that load tests model anticipated scenarios and production traffic is always different. I'd been so focused on the test results that I hadn't asked what fraction of launches with these results actually have smooth production rollouts.

[Educational] My doctoral student's dissertation proposal was beautifully written, theoretically rich, and showed real intellectual maturity. I told him I thought he'd sail through the defense. He struggled significantly — the committee found substantial gaps in his methodology. I'd been responding to the quality of his writing and thinking without asking myself how often a strong proposal predicts a clean defense. Those are actually somewhat separate skills.

[Consumer] The mechanic showed me the part that had failed. He explained what had happened. He had a confident diagnosis. I authorized the $1,100 repair. The problem came back two weeks later. He had a confident explanation for that too. At no point had I asked myself: what's the track record of this specific diagnosis predicting that this is the actual problem? I was responding to his certainty and the visual of the broken part, not to any sense of diagnostic accuracy.

[Political] I heard a very detailed story from a local business owner about how the new regulation was destroying his livelihood. It was specific, it was emotional, it was credible. I became vocal in opposing the regulation. Later I found out the regulation had been in effect in five other states for several years and the economic impact studies showed negligible effects on small business overall. I'd let one vivid story stand in for data I never looked at.

[Social] My friend told me in great detail about a scam someone in her city had fallen for — sophisticated, convincing, targeted elderly people. I've been calling my parents weekly to warn them and have sent them articles. My wife says I'm creating anxiety without giving them information that changes their actual risk. I've never looked at how common this type of scam is in our parents' area specifically. The story was so detailed and disturbing that it made the risk feel very high and very close.

[Family] My son had a frightening fever spike at six months — it came out of nowhere, very high, very fast. We rushed to the emergency room. He was fine. But I've been anxious about fevers ever since in a way that our pediatrician finds excessive. She's told me that simple fever spikes in healthy infants are common and rarely indicate serious underlying conditions. When I'm in the moment, the one time I was scared is all I can think about.

[Financial] My investment club analyzed a private company and we were all very impressed — innovative product, charismatic founder, strong early revenue. We wrote a check. I hadn't asked what fraction of deals at this stage that match this profile actually return capital. I'd just been responding to how good this specific company looked. Later I learned that most deals that look exactly like that one don't return capital. The profile isn't rare — the outcomes are often poor regardless.

[Management] I gave the account to a candidate who'd had brilliant references. Two former managers called unprompted just to praise him. His portfolio was exceptional. His take-home assignment was the best we'd seen. He didn't work out — poor follow-through, difficult to manage. At no point in the process had I asked myself: what fraction of candidates who look this strong in this process actually perform at a high level? I'd been captured by the specific picture.

[Medical] My mother has all the symptoms listed for a condition I read about online. I'm absolutely certain she has it. I called her doctor's office and told them what I thought. The symptoms are common to many conditions but in her case they seemed to line up so well. The doctor ordered the tests I was sure would confirm it. They came back negative. I hadn't thought about how common those symptoms are or how many different things they indicate.

[Legal] The contract was loaded with very specific provisions about what would happen in various scenarios. It felt comprehensive and carefully thought through. I signed it without asking my lawyer to review it. The other side clearly knew the business well and had anticipated many scenarios. I assumed that level of detail meant the contract was well-balanced. The detail had been crafted in their favor. Specificity isn't the same as fairness.

[Consumer] I bought the skincare product because the testimonials were so specific and similar — people describing the exact changes I wanted to see, using precise language about what changed and when. I didn't look up how often these products actually show measurable results in controlled studies. The testimonials felt like evidence. I had thirty vivid cases. I was not thinking about how many people bought this product and didn't post testimonials.

[Technical] The penetration test found five significant vulnerabilities and two critical ones. The report was detailed and the findings were credible. I triaged everything immediately. Later our security advisor mentioned that we should also look at what attacks we hadn't been tested for — the penetration test tests known attack patterns and our actual attack surface is larger. I'd treated the specifics of what was found as a complete picture of our risk.

[Political] The think tank released a report with detailed economic modeling showing the proposed tax policy would cost the middle class significantly. The modeling used specific numbers, showed specific scenarios, had charts. I distributed it widely. A colleague in economics pointed out that detailed-looking economic models in policy documents routinely have assumptions baked in that drive the conclusions, and I should look at what the major forecasting bodies were saying about the actual distributional effects. The detail in the report had made it feel authoritative.

[Social] A mutual friend described one member of our group in very specific terms — particular things she'd done, specific quotes. The description was unflattering. I adjusted my impression of this person significantly based on that account. It was just one description, from one perspective. I don't know how often detailed second-hand accounts reflect the person accurately versus reflect the relationship between the describer and the described. I took specificity as evidence.

[Family] My teenager applied to a competitive program and wrote an excellent application essay. I told him I thought he had a very strong chance. I was moved by the essay. I hadn't asked the counselor what the acceptance rate was among applicants with his profile. The essay felt so good that probability didn't come up in my thinking. He wasn't accepted. I should have found out the base rate before I set expectations.

[Educational] A student came to my office hours with a very specific and credible account of why she'd missed the deadline — family emergency, documentation, detailed timeline. I granted the extension. Later I found out this was the third time she'd presented a specific account for a deadline exception in the same semester. I'd been responding to each account individually without asking how often I extend deadlines for this student or what the general pattern was.

[Financial] The real estate agent showed us a comparable sale two blocks away that had closed at a price justifying our asking price. One comparable. It was specific — same floor count, similar square footage, renovated kitchen. I felt reassured. My wife asked how many other comparables were in the neighborhood and what the average showed. We hadn't asked that. We'd been satisfied by one compelling specific data point.

[Management] My most vocal employee during the engagement survey was very articulate about specific problems with our culture — examples, dates, colleagues affected. His account was detailed and compelling. I launched a culture initiative. My HR team later ran the numbers on the full survey and his concerns weren't reflected in the majority responses. I'd let the most vivid and specific account shape my picture of the whole organization.

[Medical] The researcher presented a case study of a patient who had remarkable recovery on the new protocol. The case was documented in exceptional detail — lab values, timeline, symptom progression, patient quotes. The room was moved. I went home thinking this protocol must be effective. I didn't ask about the sample size, the comparison group, or the dropout rate. The case study felt like evidence in a way that an aggregate table probably wouldn't have.

[Legal] My client described exactly what she remembered: the car came from nowhere, no signal, she had no time to react. She was specific about the weather, the exact location, the other car's color. That specificity felt like credibility. I pushed for a liability finding. The accident reconstruction expert disagreed significantly with her reconstruction and had measurements to show why. The vividness of her account wasn't the same as accuracy, but in my mind they had felt connected.

[Consumer] The restaurant had 4.8 stars on five thousand reviews. I assumed it was excellent. I'd looked at a handful of the most recent reviews and they were all glowing and specific. I had a mediocre meal. Later I read more carefully and saw that a meaningful fraction of one-star reviews described the same consistent problem I'd experienced. My sample of recent reviews had been biased toward the positive ones the algorithm surfaced. I'd taken a high average as evidence without understanding the distribution.

[Technical] The A/B test showed that variant B performed significantly better. The p-value was 0.03. I shipped variant B. My data analyst came back the next week and mentioned that we hadn't discussed the false discovery rate — we'd run fourteen A/B tests that week and a p-value of 0.03 means roughly one in thirty will look significant by chance. Variant B could be noise. I'd treated the one finding as definitive without asking how often tests like this produce false positives.

[Political] The public hearing featured three residents who gave compelling testimony against the proposed development. Each of them spoke specifically about impacts on their street, their property value, their neighborhood's character. The council chamber felt the weight of their testimony. The developer's attorney mentioned that a thousand residents had submitted written support and the council members didn't react to that the same way. Three voices with stories beat a thousand names on a sheet of paper.

[Social] My new colleague seemed intensely interested in my field — he asked specific questions, used appropriate terminology, remembered details from previous conversations. I told my manager he was exceptional and recommended him for a complex project early in his tenure. He struggled significantly. I hadn't asked how often this pattern of early interest and facility with language predicts actual capability in execution. I'd read the signals and formed a firm probability from them.

[Family] My grandmother was extremely healthy her whole life and lived to 97. My grandfather on the same side also lived into his late nineties. I've told myself I probably have good genes and worry less about preventive health than my friends do. My doctor has pointed out that individual family examples don't reliably indicate my genetic profile and I should treat my own risk factors on their merits. I've been using two vivid cases as if they were population statistics.

[Educational] The scholarship committee awarded the prize to the applicant with the most moving personal essay about overcoming adversity. Her essay was vivid and her story was genuine. The other finalist had stronger academic metrics and more rigorous research. The committee felt the essay applicant showed greater promise. I was on the committee. We'd allowed the emotional specificity of one application to displace the base rate question: which profile actually predicts success in this program?

[Financial] My startup's first twenty customers were enthusiastic, high-quality businesses — exactly the profile we'd targeted. I went into our fundraising round very confident, projecting strong growth. The lead investor asked me what fraction of companies with our profile at this stage reach series A. I didn't know. I'd been extrapolating from twenty enthusiastic customers without ever researching how common those customers' behavior is among businesses like ours at this stage.

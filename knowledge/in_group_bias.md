# In-group Bias

## Definition
The tendency to favor members of one's own group over members of out-groups, and to evaluate in-group members and their actions more positively than equivalent out-group behavior. In-group bias operates across all group categories — nationality, ethnicity, organization, sports team, political affiliation, and even minimal artificial groups created for experimental purposes. People attribute positive behaviors of in-group members to character and positive behaviors of out-group members to luck or circumstance, while applying the reverse logic to negative behaviors. The bias supports group cohesion but distorts evaluation and decision-making.

## Examples
In hiring, managers consistently rate candidates who share their university, hometown, or hobbies higher on unrelated dimensions like competence and trustworthiness, even when credentials are otherwise identical.

In finance, home country bias leads investors to overweight domestic equities in their portfolios relative to what diversification theory recommends, perceiving domestic companies as less risky due to familiarity.

In politics, citizens evaluate identical policies more favorably when attributed to their own party's candidate than to the opposing party's candidate, without independent assessment of the policy merits.

In sports journalism, referees unconsciously grant marginally more favorable calls to home teams due to crowd pressure, producing the well-documented home advantage effect across professional sports.

In international relations, nations attribute their own military operations as defensive and proportionate while characterizing equivalent operations by adversaries as aggressive and disproportionate.

## Indicators
- Trusts, rates, or likes in-group members more than equally qualified outsiders without examining the specific evidence
- Applies different standards to the same behavior depending on whether it comes from a group member or an outsider
- Explains a group member's failure through difficult circumstances while explaining an outsider's equivalent failure through character
- Holds back criticism of a group member that would be freely expressed about an outsider doing the same thing
- Favors group members when allocating resources, assigning high-profile work, or making hiring and promotion decisions without a performance basis

## False Positives
- Having more information about in-group members and therefore greater trust is calibrated epistemic behavior when the information is genuine and relevant
- Building strong team cohesion and loyalty is organizationally valuable — not all in-group preference is bias
- Cultural familiarity and shared context can genuinely reduce coordination costs in some team settings

## Related Biases
Confirmation Bias, Halo Effect, Stereotyping Bias, Bandwagon Effect, Fundamental Attribution Error

## Story Patterns

[Management] Two candidates made it to the final interview for the team lead role. One went to my alma mater — we talked about professors we'd both had. The other had stronger metrics from her previous role. I hired the first one. I told my team it was about culture fit. Looking back, I'm not sure I separated the school connection from the culture assessment as cleanly as I should have.

[Financial] I invest almost entirely in companies I recognize from living in this country. International markets make sense in theory but I always hesitate — I don't understand the companies, the regulations, the risks. My domestic equities don't feel as uncertain to me even when they should. My financial planner has shown me the diversification math three times. I keep overweighting home market.

[Legal] Both parties in this arbitration are from different countries. I've been trying to be neutral but when the domestic party presents evidence, I find it intuitive and easy to follow. When the foreign party's counsel argues, I find myself questioning the framing more. I've been monitoring this in myself and I'm not proud of what I notice. Same evidence standard. Different instinctive response.

[Social] My city has two crosstown rivals in the local basketball league. I've been to both teams' games. At home games I give our team every benefit of the doubt on contested calls. At away games I think the officials are much more accurate. I can't fully explain why the same refereeing quality looks so different depending on which bench I'm sitting closer to.

[Technical] We were evaluating two vendors for a critical infrastructure component. One of the vendors was a startup founded by two engineers from our company who had left a few years ago. Our team gave them considerable benefit of the doubt in areas where both vendors had uncertainty. We ended up choosing them. The ex-colleague connection gave them trust credit that a genuinely unknown vendor wouldn't have started with.

[Educational] I'm on the admissions committee and we're comparing applicants from a program I attended versus programs I don't know. I review the known program's applicants quickly and comfortably. With the unknown programs, I spend more time verifying credentials and asking for more documentation. I tell myself I'm being thorough. I think I'm being less trusting of the unfamiliar.

[Political] I read a policy proposal from a politician in my party and I thought it was thoughtful and worth debating on its merits. I saw an identical policy proposed by someone from the opposing party last year and I immediately found three objections. My partner asked me once whether I read policies or read the name attached to them. I told him I read both. He seemed skeptical. So am I, if I'm honest.

[Family] My mother-in-law sometimes gives advice that is identical to advice my mother gives. When my mother says it, I feel understood. When my mother-in-law says the same thing, I feel interfered with. I've been aware of this for years. I haven't fully addressed it. The relationship difference is changing the meaning of the same words.

[Management] We have contractors and full-time employees on our team. When a contractor makes a mistake, I document it and factor it into renewal decisions. When a full-time employee makes a comparable mistake, I discuss it privately and give them more runway. I tell myself it's about the nature of each relationship. I think part of it is that employees feel like my people in a way contractors don't.

[Consumer] I bought my coffee maker from a company in my home country even though an identically-reviewed model from an overseas manufacturer was $40 cheaper. I told myself there were quality assurance reasons to prefer the domestic brand. I couldn't actually describe what those reasons were when my friend asked me. The domestic label had done something that I dressed up as reasoning afterward.

[Social] My neighborhood has a community board and we've had a recurring conflict with the neighboring district about a shared park. At every meeting I find myself presenting our district's position as the reasonable, community-oriented one and theirs as territorial and narrow. A mutual city council member who doesn't live in either district told me she thinks both sides are being equally territorial. I found that hard to hear. I still think we're different.

[Technical] Our team did a code review exchange with another department's engineering team. When they reviewed our code, I went into their feedback looking for where they'd misunderstood our architecture. When we reviewed their code, I found more issues more quickly. I don't think our code is objectively better. I think I was a less careful reader of my own team's work.

[Educational] I chair a peer review committee for internal grants. Applications from my own department get more careful readings from me than applications from other departments. I'm more fluent in the methodology of my field, which might justify this. I'm also more charitable in ways that go beyond methodological fluency. I've been noticing this and trying to counterbalance it. I'm not fully succeeding.

[Political] After a political controversy, both parties' representatives gave explanations of their party's position. I thought my party's representative was measured and thoughtful. I thought the other party's representative was evasive. My husband, who follows politics from a different angle, said he thought both were equally evasive and equally clear on different points. I've been sitting with that assessment for a few weeks.

[Management] Two senior employees requested emergency leave this quarter for personal reasons. One is on my core leadership team and has been with us for eight years. The other is newer and in a support function. I granted the first one immediately. I asked more questions of the second one before approving. Same policy, same category of request. I didn't handle them the same way.

[Legal] I represented a client in a contract dispute where the opposing party was a large corporation. I genuinely believed in my client's case. I've been in cases since then where I represent corporations against individuals. I notice my narrative empathy is different. I construct the opposing individual's motivations less charitably. I try to check this. Some days I'm more successful than others.

[Social] I've lived in this city for fifteen years and I have real pride in its identity. When someone who just moved here criticizes something about the city, I get defensive and skeptical of their perspective. When a long-term resident raises the same criticism, I engage with it as legitimate local knowledge. The observation hasn't changed. The speaker's tenure in the city has changed how I receive it.

[Family] Both my children are now adults with demanding professional lives. My son misses occasional family events and I give him benefit of the doubt — I assume he's swamped at work. My daughter-in-law misses occasions and I notice it and feel a small but real irritation. Same behavior, different person, very different internal reaction. I've tried to close that gap. I'm a work in progress on this.

[Financial] My company went through a difficult quarter and my performance review was affected. I think the broader market conditions were largely responsible for our results. When a competitor's company went through a similarly difficult quarter I read it as a management failure. I've thought about this asymmetry and I'm not sure I've resolved it.

[Technical] Our engineering team adopted a particular backend framework. When we interview engineers who primarily know other frameworks, I find myself asking more probing questions about adaptability. When candidates come in knowing our stack, I take their technical competence more on faith. Two engineers I've seen in parallel — same objective skill level according to the take-home assessment — were evaluated differently because of what I was looking for.

[Educational] In my department we have rivalries between different theoretical schools. I know which methodological camp each faculty member is in before they present their work at seminars. I've noticed I'm more attentive to methodology critiques in presentations by scholars from the competing camp. When someone from my camp makes the same methodological choices, I assume there's a reason for it that I simply haven't been shown yet.

[Political] Our city council split on a contentious development vote. The councilmembers I know personally voted my way; the ones I don't know voted the other way. I described the first group as principled and the second as captured by special interests. My partner asked whether I'd actually looked at their specific reasoning. I had the reasoning vaguely. I had the allegiances very clearly.

[Management] Two project managers missed the same project milestone. One is on my direct team. One is in an adjacent department. I processed their situations very differently — I spent twenty minutes understanding the context for my team member's miss and five minutes on the other PM's. I don't think I gave the adjacent department PM a fair hearing. He deserved the same twenty minutes. He didn't get them.

[Financial] I'm an angel investor and I've backed eight companies. Six of the founders went to universities I know well. The other two I researched more heavily before investing. The due diligence wasn't proportional to risk — it was proportional to familiarity. I haven't gotten dramatically better returns from either group. I have extended more trust to one group from the start without earning it.

[Social] A new family moved into my building from a different country. I've been welcoming but I've noticed I'm quicker to interpret ambiguous interactions with them as faux pas than I would be with families I already know. When a long-time neighbor does something that could be read as inconsiderate, I give them context — they're probably tired, they didn't mean it. With the new family, I've jumped to character assessments more quickly.

[Legal] I've been on three juries over my lifetime. On two of them the defendant shared my background in some visible way. On one they didn't. I tried to deliberate neutrally on all three. My wife asked me once whether I'd been as tough on the first two as on the third. I said I thought I was consistent. When I actually thought about it, I wasn't sure.

[Technical] We have an active open-source community around our project. Contributors from companies I know and have worked with get faster code reviews from me. Contributors who are unknown individuals get more thorough scrutiny before I approve their PRs. I tell myself it's about reputation management. The actual PR content should be the variable. I'm using company affiliation as a proxy I shouldn't be using.

[Educational] I teach courses with students from many different countries. When a domestic student struggles with a concept, I assume there's a gap in preparation or a communication mismatch I can address. When an international student struggles with the same concept, I sometimes make a different internal attribution — I wonder if there's a language barrier or a different educational background affecting their grasp. Both groups deserve the same patient inquiry. I don't always start from the same place.

[Political] My professional organization took a public position on a piece of legislation. I supported it publicly because I'm active in the organization and share most of its values. Later I read an analysis suggesting the specific position was poorly reasoned. I've been reluctant to say so publicly because it would mean criticizing my own organization. I've applied more protection to my group's reasoning than I would to the same reasoning from an outside organization.

[Management] I have a peer manager who came up through a different part of the company than I did. When his team wins recognition, I find myself briefly wondering what conditions contributed to the result. When my team wins recognition, I experience it as a direct reflection of our work quality. The asymmetry is there and I know it. I haven't been able to make the two instincts symmetrical through awareness alone.

[Financial] Our investment committee has ten members. Five of us have been here longer than eight years; five joined in the last three years. In close calls, I've noticed the older group tend to vote together and the newer group tend to vote together. The older group's coalition describes itself as experienced; the newer group describes itself as fresh-eyed. Neither framing includes a real analysis of whose judgment is better on any specific question. We've both sorted ourselves.

[Social] My friend circle has been together for twenty years. When someone in the group has a conflict with a person outside the group, we tend to receive their account without much skepticism. When someone outside the group has a complaint about one of us, I find myself immediately thinking of context that complicates their account. I know I'm not applying the same standard. I've never managed to fully change it.

# Bandwagon Effect

## Definition
The tendency to adopt beliefs, behaviors, or preferences because other people do — to follow the crowd rather than rely on independent assessment. The probability that a person adopts a belief increases with the number of people who already hold it, independent of the underlying evidence. The bandwagon effect is driven by social proof (the assumption that others have information that justifies their behavior), conformity pressure, and the desire to be part of the majority. It creates self-reinforcing cycles where popularity begets further popularity.

## Examples
In finance, asset price bubbles form when investors buy assets because others are buying, bidding up prices beyond fundamental value. The dot-com bubble and the 2008 housing bubble both show bandwagon dynamics — prices rose because they had been rising, attracting more buyers.

In politics, voters who learn that a candidate has significant momentum or is "winning" shift their support toward that candidate, independent of policy assessment. Pre-election polling can influence outcomes through this mechanism.

In technology, enterprise software purchasing decisions are heavily influenced by what competitors are using. Companies adopt platforms because they are industry-standard, not because of independent capability assessment.

In academia, citation patterns show bandwagon effects: heavily cited papers receive disproportionate future citations, and research topics that gain momentum attract researchers regardless of their intrinsic importance.

In medicine, treatment fashions emerge and persist beyond evidence. Hormone replacement therapy, certain surgical procedures, and dietary interventions have all experienced periods of widespread adoption driven partly by popularity rather than evidence.

## Indicators
- Adopts a position because most people around them hold it, without finding the reasoning independently compelling
- Takes something's popularity as evidence that it must be right or good
- Changes a position when it becomes the minority view, without encountering any new arguments or evidence
- Invests in something or buys something primarily because peers or the crowd are doing the same
- Struggles to defend a view that differs from group consensus, even when the reasoning behind it is sound

## False Positives
- Using the behavior of others as one legitimate signal among several is rational — social proof can carry genuine information
- Following expert consensus in domains where you lack expertise is appropriate deference, not bandwagon effect
- Coordinating on common standards or platforms for interoperability reasons is rational, not a cognitive bias

## Related Biases

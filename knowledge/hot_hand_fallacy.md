# Hot Hand Fallacy

## Definition
The belief that a person who has experienced success in a random or probabilistic task is more likely to have further success in subsequent attempts — that "streaks" reflect genuine underlying momentum beyond what chance would predict. The term comes from basketball, where players and coaches believe certain players are "on fire" and have an elevated probability of making their next shot after a series of makes. The hot hand belief leads to altered behavior: more passes to the "hot" player, riskier shots taken, and more aggressive bets by gamblers on a winning streak.

## Examples
In finance, traders who have made several profitable trades in a row believe they are "in the zone" and take larger, riskier positions — attributing their streak to skill when it may be the result of favorable market conditions or chance.

In sports, coaches increase the shot selection of a player on a streak and reduce shot selection after a miss, even when the player's true shooting probability has not changed and there is no statistical evidence of sequential dependence.

In gambling, casino players who have won several consecutive hands of blackjack believe they are "hot" and increase their bets, despite each hand being an independent event once shuffled.

In sales management, a salesperson who closes several deals in a row is assigned more high-value prospects on the theory that their momentum will continue, when the underlying conversion rate is likely stable and the streak reflects chance variation.

In hiring, candidates who perform well early in an interview process are expected to perform well in final rounds based on their momentum, rather than independent assessment of each round.

## Indicators
- Believes someone on a winning streak is more likely to keep winning because they are "in the zone"
- Changes strategy to favor whoever has been performing well recently rather than whoever is most capable overall
- Passes to the player who scored last rather than the one with the highest stable performance rate
- Increases bets or position sizes after a winning streak, treating the streak as evidence of elevated underlying ability
- Attributes a run of good outcomes to skill or momentum rather than normal statistical variation

## False Positives
- In domains with genuine serial dependence — where confidence, physical state, or momentum truly do affect performance — the hot hand can be a real phenomenon. Some studies show genuine hot hand effects in free throws and three-point shooting when controlling for shot selection. The fallacy is in applying this belief indiscriminately to all domains, including genuinely independent ones
- A skilled analyst who consistently outperforms over a long period may have real edge — attributing this to skill is not the hot hand fallacy

## Related Biases
Gambler's Fallacy, Overconfidence Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Self-serving Bias

## Story Patterns

[Financial] My friend Marcus has made money on his last seven trades. He posts them in our group chat. I've started copying his positions. He's clearly figured something out — seven in a row isn't luck. I haven't looked at his overall track record or the market environment. Seven wins in a row feels like evidence of an edge.

[Management] Our most senior account executive has closed deals three months running at over 150% of quota. I've started routing our top prospects to her specifically. I told my team she's on fire right now and the momentum is real. My VP of sales asked if I'd considered that the accounts themselves might have been particularly strong matches for our product this quarter. I hadn't thought about the account quality — I was responding to her streak.

[Technical] We've had our last four deploys go off without incident. The team has been great but I'm now moving toward a less formal pre-deploy checklist because things have been going so well. My ops lead said he wanted to keep the checklist exactly as it is. I pushed back a little. Things have been clean. I feel like the process has been vindicated.

[Consumer] I've had amazing luck with this one slot machine in the corner — I won twice in a row on my last two visits. Tonight I walked past the good machines by the entrance and went straight to my machine. I know odds don't carry over between sessions. But the machine has been good to me. It feels different from the others.

[Educational] My student got an A on the last three assignments. I've started assuming she'll do well on everything and I've been less careful with my written feedback on her recent submissions. When she came to office hours confused about a concept I thought I'd addressed in the feedback, I realized I'd been breezing through her work because of her recent performance rather than looking at it carefully.

[Social] My friend recommended the last three restaurants we tried and they were all excellent. This weekend she picked again and I went without looking at any reviews. She's on a roll — three great picks is a real signal. The place turned out to be a disappointment. She'd been recommending places in our neighborhood; this one was somewhere different with different context. I'd treated the streak as predictive.

[Political] Our communications director wrote three op-eds in a row that went viral. I told her to draft the position statement on the upcoming bill without much oversight. She's been unstoppable. The statement came back muddier than I expected. The op-eds were in her core expertise. The bill was outside it. I'd treated her streak as domain-agnostic.

[Legal] Our litigation team has won five hearings in a row before this judge. I went into today's motion feeling confident. We were well-prepared but I'd also told the team going in that momentum was on our side. We lost the motion. The judge's ruling was clearly based on the specific facts of this argument, not any prior history with us. I'd been treating prior wins as a resource that carried forward. It didn't.

[Family] My brother has been making excellent investment decisions for the past two years and has been generous with advice. I've started taking his suggestions without much independent research. He suggested a real estate investment trust that has since underperformed significantly. When I asked him about it, he admitted he'd been in unfamiliar territory with that one. I hadn't thought to ask — his streak had made everything feel reliable.

[Technical] Our machine learning model has been performing above benchmark for the last four evaluation cycles. I've been presenting its outputs with increasing confidence in product planning meetings. My data scientist said last week that we should be careful — strong evaluation cycles don't guarantee the next one, especially as data drift occurs. I've been treating the run of good performance as a signal about future performance. She's right that it isn't.

[Management] My team's sprint velocity has been high for the last six sprints. I committed to an aggressive delivery timeline in the client meeting. My scrum master later pulled me aside and said velocity can drop unexpectedly due to factors that have nothing to do with team performance — unplanned issues, dependencies, team changes. I'd projected a six-sprint streak forward in a straight line.

[Financial] I've been on a winning streak with growth stocks for eighteen months. I've been taking on increasingly concentrated positions in single names. My financial planner asked me last quarter what I thought my actual edge was — why I was outperforming. I said I had good instincts for growth metrics. She asked me to look at the growth sector return for the same eighteen months. It had been nearly identical to my return. I'd been attributing to skill what was largely sector performance.

[Social] My friend had six consecutive successful first dates through a particular app. She's become a vocal advocate for it and says her success proves the algorithm is really well-calibrated. I asked her how many first dates she had before the streak. She said quite a few that didn't go anywhere. The six consecutive ones had felt like momentum. The prior track record suggested it might just be variability.

[Educational] I've gotten high scores on four consecutive presentations in this program. I've been preparing a bit less carefully for each one because I seem to have developed a feel for what works. My advisor mentioned that my last one was good but not quite at the level of my previous work. I was surprised — I'd felt good going in. The preparation drop had caught up with me and the streak had made me not see it coming.

[Technical] Our software team shipped five features on time. I've started giving estimates with tighter buffers on subsequent features, feeling like we've found our rhythm. Two features later we were significantly late. The on-time streak reflected a particular set of well-scoped features with clear requirements. The late ones had more ambiguity. I'd treated the streak as evidence of a general capability improvement when it reflected specific conditions.

[Consumer] I've been on a hot streak at the blackjack table tonight — won the last four hands. I've increased my bets significantly. I feel different at the table than I did an hour ago. I feel like I'm reading the cards better, making better decisions. My friend sitting next to me pointed out my actual play hasn't changed at all. I haven't changed. The cards have just been good.

[Political] Our campaign's last three town halls had standing ovations and the energy was electric. I've started booking bigger venues, expecting that the momentum will carry to new communities we haven't visited before. The first new venue was lukewarm — different demographic, different local issues. The energy in the first three towns had been built over months of ground work. I'd expected to export the result without the conditions that produced it.

[Management] My sales manager has been closing deals at an incredible rate this quarter — she's doing two and three times her colleagues. I told the board we should expand her territory significantly. A colleague asked whether we'd looked at the deal quality and the sector distribution. I said her close rate was the metric. He said that sometimes a territory's specific timing and deal mix drive results more than the rep. I hadn't disentangled those.

[Financial] The crypto trader I follow on social media had twelve consecutive winning trades documented. I started following his signals for my own portfolio. He clearly has an edge. His thirteenth signal lost significantly. His fourteenth also lost. I read the comments from other followers — everyone who'd gotten in on the streak had been increasing their positions based on his recent performance. The streak had made the risk feel lower than it was.

[Technical] Our security team has gone eighteen months without a significant incident. I've been proposing in budget discussions that we can optimize the security headcount. The CISO said she wants to maintain the team's size — eighteen months clean doesn't change the underlying threat environment. I'd been treating a clean run as evidence that our posture was more than sufficient. She was right that the two things don't follow from each other.

[Educational] My professor's last four research projects produced papers in top journals. When she proposed a new project that seemed tangential to her established expertise, I voted to fund it without much skepticism. She's been producing excellent work. The new project has stalled significantly. The prior streak had been in a research area she knew deeply. The new one was exploratory. I'd funded based on the person's recent record rather than the fit between the person and the project.

[Consumer] I've been baking sourdough every week for two months and the last five loaves have been excellent. This week I doubled the recipe for a gathering and the bread didn't rise correctly. I'd been feeling like I had a reliable process. When I reviewed what I did differently, the scaled-up version had introduced variables I hadn't thought about. The streak had made me feel like the process was robust. Scaling it revealed it wasn't as robust as the streak suggested.

[Management] My team has hired well in the last three rounds — all three recent hires have been strong performers at six months. I've been moving faster in our current hiring process, trusting the team's instincts more and shortening the structured interview components. My HR partner said she was concerned we were compressing the process based on recent outcomes rather than process quality. The three good hires might reflect process rigor. Shortening the process might produce worse results.

[Social] My friend group has been recommending movies to me and the last five recommendations have been films I loved. This weekend one of them suggested something and I watched it immediately without checking reviews or reading the synopsis. It was genuinely not my kind of film. The five-film streak had made me lower my independent evaluation. The sixth recommendation came from different taste than the previous five.

[Technical] Our data pipeline has successfully processed every large batch job for the last three months without errors. I told the team we could schedule the year-end high-volume run without a dry run. The year-end run failed. The configuration for high-volume runs differs from routine batches. The three months of successful routine runs hadn't tested the year-end configuration. The streak was real but it was evidence about a different scenario than the one we ran into.

[Legal] Our law firm has won the last four arbitration cases before the same arbitrator. Going into the fifth, I told our client we were well-positioned — we know how this arbitrator thinks and he's been consistent with us. We lost. The arbitrator ruled differently based on the specific contract language in the fifth case. I'd been treating prior wins as something that would carry forward. Each case was independent.

[Management] My account management team has retained clients at 97% for the past two years. I've started reducing the account review frequency that had historically been part of our retention process. My team lead said she wanted to keep the quarterly check-ins because the retention rate reflected the process we'd been running. I said the results spoke for themselves. She was worried the results had been produced by inputs I was starting to reduce.

[Financial] The index fund I manage has outperformed its benchmark for three consecutive calendar years. My investors have been increasing their allocations based on the track record. I've been presenting at conferences with increasing confidence. A statistician friend asked how many fund managers in a typical sample would show three-year outperformance by chance alone. The answer was uncomfortable. I hadn't thought about how common three-year streaks are even in the absence of genuine alpha.

[Political] A columnist in my city has had five consecutive accurate predictions about local political outcomes. I've been reading her columns as predictive rather than as informed opinion. Her sixth prediction about the mayoral race was wrong. Each column had been based on different evidence with different reliability. I'd started treating the streak as evidence of an ongoing capability rather than evaluating each prediction on its own merits.

[Educational] My student has gotten high marks on the last four research papers in my course. I graded his fifth paper quickly, expecting to find the quality I'd come to anticipate. My grading assistant flagged it as having a significant methodological gap I'd missed on the first pass. I went back and she was right. I'd skimmed the paper with a prior expectation built from the streak. The streak had made me less careful than I would normally be.

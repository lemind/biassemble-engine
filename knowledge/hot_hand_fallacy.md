# Hot Hand Fallacy

## Definition
The belief that a person who has experienced success in a random or probabilistic task is more likely to have further success in subsequent attempts — that "streaks" reflect genuine underlying momentum beyond what chance would predict. The term comes from basketball, where players and coaches believe certain players are "on fire" and have an elevated probability of making their next shot after a series of makes. The hot hand belief leads to altered behavior: more passes to the "hot" player, riskier shots taken, and more aggressive bets by gamblers on a winning streak.

## Examples
In finance, traders who have made several profitable trades in a row believe they are "in the zone" and take larger, riskier positions — attributing their streak to skill when it may be the result of favorable market conditions or chance.

In sports, coaches increase the shot selection of a player on a streak and reduce shot selection after a miss, even when the player's true shooting probability has not changed and there is no statistical evidence of sequential dependence.

In gambling, casino players who have won several consecutive hands of blackjack believe they are "hot" and increase their bets, despite each hand being an independent event once shuffled.

In sales management, a salesperson who closes several deals in a row is assigned more high-value prospects on the theory that their momentum will continue, when the underlying conversion rate is likely stable and the streak reflects chance variation.

In hiring, candidates who perform well early in an interview process are expected to perform well in final rounds based on their momentum, rather than independent assessment of each round.

## Indicators
- Believes someone on a winning streak is more likely to keep winning because they are "in the zone"
- Changes strategy to favor whoever has been performing well recently rather than whoever is most capable overall
- Passes to the player who scored last rather than the one with the highest stable performance rate
- Increases bets or position sizes after a winning streak, treating the streak as evidence of elevated underlying ability
- Attributes a run of good outcomes to skill or momentum rather than normal statistical variation

## False Positives
- In domains with genuine serial dependence — where confidence, physical state, or momentum truly do affect performance — the hot hand can be a real phenomenon. Some studies show genuine hot hand effects in free throws and three-point shooting when controlling for shot selection. The fallacy is in applying this belief indiscriminately to all domains, including genuinely independent ones
- A skilled analyst who consistently outperforms over a long period may have real edge — attributing this to skill is not the hot hand fallacy

## Related Biases
Gambler's Fallacy, Overconfidence Bias, Representativeness Heuristic, Self-serving Bias
